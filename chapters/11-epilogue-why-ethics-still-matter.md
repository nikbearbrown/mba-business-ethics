# Chapter 11 — Epilogue: Why Ethics Still Matter

*The apparatus the book built is not the point. The working life you build with it is.*

---

In 2009, Rajat Gupta picked up a telephone shortly after a Goldman Sachs board meeting and called Raj Rajaratnam, a hedge-fund manager and longtime personal friend. What had just been discussed in the boardroom — Warren Buffett's impending $5 billion investment in Goldman — was not yet public. Rajaratnam used the information to trade Goldman stock ahead of the announcement. The trades produced approximately $1 million in profit. Gupta personally received nothing.

By 2009, Gupta was one of the most respected business leaders in the United States. He had risen from modest circumstances in India to earn an MBA from Harvard, to become global managing director of McKinsey — the head of the world's most prestigious management consulting firm — to serve on the boards of Goldman Sachs and Procter & Gamble, to cofound the Indian School of Business and the American India Foundation. His own attorneys, in his federal sentencing memo, described him as a *pillar of the consulting community and a trusted advisor to the world's leading companies and organizations*.

Three years after the call, a jury convicted him on three counts of securities fraud. He was sentenced to two years in federal prison and fined $5 million. He served the sentence. The reputation has not, on most published accounts, recovered.

Ask the question this chapter is built around. *Why did Rajat Gupta do it?*

He did not need the money — his net worth was in the high hundreds of millions. He was not being blackmailed. He was not being deceived; he understood securities law thoroughly enough to know the call was a felony. The disclosure helped no one except Rajaratnam.

The honest answer, on the available evidence, is this: Gupta had spent forty years in environments where the people around him had been doing favors for one another at the boundary of what the rules permitted. The favor he did Rajaratnam felt, in the moment, like a continuation of that pattern. The boundary had moved, exposure by exposure, until the call that landed on the front page felt like one more in a sequence that had been ordinary.

That pattern is what this entire book has been about. This chapter returns to the question Chapter 1 opened with — *why does ethics matter* — with the full apparatus now in hand, and tries to answer it in a form the reader can carry into the rest of their working life.

## The slow erosion

Gupta's case has a structural partner in General Motors.

Beginning in roughly 2002, GM engineers became aware that a specific ignition switch installed in several million GM vehicles — Chevrolet Cobalts, Saturn Ions, and several other models — had a defect. Under certain conditions, the switch could move from the *run* position to the *accessory* position while the vehicle was being driven. The shift cut power steering, power brakes, and the airbag system. By the time the defect was publicly disclosed in 2014, it had been associated with at least 124 deaths and 273 documented injuries. GM's federal settlement in September 2015 included a $900 million deferred-prosecution agreement and the recall of approximately 2.6 million vehicles.

The GM mechanism was not Gupta's. Gupta made a single phone call. GM's failure accumulated across twelve years and through an entire organizational hierarchy. Engineers reported the issue. Quality teams investigated and produced documentation. Decisions about disclosure traveled upward. At each step, the decision not to disclose yet was individually defensible — *the issue is not yet fully understood, the consumer impact is not yet clear, the legal exposure is not yet quantified*. The cumulative effect of *not yet*, applied across twelve years, was the deaths.

And then there is Bill Swanson, CEO of the defense contractor Raytheon from 2003 to 2014. In 2004, Swanson published a small booklet titled *Swanson's Unwritten Rules of Management*, containing thirty-three brief maxims for ethical and effective leadership. It became a minor business-publishing success; several CEOs praised its practical value. In 2006, a *USA Today* investigation found that the booklet had been substantially plagiarized — most of the rules lifted, with minimal modification, from a 1944 book called *The Unwritten Laws of Engineering* and several other sources. The booklet was withdrawn. Swanson's compensation package was modified downward. In published accounts of his career, the plagiarism is now what is most cited.

Three cases, three different mechanisms, one structural commonality. Ask the question each actor would have answered differently if asked directly.

Would Gupta, on his first day at Goldman, have agreed to spend the following year leaking inside information to a hedge fund? Almost certainly not. Would the GM engineering team in 2002 have endorsed a corporate posture in which 124 deaths would result from non-disclosure? Almost certainly not. Would Swanson in 2003 have agreed to publish a largely plagiarized management book? Almost certainly not.

Each of those endpoints arrived through pathways the actors did, in fact, walk. The pathway, not the endpoint, is what the apparatus is for.

The book's frameworks name what was happening in each case precisely. Virtue ethics (Chapter 2) asks: *what kind of person am I becoming through this decision?* The Gupta who had made hundreds of small favor-exchanges was a different person from the Gupta who had not, and the difference accumulated invisibly. Utilitarianism asks: *what is the expected sum of benefit and harm across all parties?* The GM ignition decisions failed this test as soon as the death count became substantial; the test was not being run honestly inside the firm. Kant's categorical imperative asks: *could I universalize this rule?* Swanson's plagiarism fails this test transparently — the world in which every business-publishing author lifts their material from unattributed predecessors is not a world any of them would rationally will. Rawls's veil of ignorance asks: *would I design this regime if I did not know which position in it I would occupy?* None of the three actors would have.

The four lenses (Chapter 2's first major contribution), applied early and consistently, would have produced different decisions. Applied late or selectively, they did not.

The long-game horizon from Chapter 1 is the second instrument. Gupta's call would not have been made if he had run Warren Buffett's *front-page test* — *would I be willing to have this written about on the front page of the* New York Times — on the time horizon over which the consequences would play out. The GM disclosure delay would not have continued if the firm had run the long-game test on what cumulative non-disclosure would cost. Both actors were running the short-horizon calculation correctly. Neither was running the correct calculation.

The question is why. The answer is the slow erosion of the boundary, exposure by exposure, until the test that should have been running had gone quiet. The discipline against it is running the apparatus regularly — on decisions that, in the moment, do not seem to require running it.

## How firms go wrong and how they don't

An extensive body of corporate-culture research has converged on a finding that should not be surprising: the daily ethical operation of a firm tracks, more closely than any other measurable variable, the daily ethical operation of the firm's most senior leadership. The phenomenon has a name in the management literature: *tone at the top*. The phrase is informal. The mechanism is structural.

Three specific mechanisms explain why.

The first is modeling. Senior leaders' daily decisions are observed by employees who imitate them. A leader who returns small overpayments from clients establishes a norm of returning overpayments. A leader who keeps them establishes a norm of keeping them. The modeling operates regardless of what the firm's stated values are; the operative culture follows the modeled behavior, not the printed handbook. IBM's stated values — client success, innovation, trust — are the explicit form. What IBM actually does when those values come under financial pressure is the operative form. The gap between the two is what the *tone at the top* concept measures.

The second is selection. The kind of leader the firm's compensation, promotion, and retention systems produce is the kind of leader the firm will continue to have. A firm that promotes the most aggressive revenue producer regardless of how they treat colleagues is selecting for aggressiveness. A firm that includes ethical conduct in its promotion criteria is selecting for it. Over a decade, the selection effect dwarfs everything else.

The third is recovery. When ethical failures occur — and they will, in any firm, periodically — the firm's response shapes what comes next. A firm that holds senior leaders accountable establishes a norm of accountability. A firm that protects them from consequences establishes the opposite.

The Walmart case is instructive here. As of 2017, Walmart had approximately 2.3 million employees and annual revenue approaching $500 billion. Its 2007 slogan shift from *Always Low Prices* to *Save Money. Live Better.* was an explicit cultural commitment. The firm's actual operations have, on the documented evidence, fallen short: numerous lawsuits over employee benefits, foreign-worker rights, child-labor violations, and wage theft; a substantial fraction of its U.S. workforce reported to receive public assistance; the Walton family's net worth increasing substantially during the period these issues were litigated. The gap between the slogan and the operations is what the *tone at the top* concept exists to measure and close.

The Walmart case is not unique. Most large firms have some version of the gap. The chapter's framework: the gap is a choice, made or unmade every day, by the people whose tone sets the organization's culture. The accepted gap is the structural condition under which the Gupta-GM-Swanson failures occur.

Now a contrast.

Bertha Jimenez immigrated to the United States from Ecuador, earned an engineering degree at NYU, and noticed, while she was a graduate student, that the craft-brewing industry — growing rapidly in New York — produced large quantities of *spent grain* as a byproduct, most of which was disposed of as waste despite retaining substantial nutritional value. Jimenez and two friends, also immigrants, founded a Queens-based startup called Rise Products that converts spent brewery grain into a high-protein, high-fiber flour for commercial baking. The firm has supplied Kellogg's, Whole Foods, Nestlé, and several European chefs. Its stated long-term aim is to bring the technology to lower-resource markets including the founders' countries of origin.

The contrast is not between a good person and bad people. It is between two different relationships to the frameworks. Jimenez articulated her values at the beginning — reducing waste, contributing to nutrition, applying engineering to problems with measurable scale — and built the venture around them. Gupta, GM, and Swanson allowed the daily operation to drift from the stated values, gradually and then decisively. The frameworks the book has built are the structural tools for staying closer to Jimenez's pattern and further from Gupta's.

## The personal mission statement

There is a specific operational practice that, on the documented evidence, does substantial work in producing the kind of practitioner this book has been preparing the reader to become. It is not complicated. It is the personal mission statement.

The practitioner articulates, in one or two paragraphs, the values that will guide their professional decisions and the operational commitments those values produce. The statement is not a corporate document. It does not have to be public. It does not have to be elaborate. It does have to be specific enough that, when a decision arises, the statement produces a clear answer.

Published versions illustrate the form. Richard Branson's personal mission: *to have fun in my journey through life and learn from my mistakes*. Former Campbell Soup CEO Denise Morrison's: *to serve as a leader, live a balanced life, and apply ethical principles to make a significant difference*. Both short. Both specific to the person who wrote them. Both operational.

A four-step framework for constructing one.

*Step one: identify five values that matter most to you.* Honesty, integrity, loyalty, fairness, courage, kindness, dignity, justice, generosity, learning — the list is not original; the act of choosing five is. Write them down where you can see them.

*Step two: identify, for each value, where you learned it.* Most values are learned from specific people in specific places — a parent, a teacher, a mentor, a particular book, a specific tradition. Naming the source of each value clarifies why it has the weight it has.

*Step three: identify, for each value, a real or potential challenge to living it.* Honesty under pressure to perform. Loyalty when the person being loyal to is wrong. Fairness when it is expensive. The challenges are not theoretical; they are the situations in which the value will be tested.

*Step four: commit to a specific action in support of each value.* Not *I will try to be honest* but *I will not falsify a performance review to reward a favored employee*. Not *I value fairness* but *I will report safety violations to my supervisor's supervisor if my immediate supervisor will not act*. The actions are concrete enough that when the situation arises, the answer is already known.

The statement does not protect you from every bad decision. It does mean that the bad decision, when it comes, will have to fight its way past something rather than encountering nothing.

## The apparatus, one last time

Somewhere in the next several months, you have an ethical decision to make. It is probably not the Gupta call. It is probably more ordinary. A manager has asked you to misrepresent a project's status in a report. A colleague has asked you to cover for an absence they don't want to disclose. A customer has been overcharged and the system hasn't caught it. A vendor has offered a gift that is technically within the firm's gift policy but that, reported in the local newspaper, would not look good.

Run the apparatus.

Chapter 1's three lenses: what does each one reveal? Are they aligned or in tension? What does Buffett's reporter write about this decision on the time horizon over which the consequences actually play out?

Chapter 2's four traditions: what does virtue ethics ask? What does utilitarianism ask? What does the categorical imperative ask? What would a rational person choose behind Rawls's veil of ignorance? If all four answers point the same direction — as they did for Chiquita's seven-year payment of armed groups in Colombia — the question is no longer philosophical. It is operational.

Chapter 3's stakeholder map: who is affected? Are they all visible to you? Have you asked what each affected party wants and how the decision will affect them?

Chapters 5 through 10: does the decision involve cross-cultural assumptions, employment-relationship duties, rights of parties without contractual standing, industry-specific pressure, or a transitional context that changes the stakes? If yes, the relevant chapter's framework applies.

The apparatus does not produce a single answer. It produces a structured way of seeing the components of the answer. That visibility is the point. The decision the apparatus makes visible is the decision you can be held accountable for. The decision that stays invisible — made on instinct, under pressure, without the apparatus running — is the decision that, accumulated over forty years, produces the Gupta call.

## Why ethics still matter

The book ends where Chapter 1 began: *why does ethics matter?*

The answer that the full apparatus now supports: ethical practice is the operational form of self-respect that scales. It is not a constraint on a successful career; it is the structural condition for a sustainable one.

The frameworks the book built — the three lenses, the four traditions, the stakeholder map, the cross-cultural distinction, the employer/employee duties, the rights-of-all framework, the industry-specific analysis — are not abstractions. They are the operational tools that produce the difference between the career that accumulates, decision by decision, into something worth having, and the career that accumulates, decision by decision, into something else.

There is a line attributed to the first-century BCE Jewish scholar Rabbi Hillel that has served as a useful summary for two thousand years. *If I am not for myself, then who will be? And if I am only for myself, then what am I? And if not now, when?*

The first line is the recognition that your own interests are real and worth attending to. The second is the recognition that an exclusive focus on them, divorced from the interests of others, is not a complete life. The third is the recognition that the time to begin is the only time there is.

You have the apparatus. The rest is your career.

## The single exercise

**Exercise 11.1.** Write your personal mission statement. Use the four-step framework: identify five values, identify the source of each, identify the challenge each will face, commit to a specific action for each. No more than two paragraphs. Write it somewhere you can find it. Revise it annually for the rest of your working life. Use it the next time the apparatus has to be applied to a real decision.

That is all.
