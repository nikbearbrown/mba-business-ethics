# Chapter 11 — Epilogue: Why Ethics Still Matter
*The Boundary Moves One Exposure at a Time.*

In 2009, Rajat Gupta picked up a telephone shortly after a Goldman Sachs board meeting and called Raj Rajaratnam, a hedge-fund manager and longtime personal friend. What had just been discussed in the boardroom — Warren Buffett's impending $5 billion investment in Goldman — was not yet public. Rajaratnam traded Goldman stock ahead of the announcement. The trades produced approximately $1 million in profit. Gupta personally received nothing.

By 2009, Gupta was one of the most respected business leaders in the United States. He had risen from modest circumstances in India to earn an MBA from Harvard, to become global managing director of McKinsey — the head of the world's most prestigious management consulting firm — to serve on the boards of Goldman Sachs and Procter & Gamble, to cofound the Indian School of Business and the American India Foundation. His own attorneys described him in his sentencing memo as a *pillar of the consulting community and a trusted advisor to the world's leading companies and organizations*.

Three years after the call, a jury convicted him on three counts of securities fraud. He was sentenced to two years in federal prison and fined $5 million.

Ask the question this chapter is built around: *why did Rajat Gupta do it?*

He did not need the money. His net worth was in the high hundreds of millions. He was not being blackmailed or deceived. He understood securities law well enough to know the call was a felony. The disclosure helped no one except Rajaratnam.

The honest answer, on the available evidence, is this: Gupta had spent forty years in environments where the people around him had been doing favors for one another at the boundary of what the rules permitted. The favor he did Rajaratnam felt, in the moment, like a continuation of that pattern. The boundary had moved, exposure by exposure, until the call that landed on the front page of the newspaper felt like one more in a sequence that had been ordinary.

That slow movement of the boundary is what this entire book has been about. This chapter returns to the question Chapter 1 opened with — *why does ethics matter* — with the full apparatus now built, and tries to answer it in a form the reader can carry into the rest of their working life.

## The slow erosion, in three cases

Gupta's case has a structural partner in General Motors.

Beginning in roughly 2002, GM engineers became aware that a specific ignition switch installed in several million vehicles — Chevrolet Cobalts, Saturn Ions, and related models — had a defect. Under certain conditions, the switch could move from the run position to the accessory position while the car was being driven. The shift cut power steering, power brakes, and the airbag system. By the time the defect was publicly disclosed in 2014, it had been associated with at least 124 deaths and 273 documented injuries. GM's federal settlement in September 2015 included a $900 million deferred-prosecution agreement and the recall of approximately 2.6 million vehicles.

The GM mechanism was not Gupta's. Gupta made a single phone call. GM's failure accumulated across twelve years and through an entire organizational hierarchy. Engineers reported the issue. Quality teams investigated and produced documentation. Decisions about disclosure traveled upward. At each step, the decision not to disclose yet was individually defensible — *the issue is not yet fully understood, the consumer impact is not yet clear, the legal exposure is not yet quantified*. The cumulative effect of *not yet*, applied across twelve years, was the deaths.

And then there is Bill Swanson. Swanson was CEO of Raytheon, the defense contractor, from 2003 to 2014. In 2004, he published a small booklet titled *Swanson's Unwritten Rules of Management*, containing thirty-three brief maxims for ethical and effective leadership. It became a minor business-publishing success; several CEOs praised its practical value. In 2006, a *USA Today* investigation found that the booklet had been substantially plagiarized — most of the rules lifted, with minimal modification, from a 1944 book called *The Unwritten Laws of Engineering* and several other sources. The booklet was withdrawn. Swanson's compensation was modified downward. In published accounts of his career, the plagiarism is now the most-cited fact.

Three cases, three different mechanisms, one structural commonality. Ask the question each actor would have answered differently if asked in advance.

Would Gupta, on his first day at Goldman, have agreed to spend the following year leaking inside information to a hedge fund? Almost certainly not. Would the GM engineering team in 2002 have endorsed a corporate posture in which 124 deaths would result from non-disclosure? Almost certainly not. Would Swanson in 2003 have agreed to publish a largely plagiarized management book? Almost certainly not.

Each of those endpoints arrived through pathways the actors did, in fact, walk. The pathway, not the endpoint, is what the apparatus is for.

Each of the three failures is also a different kind of erosion, which is why it requires a different lens to catch early. Gupta's failure is the virtue-ethics failure: *what kind of person am I becoming through this pattern of decisions?* The person who had made hundreds of small boundary-crossings was different from the person who had not, and the difference accumulated invisibly. GM's failure is the utilitarian failure: *what is the expected sum of harm across all parties when I add up the count honestly?* The test was not being run honestly inside the firm; if it had been, it would have returned a failing verdict years before 2014. Swanson's failure is the Kantian failure: *could I universalize this rule?* The world in which every business-publishing author lifts their material from unattributed predecessors is not a world any of them would rationally will. Each of the four lenses from Chapter 2 is calibrated to catch a different kind of drift. Running all four, regularly, is what produces the early warning.

The long-game horizon from Chapter 1 is the second instrument. Gupta's call would not have been made if he had run Warren Buffett's *front-page test* on the correct time horizon — the horizon over which the conviction, the sentencing, and the reputation's failure to recover actually play out. The GM disclosure delay would not have continued if the firm had honestly run the long-game calculation on what twelve years of cumulative non-disclosure would eventually cost. Both actors were running short-horizon calculations. Neither was running the correct one.

## How firms go wrong and how they don't

An extensive body of corporate-culture research has converged on a finding that should not be surprising: the daily ethical operation of a firm tracks, more closely than any other measurable variable, the daily ethical operation of its most senior leadership. The management literature calls this *tone at the top*. The phrase is informal. The mechanism is structural.

Three mechanisms explain why.

The first is modeling. Senior leaders' daily decisions are observed by employees who imitate them. A leader who returns small overpayments from clients establishes a norm of returning overpayments. A leader who keeps them establishes the opposite. The modeling operates regardless of what the firm's stated values say; the operative culture follows the modeled behavior, not the printed handbook. IBM's stated values — client success, innovation, trust — are the explicit form of what IBM says it stands for. What IBM actually does when those values come under financial pressure is the operative form. The gap between the two is what *tone at the top* measures.

The second is selection. The kind of leader the firm's compensation, promotion, and retention systems produce is the kind of leader the firm will continue to have. A firm that promotes the most aggressive revenue producer regardless of how they treat colleagues is selecting for aggressiveness. A firm that includes ethical conduct in its promotion criteria is selecting for it. Over a decade, the selection effect dwarfs everything else.

The third is recovery. When ethical failures occur — and they will, in any firm, periodically — the firm's response shapes what comes next. A firm that holds senior leaders accountable establishes a norm of accountability. A firm that protects them from consequences establishes the opposite. The chapter's claim is not that all firms succeed at the three mechanisms; it is that the firms that do produce something durable, and the firms that do not accumulate the conditions in which the Gupta-GM-Swanson failures occur.

The Walmart case is instructive on the gap side. As of 2017, Walmart had approximately 2.3 million employees and annual revenue approaching $500 billion. Its 2007 slogan shift from *Always Low Prices* to *Save Money. Live Better.* was an explicit cultural commitment. The firm's actual operations have, on the documented evidence, fallen short across multiple dimensions: employee benefits, foreign-worker rights, child-labor violations, wage theft, a substantial fraction of the U.S. workforce on public assistance. The Walton family's net worth increased substantially during the period these issues were litigated. The gap between the slogan and the operations is not accidental; it is the cumulative product of decisions at the top that did not run the apparatus seriously.

Now a contrast, to make the other direction legible.

Bertha Jimenez immigrated to the United States from Ecuador, earned an engineering degree at NYU, and noticed, while she was a graduate student, that the craft-brewing industry — growing rapidly in New York — produced large quantities of spent grain as a byproduct, most of which was disposed of as waste despite retaining substantial nutritional value. Jimenez and two friends, also immigrants, founded a Queens-based startup called Rise Products that converts spent brewery grain into a high-protein, high-fiber flour for commercial baking. The firm has supplied Kellogg's, Whole Foods, Nestlé, and several European chefs. Its stated long-term aim is to bring the technology to lower-resource markets including the founders' countries of origin.

The contrast between Jimenez and the failure cases is not between a good person and bad people. It is between two different relationships to the frameworks. Jimenez articulated her values at the beginning — reducing waste, contributing to nutrition, applying engineering to problems with measurable scale — and built the venture around them. Gupta, GM, and Swanson allowed the daily operation to drift from the stated values, gradually and then decisively. The frameworks the book has built are the structural tools for staying closer to the first pattern and further from the second.

## The personal mission statement

There is a specific operational practice that does substantial work in producing the kind of practitioner this book has been preparing the reader to become. It is the personal mission statement.

The practitioner articulates, in one or two paragraphs, the values that will guide their professional decisions and the operational commitments those values produce. The statement is not a corporate document; it does not have to be public or elaborate. It does have to be specific enough that, when a decision arises, the statement produces a clear answer.

Published versions illustrate the form. Richard Branson's personal mission: *to have fun in my journey through life and learn from my mistakes.* Former Campbell Soup CEO Denise Morrison's: *to serve as a leader, live a balanced life, and apply ethical principles to make a significant difference.* Short. Specific. Operational.

A four-step framework for constructing one.

*Identify five values that matter most to you.* Honesty, integrity, loyalty, fairness, courage, kindness, dignity, justice, generosity, learning — the list is not original; the act of choosing five is. Write them down where you can see them.

*Identify, for each value, where you learned it.* Most values are learned from specific people in specific places — a parent, a teacher, a mentor, a particular book, a specific tradition. Naming the source of each value clarifies why it has the weight it has.

*Identify, for each value, a real or potential challenge to living it.* Honesty under pressure to perform. Loyalty when the person being loyal to is wrong. Fairness when it is expensive. The challenges are not theoretical; they are the situations in which the value will be tested.

*Commit to a specific action in support of each value.* Not *I will try to be honest* but *I will not falsify a performance review to reward a favored employee.* Not *I value fairness* but *I will report safety violations to my supervisor's supervisor if my immediate supervisor will not act.* The actions are concrete enough that when the situation arises, the answer is already known.

The statement does not protect you from every bad decision. It does mean that the bad decision, when it comes, will have to fight its way past something rather than encountering nothing. The difference between the Gupta who made the call and the Gupta who could have declined it was not capability or intelligence. It was whether something was in the way. The personal mission statement is the thing you put in the way.

## The apparatus, one last time

Somewhere in the next several months, you have an ethical decision to make. It is probably not the Gupta call. It is probably more ordinary. A manager has asked you to misrepresent a project's status in a report. A colleague has asked you to cover for an absence they don't want to disclose. A customer has been overcharged and the system hasn't caught it. A vendor has offered a gift that is technically within the firm's gift policy but that, reported in the local newspaper, would not look good.

Run the apparatus.

Chapter 1's three lenses: what does each one reveal? Are they aligned or in tension? What does Buffett's reporter write about this decision on the time horizon over which the consequences actually play out?

Chapter 2's four traditions: what does virtue ethics ask — what kind of person am I becoming through this decision? What does utilitarianism ask — what is the sum of benefit and harm across all parties, honestly counted? What does the categorical imperative ask — could I universalize this rule? What would a rational person choose behind Rawls's veil of ignorance, not knowing which position in the outcome they would occupy?

Chapter 3's stakeholder map: who is affected? Are they all visible to you? Have you asked what each affected party wants and how the decision will affect them?

Chapters 4 through 10: does the decision involve society, the environment, or government as background stakeholders? Does it implicate cross-cultural assumptions, employment-relationship duties, rights of parties without contractual standing, industry-specific pressure, or a transitional context? If yes, the relevant chapter's framework applies.

The apparatus does not produce a single answer on demand. It produces a structured way of seeing the components of the answer. That visibility is the point. The decision the apparatus makes visible is the decision you can be held accountable for — and, more importantly, the decision you can look at clearly before making it rather than discovering, years later, what you were actually doing.

The decision that stays invisible — made on instinct, under pressure, without the apparatus running — is the decision that, accumulated over forty years, produces the Gupta call. Not because any single decision is Gupta's call. Because the accumulation is.

## Why ethics still matter

The book ends where Chapter 1 began: *why does ethics matter?*

The answer that the full apparatus supports: ethical practice is the operational form of self-respect that scales. It is not a constraint on a successful career; it is the structural condition for a sustainable one.

The frameworks the book has built — the three lenses, the four traditions, the stakeholder map, the cross-cultural distinction, the employer/employee duties, the rights-of-all framework, the industry-specific analysis — are not abstractions. They are the operational tools that produce the difference between the career that accumulates, decision by decision, into something worth having, and the career that accumulates, decision by decision, into something else.

There is a line attributed to the first-century BCE Jewish scholar Rabbi Hillel: *If I am not for myself, then who will be? And if I am only for myself, then what am I? And if not now, when?*

The first question recognizes that your own interests are real and worth attending to. The second recognizes that an exclusive focus on them, divorced from the interests of others, is not a complete life. The third recognizes that the time to begin is the only time there is.

The book has tried to give you the tools. What those tools build is the career you will actually have. That part is yours.
