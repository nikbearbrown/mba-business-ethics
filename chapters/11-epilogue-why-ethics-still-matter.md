# Epilogue: Why Ethics Still Matter

In October 2012, in a federal courtroom in Manhattan, a sixty-three-year-old man named Rajat Gupta sat with his attorneys and waited for the jury's verdict on four counts: three counts of securities fraud and one count of conspiracy. Until three years earlier, Gupta had been one of the most respected business leaders in the United States. He had grown up in modest circumstances in India. He had earned an MBA from Harvard. He had risen through McKinsey & Company to become its global managing director — the head of the world's most prestigious management consulting firm — a position he held from 1994 through 2003. He had served on the boards of Goldman Sachs and Procter & Gamble. He had cofounded the Indian School of Business and the American Indian Foundation. He had been described, in a federal sentencing memo prepared by his own counsel, as a *pillar of the consulting community and a trusted advisor to the world's leading companies and organizations*.

In 2009, while serving on Goldman Sachs's board of directors, Gupta picked up a telephone shortly after a Goldman board meeting at which Warren Buffett's $5 billion investment in the firm had been discussed but not yet announced publicly. He called a hedge-fund manager named Raj Rajaratnam — a former colleague from another consulting firm and a longtime personal friend — and disclosed the impending Buffett investment. Rajaratnam used the information to trade Goldman stock ahead of the public announcement. The trades produced approximately $1 million in profit for Rajaratnam's fund. Gupta personally received no payment from the disclosure. The federal investigation that uncovered Rajaratnam's broader insider-trading network, which had begun before the Goldman call, had recorded several other Gupta-Rajaratnam phone conversations along the same pattern.

The jury convicted Gupta on three of the four counts. He was sentenced to two years in federal prison and ordered to pay $5 million in fines. He served his sentence and emerged with the public reputation that, on most published accounts of the case, has not recovered.

Ask the question this final chapter is built around. *Why did Rajat Gupta do it?*

He did not need the money. His net worth at the time of the call was reported to be in the high hundreds of millions of dollars. He was not under personal financial pressure. He was not being blackmailed. He was not being deceived by an associate; he understood securities law thoroughly enough to know that what he was doing was a felony. He was not in a position where the disclosure would help anyone except Rajaratnam — not McKinsey, not Goldman Sachs, not the broader public, not himself.

The honest answer, on the available evidence, is that Gupta had spent forty years in environments where the people around him had been doing favors for one another at the boundary of what the rules permitted, and the favor he did Rajaratnam felt, in the moment, like a continuation of that pattern. The boundary moved. The favor turned out to be a felony. The pattern that had produced the boundary movement is the pattern this entire book has been about.

This chapter, the last one, returns to the question Chapter 1 introduced: *why does ethics matter*. It returns to that question with the apparatus the rest of the book has built. The Gupta case is one starting point. The chapter has several others.

## Concept 1: The Gupta pattern, the GM pattern, and the slow accumulation of small choices

Three cases anchor what this concept is about, and the cases are different enough to be illuminating against each other.

The first is Rajat Gupta. The mechanism the chapter has already identified: forty years of small favors at the regulatory boundary, until the favor that crossed the line was indistinguishable, from inside Gupta's experience, from the favors that had not. Gupta's intellectual understanding of securities law was not the constraint. The constraint that was supposed to be operational was the daily-decision *would I be willing to have this written about on the front page of the* New York Times — Buffett's test from Chapter 1. The test had eroded over a long time, exposure by exposure, until the call that landed on the front page felt, to Gupta in the moment, like one more in a sequence that had been ordinary.

The second is General Motors. Beginning in roughly 2002, GM engineers became aware that a specific ignition switch installed in several million GM vehicles — Chevrolet Cobalts, Saturn Ions, several other models — had a defect that could allow the switch to move from the *run* position to the *accessory* position while the vehicle was being driven. The shift cut power steering, power brakes, and the airbag system. By the time the defect was publicly disclosed in 2014, it had been associated with at least 124 deaths and 273 documented injuries. GM's federal settlement, announced in September 2015, included a $900 million deferred-prosecution agreement and the recall of approximately 2.6 million vehicles. Subsequent research and litigation produced higher confirmed-death counts; some estimates exceed 200.

The GM mechanism was not Gupta's. It was a slow institutional erosion of the boundary between *known engineering defect* and *required corporate disclosure*. Engineers reported the issue. Quality teams investigated and produced documentation. Decisions about disclosure traveled up the corporate hierarchy. At each step, the decision to *not yet disclose* was defensible on its own terms — the issue is not yet fully understood, the consumer impact is not yet clear, the legal exposure is not yet quantified. The cumulative effect of *not yet*, applied across twelve years, was the deaths.

The third is Bill Swanson, who served as CEO of the defense contractor Raytheon from 2003 to 2014. In 2004, while still operating below the highest level of public scrutiny, Swanson published a small booklet titled *Swanson's Unwritten Rules of Management*, which contained thirty-three brief maxims for ethical and effective business leadership. The booklet became, briefly, a minor business-publishing success. Several CEOs wrote about its practical value. In 2006, a *USA Today* investigation discovered that the booklet had been substantially plagiarized — most of the rules had been lifted, with minimal modification, from a 1944 book titled *The Unwritten Laws of Engineering* and from several other sources. The booklet was withdrawn. Swanson's compensation and retirement package were modified downward. He completed his Raytheon tenure but, in the published accounts of his career, the plagiarism is what is now most-cited.

The three cases differ in their specifics. The structural commonality is the slow accumulation of small choices, each defensible in isolation, that produced an outcome the actor would never have endorsed if presented with it directly. *Would Gupta, on October 23, 2008, on his first day on the Goldman Sachs board, have agreed to spend the next year leaking inside information to a hedge fund?* Almost certainly not. *Would the GM engineering team in 2002 have agreed to a corporate posture in which 124 deaths would result from non-disclosure?* Almost certainly not. *Would Swanson in 2003 have agreed that his published management book should be largely plagiarized?* Almost certainly not. Yet each of those endpoints arrived through pathways the actors did, in fact, walk.

### The mechanism: the slow erosion and the corrective alarm

The book's apparatus identifies several specific concepts that name what is happening in each case.

**Integrity** (Chapter 1, Concept 1). The unity between what we say and what we do. Integrity erodes through fragmentation — when we permit ourselves to operate on different ethical principles in different rooms, the rooms eventually fall out of contact with one another, and the operating principles drift in directions we did not consciously choose.

**The long-game horizon** (Chapter 1, Concept 3). The recognition that decisions defensible on a quarterly horizon are often indefensible on a decadal one. The Gupta call would not have been made if Gupta had run the *front-page test* on the time horizon over which the consequences would actually play out. The GM disclosure delay would not have continued if the firm had run the long-game test on what the cumulative non-disclosure cost would be. The Swanson plagiarism would not have happened if Swanson had run the test on what would happen when the borrowed material was identified.

**The four ethical lenses** (Chapter 2). Virtue ethics asks *what kind of person am I becoming through this decision*. The Gupta who had made hundreds of small favor-exchanges was a different person from the Gupta who had not, and the difference accumulated invisibly. Consequentialism asks *what is the expected sum of benefit and harm across all parties*. The GM ignition decisions failed the consequentialist test as soon as the death count became substantial; the test was not being run honestly inside the firm. Deontology asks *what duty does this decision honor or violate*. Swanson's plagiarism violated, transparently, the duty to attribute. Rawlsian fairness asks *what regime would I support behind the veil of ignorance*. None of the three actors would have supported, behind a veil, the regime they ended up operating in.

The mechanism is consistent across the cases. The book's apparatus, applied early and consistently, would have produced different decisions. The apparatus, applied late or selectively, did not.

## Concept 2: Tone at the top, corporate culture, and the architecture of ethical workplaces

In the late 2010s and 2020s, an extensive body of corporate-culture research has converged on a finding that should not be surprising and that, on most days, gets ignored: *the daily ethical operation of a firm tracks, more closely than any other variable, the daily ethical operation of the firm's most senior leadership*. The phenomenon has a name in the management literature: *tone at the top*. The phrase is informal. The mechanism is structural.

Several specific concepts anchor the discussion.

**Modeling.** Senior leaders' daily decisions are observed by employees, who imitate them. A senior leader who returns small overpayments from clients establishes a norm of returning overpayments. A senior leader who keeps the overpayments establishes a norm of keeping them. The modeling operates regardless of what the firm's stated values are; the operative culture follows the modeled behavior, not the printed handbook.

**Selection.** The kind of leader the firm's compensation, promotion, and retention systems produce is the kind of leader the firm will continue to have. A firm that promotes the most aggressive revenue producer regardless of the producer's treatment of colleagues is selecting for aggressiveness. A firm that includes ethical conduct in its promotion criteria is selecting for it.

**Recovery.** When ethical failures occur — and they will, in any firm, periodically — the firm's response shapes what comes next. A firm that holds senior leaders accountable when they fail establishes a norm of accountability. A firm that protects senior leaders from the consequences of their failures establishes the opposite norm.

The Walmart case from the book's source material is worth carrying. Walmart, as of 2017, had approximately 2.3 million employees globally and annual revenue of approximately five hundred billion dollars. Its 2007 slogan shift from *Always Low Prices* to *Save Money. Live Better.* signaled an explicit cultural commitment to the well-being of its workforce and customer base. The firm's actual operations have, on the documented evidence, fallen short of the slogan in important ways: the firm has been the defendant in numerous lawsuits over employee benefits, foreign-worker rights, child-labor violations, and wage theft. A substantial fraction of its U.S. workforce has been reported to receive public assistance in some form. The Walton family — the firm's founding-family majority owners — has increased its net worth substantially during the period in which these issues have been litigated.

The Walmart case is not unique. Most large firms have some version of the gap between stated commitment and operational reality. The chapter's framework: the gap is what the *tone at the top* concept measures. The firm whose senior leadership has actively closed the gap operates differently from the firm whose senior leadership has accepted the gap as a feature. The accepted gap is the structural condition under which the Gupta-GM-Swanson failures occur.

### The Cheesecake Factory in Hong Kong: the cross-cultural test

The book's source material includes a specific case worth pulling out for the synthesis. In May 2017, the U.S. casual-dining chain *Cheesecake Factory* opened its first Hong Kong location. The restaurant's menu and decor were unchanged from its U.S. operations — the same approximately 250 menu items featuring substantial cheese, dairy, and meat content; the same large portions; the same elaborate dessert display. The restaurant was, in the immediate post-opening months, overwhelmed by Hong Kong customers who were attracted partly by the food and partly by the *American experience* the chain promised.

The chapter's apparatus produces the questions. *Does Cheesecake Factory have an obligation, in the Hong Kong market, to adapt its offerings to local dietary norms — substantially lower fat and dairy content than the U.S. menu?* The cross-cultural framework of Chapter 5 says that local norms vary in ways that the firm should respect. The thin universal core of Chapter 5 says that *honesty in offerings* is universal. The Cheesecake Factory is being honest — it is selling exactly what it sells in Los Angeles. Is the cross-cultural duty satisfied by transparency about what is on offer, or does it require something more?

The honest answer, on the chapter's framework, is that the Cheesecake Factory case is not the central ethical issue in cross-cultural retail. It is a case where customers are genuinely choosing what they want, and the firm is providing it. The harder cases are the ones where the firm's product produces genuine harm in the local market — where the cross-cultural framework demands more than disclosure. The Cheesecake Factory in Hong Kong is, on most readings, a case where the framework does not demand more.

The contrast worth carrying is to Google's 2006-2010 China operation. Google, then operating under its founding *Don't be evil* slogan, agreed to censorship requirements imposed by the Chinese government as a condition of operating in the Chinese market. Searches for *Tiananmen Square* on Google's Chinese-language site produced different results than the same searches on Google's U.S. site. Searches for several other politically sensitive topics produced no results. Google's posture was that the censored Chinese-language access produced more good than no Chinese-language access at all. In 2010, Google withdrew the search service from China after a series of escalating disputes with the Chinese government. Google has, in the years since, attempted partial returns to the Chinese market with various products. The full search engine has not returned.

The Google case is the Cheesecake Factory case in a more demanding form. The product was not food. It was information. The local-norms-vs-universal-core distinction operates harder. The chapter's framework does not produce a single clean answer for either case. The framework produces the questions the firm has to answer honestly to itself, and the documentation that allows external observers to evaluate the honesty.

## Concept 3: The personal mission statement and the architecture of an ethical career

Toward the end of its source material, the book turns to a specific operational practice that, on the documented evidence, does substantial work in producing the kind of ethical practitioners the book has been preparing the reader to become. *The personal mission statement.*

The practice is simple. The practitioner — the reader, in this case — articulates, in one or two paragraphs, the values that will guide their professional decisions and the operational commitments those values produce. The statement is not a corporate mission statement; it is a personal one. It does not have to be public. It does not have to be elaborate. It does have to be specific enough that, when the practitioner faces a decision, the statement produces a clear answer.

Several published versions illustrate the form. The Virgin Group founder Richard Branson's stated personal mission: *to have fun in my journey through life and learn from my mistakes*. The former Campbell Soup CEO Denise Morrison's stated mission: *to serve as a leader, live a balanced life, and apply ethical principles to make a significant difference*. Both are short. Both are specific to the person who wrote them. Both are operational — they produce different answers to specific decisions than alternative statements would have produced.

### The mechanism: identifying values, prioritizing them, and committing to action

The book's source material proposes a four-step framework for constructing a personal mission statement. The framework is worth carrying in the form the source presents it.

*Step one: identify five values that matter most to you.* The list might include honesty, integrity, loyalty, fairness, courage, kindness, hope, dignity, justice, faith, work, family, freedom, learning, beauty, generosity. The list need not be original. The act of choosing five from the longer list — and writing the chosen five down where you can see them — is the operative move.

*Step two: identify, for each value, where you learned it.* Most values are learned from specific people in specific places — a parent, a teacher, a coach, a mentor, a particular piece of literature, a specific religious or philosophical tradition. Naming the source of each value clarifies why it has the weight it has and what kind of evidence might cause you to revise it.

*Step three: identify, for each value, a real or potential challenge to living it.* Honesty under pressure to perform. Loyalty when the person being loyal-to is wrong. Fairness when fairness is expensive. The challenges are not theoretical; they are the situations in which the value will be tested.

*Step four: commit to a specific action in support of each value.* The action is the operational expression of the commitment. *I will not falsify performance reviews to reward favored employees.* *I will report safety violations to my supervisor's supervisor if my immediate supervisor will not act.* *I will not mislead a customer about a product's limitations.* The actions are concrete enough that, when the situation arises, the practitioner knows what to do without re-deriving the answer.

### The Bertha Jimenez case: values producing a venture

The book's source includes a small case worth using to illustrate the framework's operation.

Bertha Jimenez immigrated to the United States from Ecuador. She earned an engineering degree at New York University. While she was a graduate student, she became aware that the craft-brewing industry — which had been growing rapidly in New York — produced large quantities of *spent grain* as a byproduct, most of which was disposed of as waste despite retaining substantial nutritional value. Jimenez and two friends, also immigrants, founded a Queens-based startup called *Rise Products* that converts spent brewery grain into a high-protein, high-fiber flour for commercial baking.

The firm's mission — *Upcycling is the future of food* — is the operational expression of a set of personal values that include reducing waste, contributing to nutrition in low-resource settings, and applying the founders' engineering training to a problem with measurable scale. The firm has supplied Kellogg's, Whole Foods, Nestlé, and several Italian chefs. The firm's stated long-term aim is to bring the technology to lower-resource markets including the founders' countries of origin.

The case is not unusual in its structure. Most successful entrepreneurial firms have a similar pattern: founders with specific personal values, identifying a market opportunity that aligns with the values, building a venture that operationalizes the alignment. The Rise Products case is one specific instance of the pattern. The reader's career, if it includes a venture, will produce its own instance.

### The ethical career — what the book has been about

The book's central argument, in the form the epilogue can now state directly, is this. *Ethics is not a constraint on a successful career; it is the structural condition for a sustainable one.* The frameworks the book has built — the three lenses, the four traditions, the stakeholder map, the cross-cultural distinction, the employer/employee duties, the rights-of-all framework, the industry-specific pressure recognition, the future-of-work analysis — are not abstractions. They are the operational tools that produce the difference between a career like Rajat Gupta's in 2003 (forty years of accumulated favor-exchanges) and a career like Bertha Jimenez's in 2018 (a venture organized around clearly-articulated values from inception).

The frameworks do not guarantee Jimenez-style outcomes. They do not eliminate the small-choice accumulation that produced Gupta's. They do produce the awareness that the small choices are accumulating, the framework for recognizing when the accumulation is moving in a problematic direction, and the discipline for catching the trajectory before it produces an irrecoverable failure.

That awareness is what the book has been trying to produce. The reader who has reached this final chapter has the apparatus. The reader's career will, between now and its end, produce the cases on which the apparatus will be tested. The chapter's last task is to hand the apparatus over with as little ceremony and as much specificity as possible.

## Integration: the book's apparatus, applied to the reader's first ethical decision after this chapter

The reader has, somewhere in the next several months, an ethical decision to make. The decision is probably not the Rajat Gupta call. It is probably not the GM ignition-switch disclosure. It is probably more ordinary than either. Perhaps a manager has asked the reader to misrepresent a project's status in a status report. Perhaps a colleague has asked the reader to cover for an absence the colleague does not want to disclose. Perhaps a customer has been overcharged and the system has not caught it. Perhaps a vendor has offered a gift that is technically permitted under the firm's gift policy but that, if reported in the local newspaper, would not look good.

The decision is the kind that the book has been preparing the reader for. Run the apparatus.

*Chapter 1's three lenses.* What does each lens reveal? Are they aligned? Do they diverge? Buffett's reporter — what would the front-page story look like?

*Chapter 2's four traditions.* What does virtue ethics ask? What does utilitarianism ask? What does Kantian deontology ask? What does Rawls's veil of ignorance ask? Are the answers consistent?

*Chapter 3's stakeholder map.* Who is affected by the decision? Are they all visible to you? Have you asked yourself what each affected party wants and how the decision will affect them?

*Chapter 4's three special stakeholders.* Are society, the environment, or government among the affected parties? If yes, the special-stakeholder framework applies.

*Chapter 5's cross-cultural framework.* Does the decision involve people whose ethical assumptions are different from yours? If yes, the thin core / thick local distinction applies.

*Chapter 6 and 7's employer/employee framework.* Is the decision happening inside an employment relationship? What duties are operating? Are they pulling in the same direction or different directions?

*Chapter 8's rights-of-all framework.* Are there parties whose rights are at stake who do not have a direct contractual standing in the decision?

*Chapter 9's industry-pressure framework.* What specific ethical pressure does your industry concentrate? Is the current decision an instance of that pressure?

*Chapter 10's future-of-work framework.* Is the decision happening inside a transitional context — automation, gig classification, AI deployment, hybrid work — that the apparatus has prepared you to recognize?

The apparatus does not produce a single answer. It produces a structured way of thinking about the decision that makes the components of the answer visible. *That visibility is the point.* The decision the apparatus makes visible is the decision the practitioner can be held accountable for. The decision that remains invisible — the one made on instinct, under pressure, without the apparatus running — is the decision that, accumulated over forty years, produces the Gupta call. The reader has the apparatus. Use it.

## Chapter summary

The book ends, as it began, with the question Chapter 1 introduced: *why does ethics matter*. The answer the book has been building toward is now visible.

Ethics matters because the alternative — the ungrounded daily decision-making that produces, over a forty-year career, the Rajat Gupta call — is not actually rational on any time horizon longer than the next quarter. The frameworks that ethics has produced over the past two and a half millennia are the structural conditions for the kind of working life that the reader is, presumably, hoping to have.

Ethics matters because the firms in which the reader will work have built operational cultures that either honor or undermine ethical practice, and the reader's choices contribute to which kind of firm they continue to be. The *tone at the top* mechanism is also a *tone at every level* mechanism. The reader is not exempt from the modeling that builds firm culture; the reader's daily choices are part of it.

Ethics matters because the world the reader will work in is changing — automation, AI, gig economy, climate-amplified risk, cross-cultural complexity — in ways that the existing frameworks were not built to handle, and that will produce new ethical questions over the reader's working life. The frameworks are the apparatus the reader will use to think honestly about the new questions. They are not solutions. They are the tools for arriving at solutions, in cases that have not yet appeared.

The single idea that matters most, in the form the epilogue can finally state it: *ethical practice is the operational form of self-respect that scales*. The reader who treats their daily decisions as ethically consequential is the reader whose career, summed across decades, produces the kind of working life worth having. The reader who does not — the reader who treats ethics as a constraint to be managed rather than a structural condition to be honored — produces the other kind. The book has been one extended case for the first option. The reader's career is where the case is tested.

The common mistake to watch for, on the apparatus the book has built, is the one Rajat Gupta made. *The mistake is the slow erosion of the boundary, exposure by exposure, until the line that should not have been crossed feels indistinguishable from the lines that were crossed safely.* The discipline against the mistake is the discipline of running the apparatus regularly — the front-page test, the four lenses, the stakeholder map, the long-game horizon — on decisions that, in the moment, do not seem to require running them.

You should now be able to read a current corporate-ethics case — one in the news this week — and produce, in twenty minutes without notes, a defensible analysis using the apparatus the book has built. That is what the book was for.

## A final note from the author to the reader

The book has been, throughout, an apparatus. The apparatus is not the point. The point is the working life the reader will build using it.

There is a line attributed to the first-century BCE Jewish religious leader Rabbi Hillel that has, for two thousand years, served as a useful summary of what an ethical career requires. *If I am not for myself, then who will be? And if I am only for myself, then what am I? And if not now, when?*

The first line is the recognition that the reader's own interests are real and worth attending to. The second is the recognition that an exclusive focus on the reader's interests, divorced from the interests of others, is not a complete life. The third is the recognition that the time to begin is the only time there is.

The reader has the apparatus. The reader's career is now the case on which it operates. The book has done what books can do. The rest is the reader's.
