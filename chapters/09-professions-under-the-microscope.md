# Professions Under the Microscope

On the morning of February 19, 2017, a software engineer named Susan Fowler — who had recently left her job at Uber Technologies after thirteen months — published a blog post on her personal website titled *Reflecting on One Very, Very Strange Year at Uber*. The post described, in carefully documented detail, a workplace in which Fowler's first manager had propositioned her on her first day on the team; in which her subsequent reports to Uber's human-resources department had been dismissed because the manager was a *high performer*; in which female engineers had been told, when they raised concerns, that their performance reviews had been retroactively downgraded as a basis for terminating them; in which, by the time Fowler left, the engineering organization had gone from approximately 25 percent women to approximately 6 percent.

Within forty-eight hours, the post had been read by millions of people. Within a week, Uber had retained the former U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder to lead an investigation. Within four months, the firm's CEO and co-founder Travis Kalanick had been forced out by an ultimatum from the firm's largest investors. Within a year, the firm had paid out roughly fifty million dollars in settlements related to the disclosed misconduct, restructured its leadership, and undertaken a documented overhaul of its workplace policies. The firm's valuation, despite the upheaval, continued to climb; by 2019 Uber went public at a market capitalization of approximately seventy-five billion dollars.

The Fowler post is the cleanest illustration in recent corporate history of a structural problem this chapter is built around. *The character of a firm tracks the character of its founders, and bureaucracy — the layering of formal procedures and accountability structures that grows with scale — does not, on its own, fix bad founder culture.* Bureaucracy at Uber existed. Reporting channels existed. The formal apparatus was in place. The apparatus did not function until it was forced to function by external pressure that the founder could not absorb. The chapter is about what specific professional contexts — entrepreneurship, advertising, insurance, healthcare — make those structural problems most likely, and about how to recognize them before the Fowler-style disclosure is the firm's only correction mechanism.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Explain** how founder culture in entrepreneurial firms scales into operational ethics, using Uber and Kinko's as worked cases.
- **Apply** John Kenneth Galbraith's *dependence effect* and the child-vulnerability framework to a contemporary advertising case.
- **Evaluate** the structural problem of catastrophic-risk pooling using Hurricane Harvey 2017 and the 2017 *Consumer Reports*–ProPublica redlining study.
- **Compare** U.S. multipayer health care to single-payer (Canada, U.K.) and other multipayer (Germany, France, Japan) systems on cost efficiency and outcome metrics.
- **Identify** the structural conditions under which professional bureaucracy succeeds in reining in unethical practice and the conditions under which it fails.

## Why this chapter matters

Most readers of this book will work in one of the sectors this chapter covers, or in a sector with similar structural pressures. The chapter's professional ethics are not abstract. They are operational features of daily work in entrepreneurship, marketing, insurance, healthcare, and adjacent fields. The chapter argues that the ethical pressures in each of these sectors are predictable enough that practitioners can prepare for them — and that preparation is what separates the practitioners who navigate the pressures from the ones who become casualties of them.

## Concept 1: Entrepreneurship and the bureaucracy paradox

In 1970, a 22-year-old named Paul Orfalea — who had been diagnosed with severe dyslexia, who had been told by multiple educators that he would never finish college, and who had recently rented a small storefront near the University of California Santa Barbara — opened a copy shop with a rented Xerox machine. He named the shop after his college nickname, *Kinko's*. Over the following twenty-five years, the shop grew into a chain of approximately twelve hundred stores employing approximately twenty-three thousand people across most of the United States. In 2004, Orfalea sold the company to FedEx for $2.4 billion.

The Kinko's growth story is, on its outline, an entrepreneurial success of the most familiar kind. The internal story, as Orfalea later documented in his memoir *Copy This!*, is more complicated. Orfalea's signature management style — intense, personal, often angry — had been an asset in the company's early years, when he was working alongside a small team of trusted partners. As the firm grew, the same style became a liability. Orfalea later wrote that he had, over the course of the firm's expansion, alienated nearly every long-term partner he had begun the firm with, and that the years following the FedEx sale had been spent partly in repairing those relationships.

The Orfalea pattern is the structural feature this concept is built around. *Entrepreneurial firms, in their early years, are extensions of their founders' personalities. The personality that produces the early success is, often, the same personality that produces the later ethical failure as the firm scales.* The founder's tone becomes the firm's culture; the founder's blind spots become the firm's blind spots; the founder's virtues and vices, multiplied across thousands of employees, become operational features of the firm's relationship to its workforce, customers, and broader society.

### The mechanism: founder culture, scale, and Weber's iron cage

Several specific concepts anchor the entrepreneurial-ethics question.

**Founder culture** is the cluster of operational norms, communication styles, decision-making heuristics, and ethical defaults that the founder brings to the firm at its inception. Founder culture is not, in early-stage firms, a code of conduct. It is the daily texture of how the founder behaves in front of others. The behavior produces imitation; the imitation produces norm; the norm becomes culture.

**The bureaucracy paradox** is what happens when the firm grows beyond the size at which founder culture can be transmitted face-to-face. Max Weber, in his *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft* (Economy and Society, 1925), described bureaucracy as the necessary structural form of large-scale organized activity in modern economies. Bureaucracy substitutes formal procedures for personal judgment; it produces predictability and accountability at scale. Bureaucracy, Weber wrote, *is among those social structures which are the hardest to destroy*. Once installed, it continues. The paradox the entrepreneurial firm faces is that the formal procedures of bureaucracy do not, by themselves, fix bad founder culture; they layer on top of it, leaving the operating texture of the firm shaped by the founder's defaults even as the policies and handbooks grow more elaborate.

**The exit condition** is the moment at which founder culture and bureaucratic structure produce divergent answers to a specific question, and the firm has to choose between them. Uber's choice — to defend Kalanick's culture against the formal complaint mechanisms — was the kind of choice that, when it goes the wrong way, eventually produces the Fowler-style external intervention.

### The trade-off: founder culture is high-bandwidth and brittle

Founder culture has real virtues. It is high-bandwidth (a small team operating in personal proximity coordinates faster than a large team coordinating through formal procedures). It is adaptive (the founder can change direction without consultation). It is energizing (the founder's commitment to the firm's mission is contagious in early-stage settings). Most successful early-stage firms have founder cultures that, on net, contributed to their success.

The trade-off is brittleness. The founder culture that scales well in the first hundred employees often does not scale to the first thousand, and almost never to the first ten thousand. The founder's individual quirks, multiplied across a firm of that size, become structural features the firm cannot easily disown. The remedy — installing professional management with the authority to override the founder's defaults — is the remedy most successful large firms have applied at some point in their history. The remedy is not free. It typically requires the founder to either step back from operational control or leave the firm. The Uber and the Travis Kalanick case is one variant. The Apple and the Steve Jobs 1985 firing was another. The pattern is durable.

### Worked example: the Uber timeline

The Uber narrative is worth tracking with specific dates because the velocity of the events is part of the lesson.

*March 2009.* Travis Kalanick and Garrett Camp found UberCab in San Francisco.

*2010-2014.* The firm grows rapidly. By 2014, it operates in roughly 100 cities across 35 countries. Kalanick's reputation as a brilliant, combative, often verbally aggressive operator is well-established within the technology industry.

*2014-2016.* A series of incidents — internal and external — accumulate. A senior executive proposes investigating journalists' personal lives. A *Bloomberg* article describes a culture of harassment. Multiple internal complaints reach senior management without producing visible action.

*February 2017.* Susan Fowler publishes her blog post.

*March 2017.* The Holder investigation begins.

*June 2017.* Kalanick is forced out.

*2018-2019.* Settlements are paid; cultural restructuring is documented; the firm's IPO proceeds.

The compressed timeline — from the Fowler post to Kalanick's exit in roughly four months — is the telling feature. The structural problems had existed for years. The external pressure that produced the correction arrived in weeks. The pattern is not unusual; it is the modal pattern for large entrepreneurial firms with founder-culture problems. The firms whose founders are willing to step back before the external pressure arrives produce smoother corrections. The firms whose founders are not willing — Uber, Theranos, WeWork (under Adam Neumann), several others — produce the more disruptive ones.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that bureaucracy is the problem. Sometimes it is, in the sense that excessive bureaucracy strangles innovation. More often it is the absence of effective bureaucracy that produces the worst founder-culture failures. Uber's HR department's failure to act on complaints is not a story of overreaching bureaucracy; it is a story of a bureaucracy not yet strong enough to override the founder's defaults.

The second is that founder culture is *bad*. Most founder cultures, in successful firms, are net positive. The question is not whether to have one; it is how to recognize when the founder culture has stopped serving the firm and to install the remedies before the external pressure forces them.

The third is that the bureaucracy-paradox is unique to technology firms. It is not. Any rapidly-scaling firm with a strong founder produces the same dynamic. Banking, retail, manufacturing, healthcare — the same pattern recurs.

## Concept 2: Advertising and the asymmetry of persuasion

In 1958, the Harvard economist John Kenneth Galbraith published *The Affluent Society*, a book that argued, among many other things, that the modern American economy had reached a stage at which the production of new goods no longer satisfied pre-existing wants but, instead, *created* the wants the goods then satisfied. Galbraith called this *the dependence effect* — the dependence of demand on the production process that supposedly responded to it. The argument has been contested for sixty-seven years. The contested argument has not gone away.

The contemporary version of the question runs as follows. A firm advertises a product to a population. Some of the population, on encountering the advertising, develop a preference for the product they did not previously hold. The preference produces purchase. The purchase produces revenue. The revenue funds further advertising. Where, in this loop, did the *preference* originate? If the preference originated in the audience's pre-existing needs and the advertising merely matched the product to the need, the loop is benign. If the preference was *created* by the advertising — manufactured in audiences who would not, absent the advertising, have desired the product — the loop is something else. The asymmetry of information and persuasive resources between the firm and the audience makes the loop suspect. The chapter is about how to think carefully about the suspicion.

### The mechanism: information asymmetry and the vulnerability classes

Several specific cases anchor the discussion.

**The Pet Rock.** In April 1975, an advertising executive named Gary Dahl conceived of a product called the Pet Rock — a smooth gray stone, packaged in a small cardboard box with breathing holes and a 32-page humorous owner's manual. The product had no functional utility. It sold approximately five million units in the first six months and made Dahl, briefly, a millionaire. The product has remained in print, in various forms, ever since; in 2017 it retailed for $19.95. The Pet Rock case is the canonical illustration of advertising-driven demand for a product with no underlying utility. The case is also the canonical illustration of consumer agency: every Pet Rock buyer chose to buy the product, presumably aware of its non-utility. The dependence effect is real; the question is what work it does in cases the buyer entered with full information.

**Subliminal advertising.** In 1957, a market researcher named James Vicary claimed to have demonstrated that brief subliminal messages flashed during a New Jersey movie screening — *Drink Coca-Cola* and *Eat Popcorn* — had increased sales of those products at the theater concession stand. The claim was widely reported (Vance Packard's *The Hidden Persuaders*, 1957, made it canonical). Vicary later admitted, in 1962, that the claim had been fabricated. Subsequent research has produced a much narrower finding. A 2015 study at the University of South Carolina demonstrated that subliminal messages can increase the salience of a product *in the short term* (within roughly a 15-minute window after the message), but only when the audience is already in a state related to the product's function — only when subjects had been recently primed to be thirsty did the subliminal *Drink Coca-Cola* produce measurably higher Coca-Cola purchase. The cultural durability of the subliminal-advertising threat substantially exceeds the empirical evidence for it. The empirical evidence for subliminal effects is real but narrow.

**Children as a vulnerability class.** Developmental psychology research, replicated across multiple decades, has established that children younger than approximately seven or eight years old do not reliably distinguish advertising from other adult communication. They treat advertising messages as trustworthy adult information. The implication is that advertising directed at children operates on an audience that lacks the cognitive machinery to evaluate the persuasive intent. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission has, since the 1970s, attempted multiple regulatory regimes for child-directed advertising, with mixed success. Several other countries — the United Kingdom, the Nordic countries, Brazil — have adopted more restrictive regimes. The ethical question is settled in those jurisdictions; the U.S. position remains contested.

**Self-esteem appeals.** The third major class of persuasive vulnerability is the appeal to the audience's self-esteem rather than to the product's merit. The toy advertised to a parent as the proof of love. The car advertised to a young man as the proof of masculinity. The cosmetics advertised to a woman as the only path to her own beauty. The appeals work because they connect to needs the audience holds — needs for love, status, beauty — that the product cannot, on inspection, actually satisfy. The ethical question is the question Galbraith named: *is the persuader using the audience's vulnerability to the audience's benefit, or to the persuader's?*

### The trade-off: useful information vs. manufactured demand

Advertising is not, on the whole, evil. Most advertising delivers information that audiences value about products that audiences want. A firm that produces a high-quality product needs some way to inform potential customers of its existence, and advertising is the dominant mechanism. The trade-off is between *informational* advertising (here is the product, here is what it does, here is the price) and *persuasive* advertising in the dependence-effect sense (here is the product, here is the emotional or status need it will satisfy, please ignore the question of whether it actually will).

The line is not sharp. Most contemporary advertising operates somewhere on the spectrum between the two extremes. The chapter's framework is to ask, of any specific campaign: *what information is being delivered, what emotional appeal is being made, and would the audience, on full information, choose to be persuaded by the appeal?* The campaigns that pass the test (informational, with emotional appeals consistent with the product's actual benefits) are defensible. The ones that fail it (substantially manipulative, with appeals that the audience would, on reflection, reject) are not.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that audiences have full agency in their consumption decisions. They do, in a basic sense. They also operate inside an environment in which advertising messages are, in modern Western economies, essentially unavoidable. The agency the audience has is the agency to make consumption decisions inside an environment that the advertising industry has substantially shaped.

The second is that subliminal advertising is the main concern. It is not. The empirical evidence for subliminal effects is narrow and the regulatory regime against them is settled. The harder questions are the ones about supraliminal advertising — the explicit, conscious, well-funded advertising that operates on the documented vulnerabilities of children, the elderly, the financially anxious, and the chronically self-conscious.

The third is that regulating advertising is censorship. It is, in some readings. It is also the same kind of regulation we apply to other classes of speech that produce identifiable harm — securities-fraud regulation, false-advertising regulation, regulation of advertising for prescription drugs and tobacco. The framework is in place. The question is how broadly it should be applied.

## Concept 3: Insurance and healthcare — the structural problem of risk pooling

In late August 2017, Hurricane Harvey made landfall on the Texas Gulf Coast as a Category 4 storm. The storm stalled over the Houston metropolitan area for roughly four days, dumping approximately 52 inches of rain — the largest single rainfall event in continental U.S. history at the time of the storm, exceeding 60 trillion gallons of water across the affected region. The flooding damaged or destroyed approximately 200,000 homes. The total property loss was estimated at approximately thirty billion dollars.

The insurance numbers are the part that this concept is built around. Of the approximately thirty billion dollars in property loss, an estimated forty percent was covered by insurance. Sixty percent — roughly eighteen billion dollars — was uninsured loss borne directly by Houston households. The reason: standard U.S. homeowners' insurance covers wind damage but not flood damage. Flood coverage is sold separately, primarily through the federal *National Flood Insurance Program* established by the National Flood Insurance Act of 1968 and administered by the Federal Emergency Management Agency. As of August 2017, only approximately five million U.S. households held flood coverage; the figure was a small fraction of households living in flood-prone areas.

The Harvey case exposes the structural problem this concept is about. *Insurance, as a private-sector product, works for risks that are statistically predictable, geographically dispersed, and actuarially priceable. It does not work, on a private-sector basis, for catastrophic risks that are concentrated, correlated, and increasingly affected by climate change.* The federal flood-insurance program exists because the private sector withdrew from flood coverage; it could not be priced sustainably. Hurricane, wildfire, and increasingly windstorm and severe-weather coverage are now facing similar structural pressure.

### The mechanism: risk pooling, redlining, and catastrophic correlation

Several specific concepts anchor the insurance ethics.

**Risk pooling** is the foundational mechanism of the insurance industry. Many parties pay small premiums; the pool covers the losses of the few who experience the insured event. The mechanism works when (a) the events are statistically independent across the pool, (b) the events are predictable enough to be priced, and (c) no single event is large enough to exhaust the pool. All three conditions fail in the case of climate-amplified catastrophic events. Hurricanes hit thousands of households at once; wildfires destroy entire neighborhoods; the events are not independent across the pool, and the loss concentrations exhaust the pool faster than the actuarial assumptions permit.

**Redlining** is the discriminatory pricing of insurance products on the basis of neighborhood characteristics that proxy for race or ethnicity. The 2017 *Consumer Reports*–ProPublica investigation found that auto insurers in California, Illinois, Missouri, and Texas were charging higher premiums in predominantly minority neighborhoods than would be justified by the actual claims history in those neighborhoods — premium differentials wider than the underlying risk could explain. The investigation named major carriers (Allstate, Geico, Liberty Mutual). The practice was illegal under the laws of most of the affected states. Enforcement was limited.

**Carrier withdrawal.** As climate-amplified catastrophic events have become more frequent, insurers have responded by exiting the most-affected markets. State Farm, Allstate, and several other major carriers announced, in 2023-24, partial withdrawals from the California homeowners' market in response to wildfire risk. Florida's homeowners' insurance market has been progressively destabilized through the 2010s and 2020s. The withdrawal pattern is structural: private-sector insurance cannot, at sustainable price points, cover catastrophic correlated risks at the scale climate change is producing.

### Healthcare: the multipayer problem

The U.S. healthcare system is, in international comparison, an outlier. Most peer countries operate either *single-payer* systems (Canada, the United Kingdom — taxes pay providers directly) or *multipayer-but-regulated* systems (Germany, France, Japan — multiple payers operate inside a tightly regulated framework that constrains pricing and ensures universal coverage). The U.S. operates a *multipayer-and-loosely-regulated* system: patients, employers, private insurers, and government programs (Medicare, Medicaid) all pay providers, with substantially more pricing variation than the regulated multipayer systems permit.

The Commonwealth Fund's 2017 *Mirror, Mirror* comparative study placed the United States last among eleven peer countries on overall healthcare-system performance, while spending substantially more per capita than any of them. The combination — highest cost, weakest performance — has persisted across multiple comparative studies. The U.S. provider compensation is, on average, higher than peer-country compensation; the U.S. administrative overhead is also higher; the U.S. prescription drug cost is substantially higher (William B. Schultz, writing in the *Washington Post* in August 2017, documented that prescription drugs had risen from approximately seven percent of U.S. healthcare costs in the 1990s to approximately seventeen percent by 2017, and accounted for approximately twenty percent of Medicare spending).

The Affordable Care Act of 2010 (ACA, *Obamacare*) was the first comprehensive federal attempt to address the system's structural problems since the 1965 Medicare-Medicaid creation. The ACA mandated preventive care coverage, prohibited preexisting-condition exclusion, expanded Medicaid eligibility, and created insurance exchanges with subsidies for households below specified income thresholds. The Act has been politically contested since its enactment. Multiple subsequent legislative efforts have attempted partial repeal; multiple state-level alternatives have been proposed (the most prominent being Massachusetts's 2006 *Romneycare* — the state-level model the ACA was based on, which on subsequent analysis has been judged broadly successful — and California's SB 562 in 2017, an unenacted single-payer proposal estimated at $370-$400 billion in state expenditure annually).

The chapter's framework: the U.S. healthcare system is, as a matter of empirical fact, the most expensive and least effective among peer-country systems. The system is also, structurally, the system that delivers the most revenue to providers per unit of care delivered. The two facts are connected. Reform produces winners and losers among the firms whose revenue depends on the existing structure; the losers have, since 1965, organized to delay reform. Whether the reform pace will accelerate in the late 2020s — under climate-amplified pressure on workforce health, demographic pressure from aging cohorts, and continued fiscal pressure on Medicare — is the live policy question.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that catastrophic-risk insurance is a private-sector product. It is, in the U.S., increasingly a federal product — flood insurance through the NFIP, wind insurance backstopped through state-administered windstorm pools, crop insurance through the Federal Crop Insurance Corporation. The pretense that the private sector handles catastrophic risk is empirically false; the federal taxpayer is the ultimate bearer of much of the catastrophic-risk pool.

The second is that single-payer healthcare is *socialism*. It is, in the technical sense, but the technical sense is much narrower than the political one. Most peer-country systems are not socialist in any broad sense; they are heavily regulated multipayer systems that produce better outcomes at lower cost than the U.S. system. The framing question is whether the United States can adopt elements of those systems — universal coverage, price negotiation, administrative simplification — without adopting the *socialist* label that the political opposition to such reforms has applied.

The third is that healthcare reform is a zero-sum fight between providers and patients. It is, in part. It is also a fight among providers — the providers whose business models depend on the current structure (specialty practices, branded pharmaceutical companies) versus providers whose business models would benefit from reform (primary-care practices, generic pharmaceutical companies, integrated systems). The fight is more complicated than the public framing suggests.

## Integration: applying the chapter's apparatus to a single sector

Consider an emerging professional context the chapter's apparatus has not yet been explicitly applied to: *artificial intelligence in hiring decisions*. The sector is new enough that the regulatory framework is still being constructed; the structural pressures, however, are recognizable from the chapter's existing concepts.

*Concept 1 — founder culture.* The major AI-hiring firms (HireVue, Pymetrics, ZipRecruiter's algorithmic recommendations) are founder-driven entrepreneurial firms operating under the bureaucracy paradox. The founder cultures of those firms shape the operational ethics of their products. The firms whose founder cultures have included serious attention to algorithmic fairness produce, on the available evidence, products with better fairness properties. The firms whose founder cultures have not — and the public cases include products that recommended white candidates over equally-qualified Black candidates, products that deprioritized resumes containing the word *women's*, products whose video-analysis algorithms produced lower scores for non-native English speakers — produce predictably worse products.

*Concept 2 — information asymmetry.* The candidate being screened by an AI hiring system has substantially less information about the system than the firm using it does. The candidate does not know, typically, what features of the candidate's submission are being weighted, how the model was trained, or what the model's known failure modes are. The information asymmetry is structural and resembles the advertising case more closely than the typical employment relationship. Several jurisdictions (New York City's Local Law 144 of 2021, the EU AI Act of 2024) have begun to require disclosure of AI-driven hiring decisions; the regulatory regime is in early development.

*Concept 3 — risk pooling.* The AI-hiring context has its own version of the risk-pooling problem: the candidate population is heterogeneous, the consequences of bad decisions fall on the candidate rather than the employer, and the employer's incentives to invest in fairness are constrained by competitive pressure to use the available tools. Without a regulatory backstop, the equilibrium tends toward use of the most predictively powerful tool regardless of fairness properties. The regulatory backstop is what shifts the equilibrium; the questions are how strong it should be, who should enforce it, and how quickly it should be developed.

The AI-hiring case is a useful integration because each of the chapter's three concepts illuminates a different facet of it. The chapter's apparatus is portable. The frontier sectors will keep producing new versions of the same structural pressures, and the apparatus that recognizes the pressures in entrepreneurship, advertising, insurance, and healthcare will recognize them in AI hiring, in algorithmic lending, in autonomous vehicles, and in whatever the next frontier sector turns out to be.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can recognize the founder-culture-bureaucracy paradox in entrepreneurial firms and identify the structural conditions under which it produces ethical failure. You can apply the dependence-effect framework and the child-vulnerability standard to advertising cases. You can analyze the structural problem of catastrophic-risk pooling in insurance and identify why private-sector solutions are increasingly inadequate. You can compare the U.S. healthcare system to peer-country systems on cost and outcome metrics and articulate the structural reasons for the U.S. outlier status. You can apply the chapter's apparatus to frontier sectors that did not exist when the chapter's source material was written.

The single idea that matters most is that *industries concentrate specific ethical pressures*. The pressures in entrepreneurship are not the pressures in healthcare; the pressures in advertising are not the pressures in insurance. The chapter's apparatus is the recognition that the pressures are *predictable* and *industry-specific*, and that practitioners who know which pressures their industry concentrates will navigate them better than practitioners who do not.

The common mistake to watch for is the assumption that good intentions are sufficient. They are not. The Uber engineers who reported harassment had good intentions; the Uber HR department that received the reports may have had good intentions; the system did not respond to the intentions, it responded to the structural pressures of the founder culture. The chapter's apparatus is about the pressures rather than the intentions, because the pressures are what determine outcomes.

You should now be able to teach the founder-culture-bureaucracy paradox, the dependence-effect framework, and the catastrophic-risk-pooling problem to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in twenty-five minutes, and to apply each to a current case the colleague brings you.
