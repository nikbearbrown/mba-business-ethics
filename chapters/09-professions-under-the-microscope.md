# Chapter 9 — Professions Under the Microscope

*On why the same failure keeps appearing in different industries, wearing different clothes.*

---

On February 19, 2017, a software engineer named Susan Fowler published a blog post on her personal website. She had left Uber thirteen months earlier. The post described, in carefully documented detail, what her year there had looked like: her first manager had propositioned her on her first day on the team; when she reported it to HR, she was told he was a high performer; when she continued to push, her own performance reviews were retroactively downgraded as a basis for removing her; by the time she left, the engineering organization had gone from approximately 25 percent women to approximately 6 percent.

Within forty-eight hours the post had been read by millions of people. Within a week Uber had retained former U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder to lead an investigation. Within four months, Travis Kalanick had been forced out by an ultimatum from the firm's largest investors.

Now here is the thing to notice. Uber had reporting channels. Uber had an HR department. Uber had formal procedures. The apparatus existed. It just didn't function — not until external pressure arrived that the founder could no longer absorb. And this is not unusual. It is the modal pattern for large entrepreneurial firms with founder-culture problems. The apparatus exists; the apparatus doesn't work; the correction arrives from outside.

The chapter is about why that pattern recurs across professional contexts that look nothing like each other. Entrepreneurship, advertising, insurance, healthcare — four sectors with different products and different customers and different regulatory regimes. What they share is a set of structural pressures that are predictable enough that practitioners can prepare for them. The chapter names the pressures. That is all it does. But naming them, in advance, is the difference between navigating the structure and becoming a casualty of it.

## The founder-culture paradox

In 1970, a twenty-two-year-old named Paul Orfalea — who had severe dyslexia and had been told by multiple educators he would never finish college — rented a small storefront near UC Santa Barbara, installed a rented Xerox machine, and opened a copy shop. He named it after his college nickname: Kinko's. By 2004 he had sold it to FedEx for $2.4 billion, with approximately twelve hundred stores and twenty-three thousand employees.

Orfalea later wrote about what the growth had cost him. His management style — intense, personal, often angry — had been an asset when he was working alongside a small team of trusted partners who knew him and could calibrate. As the firm grew, the same style became a liability. He had, over the course of the expansion, alienated nearly every long-term partner he had begun with. The years after the FedEx sale were spent partly in repair.

The Orfalea pattern has a structure. *Entrepreneurial firms, in their early years, are extensions of their founders' personalities. The personality that produces the early success is, often, the same personality that produces the later ethical failure as the firm scales.* The founder's tone becomes the firm's culture. The founder's blind spots become the firm's blind spots. The founder's virtues and vices, multiplied across thousands of employees, become operational features of the firm's relationship to its workforce, its customers, and the world.

Max Weber named the machinery behind this in *Economy and Society* (1925). Bureaucracy, he wrote, is the necessary structural form of large-scale organized activity in modern economies — the substitution of formal procedures for personal judgment, which produces predictability and accountability at scale. Once installed, bureaucracy is *among those social structures which are the hardest to destroy*. It persists. It functions on its own terms. But it does not, by itself, fix bad founder culture. It layers on top of the founder's defaults, leaving the operative texture of the firm shaped by those defaults even as the policies and handbooks grow more elaborate. The Uber HR department functioned perfectly well as a bureaucratic organ. It produced records, it had processes, it documented complaints. What it could not do was override what the founder had made the culture.

This is the paradox. The founding personality produces the success and the vulnerability simultaneously. The remedy — installing professional management with the authority to override the founder's defaults — is what successful large firms have applied at some point in their histories. Steve Jobs was fired from Apple in 1985. He came back, and Apple produced some of its best work. Travis Kalanick was forced out of Uber in 2017. The firm went public two years later at $75 billion. The pattern is: founder builds the thing that only a founder can build, founder culture becomes the structure's liability, the structure corrects. The firms whose founders are willing to step back before the external pressure arrives produce smoother corrections. The ones who are not — Uber, Theranos, WeWork — produce the more disruptive ones.

What is worth tracking about the Uber timeline is the velocity. The structural problems had existed for years. The Fowler post appeared on February 19. Kalanick was out by June. Four months. The external pressure that produced the correction arrived and ran its course faster than the internal apparatus had moved in two years of complaints. That is not a coincidence. It is the information structure of the problem: internal complaints can be absorbed; external pressure cannot. The firms that fix the problem before the external pressure arrives are the firms that survive it gracefully. The mechanism for fixing it early is the same in every case — genuine authority for the internal apparatus to override the founder's defaults — and it requires the founder to choose to install it.

Most founders do not choose this until they have to.

## Advertising and the asymmetry of persuasion

In 1958, the economist John Kenneth Galbraith published *The Affluent Society* and introduced a concept he called the *dependence effect* — the observation that, in modern industrial economies, demand has become dependent on the same production process that supposedly responds to it. The firm produces the good; the advertising creates the want; the want produces the purchase; the purchase funds the advertising. Galbraith argued that the direction of causality most people assume — consumers have needs, firms satisfy them — had been reversed. The argument has been contested for sixty-seven years. It has not gone away.

The contemporary version runs like this. A firm advertises a product. Some of the audience develops a preference they did not previously hold. The preference produces purchase. Where did the preference come from? If it came from the audience's pre-existing needs and the advertising simply matched the product to the need, the loop is benign. If the preference was manufactured in audiences who would not, absent the advertising, have desired the product — that is something else. The asymmetry of information and persuasive resources between the firm and the audience is what makes the loop suspect. The firm knows more about what the advertising does than the audience does.

Start with the easy case. In April 1975, an advertising executive named Gary Dahl conceived of the Pet Rock — a smooth gray stone in a small cardboard box with breathing holes and a thirty-two-page humorous owner's manual. No functional utility. Approximately five million units sold in the first six months. The Pet Rock is the canonical illustration of advertising-manufactured demand for a product with no underlying utility. It is also the canonical illustration of consumer agency: every buyer chose the product, presumably aware of its non-utility. The dependence effect is real, but it does less work in the case of full information.

The cases where it does the most work are the vulnerability cases. Developmental psychology research, replicated across multiple decades, has established that children younger than approximately seven or eight do not reliably distinguish advertising from other adult communication. They treat advertising messages as trustworthy information from adults. The implication is that advertising directed at children operates on an audience that lacks the cognitive machinery to evaluate the persuasive intent. This is not controversial in the research literature. Several countries have adopted restrictive regimes: the United Kingdom, the Nordic countries, Brazil. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission has attempted multiple regulatory regimes since the 1970s with mixed success.

The second class of vulnerability is the self-esteem appeal — advertising that connects to needs the audience holds (love, status, belonging) that the product cannot, on inspection, actually satisfy. The toy advertised to a parent as the proof of love. The car advertised as the proof of masculinity. The cosmetics that promise to make you beautiful in a way you are currently not. These appeals work because the needs are real. What they sell is the promise that the product will satisfy a need that runs deeper than the product. The ethical question is the one Galbraith named: is the persuader using the audience's vulnerability to the audience's benefit, or to the persuader's?

The subliminal advertising scare is worth a brief note because it has distorted the public conversation. In 1957, a market researcher named James Vicary claimed that subliminal messages flashed during a New Jersey movie screening had increased concessions sales. The claim was widely reported; Vance Packard's *The Hidden Persuaders* made it canonical. Vicary later admitted, in 1962, that it had been fabricated. Subsequent research has produced a narrow real finding: subliminal messages can increase the salience of a product in a roughly fifteen-minute window after the message, but only in subjects already primed to be receptive to it. Thirsty subjects shown a subliminal *Drink Coca-Cola* choose Coca-Cola at slightly higher rates. Non-thirsty subjects show no effect. The threat of subliminal advertising has been substantially exaggerated relative to the evidence. The harder ethical territory is the explicit, conscious, well-funded advertising that operates on the documented vulnerabilities of children, the elderly, the financially anxious, and the chronically self-conscious. That advertising is supraliminal — fully visible, fully intentional, and fully legal.

The test the chapter proposes is simple enough to apply: *what information is being delivered, what emotional appeal is being made, and would the audience, on full information, choose to be persuaded by the appeal?* Advertising that passes this test is informational with emotional appeals consistent with what the product actually delivers. Advertising that fails it is manipulation — the use of the audience's genuine needs to move the audience toward a purchase the needs don't actually require. Most contemporary advertising sits somewhere on the spectrum between the two. The practitioner's job is to know where on the spectrum their work sits.

## Insurance, healthcare, and the structural problem of catastrophic risk

In late August 2017, Hurricane Harvey made landfall on the Texas Gulf Coast as a Category 4 storm, stalled over the Houston metropolitan area for roughly four days, and dumped approximately fifty-two inches of rain — the largest single rainfall event in continental U.S. history to that point. The flooding damaged or destroyed approximately two hundred thousand homes. Total property loss: approximately thirty billion dollars. Amount covered by insurance: roughly forty percent. The remaining eighteen billion dollars was uninsured loss borne directly by Houston households.

The reason is structural. Standard U.S. homeowners' insurance covers wind damage. It does not cover flood damage. Flood coverage is sold separately, primarily through the federal National Flood Insurance Program — a federal program, not a private-market product — established in 1968. As of August 2017, approximately five million U.S. households held flood coverage. That is a small fraction of households in flood-prone areas.

The Harvey case exposes a principle that the insurance industry has known for a long time. *Insurance, as a private-sector product, works for risks that are statistically independent across the pool, geographically dispersed, and actuarially priceable. It does not work, on a private-sector basis, for catastrophic risks that are concentrated, correlated, and increasingly amplified by climate change.* The mechanism of insurance is risk pooling: many parties pay small premiums; the pool covers the losses of the few who experience the insured event. The mechanism requires the losses to be dispersed. When a hurricane or a wildfire hits, the losses are not dispersed. Thousands of households are hit simultaneously. The pool is exhausted. The private sector withdrew from flood coverage; the federal government created the NFIP to replace it. The same dynamic is now operating in California wildfire coverage: State Farm, Allstate, and several other major carriers announced partial withdrawals from the California homeowners' market in 2023-24. The private sector is exiting the risk. The federal and state backstops are expanding.

Alongside the structural catastrophe problem sits a discrimination problem. The 2017 *Consumer Reports*–ProPublica investigation found that auto insurers in California, Illinois, Missouri, and Texas were charging higher premiums in predominantly minority neighborhoods than the actual claims history in those neighborhoods could justify. The investigation named major carriers: Allstate, Geico, Liberty Mutual. The practice was illegal under the laws of most of the affected states. Enforcement was limited. This is redlining — the discriminatory pricing of insurance products on the basis of neighborhood characteristics that proxy for race or ethnicity — and it has persisted in the auto insurance market for the same reason it persisted in mortgage lending: the enforcement regime is reactive, depends on individual complaints, and is under-resourced relative to the scale of the practice.

The healthcare case is the same structural problem scaled to the entire system. The U.S. healthcare system is, in international comparison, an outlier on two dimensions simultaneously: it costs the most and it performs the worst. The Commonwealth Fund's 2017 *Mirror, Mirror* comparative study placed the United States last among eleven peer countries on overall system performance while spending substantially more per capita than any of them. The combination has persisted across multiple comparative studies and multiple decades.

Most peer countries operate either single-payer systems — Canada and the United Kingdom, where taxes pay providers directly — or regulated multipayer systems — Germany, France, Japan — where multiple payers operate inside a tightly regulated framework that constrains pricing and ensures universal coverage. The U.S. operates a loosely regulated multipayer system: patients, employers, private insurers, and government programs all pay providers, with substantially more pricing variation than the regulated systems permit. The variation produces the administrative overhead, the prescription drug cost inflation (from approximately seven percent of U.S. healthcare costs in the 1990s to approximately seventeen percent by 2017), and the incentive structures that reward volume over outcome.

The Affordable Care Act of 2010 addressed some of the system's most visible failures — it prohibited preexisting-condition exclusions, expanded Medicaid eligibility, and created insurance exchanges with income-based subsidies. It did not resolve the structural cost problem. The structure that produces the highest per-capita cost in the peer-country comparison also delivers the most revenue to providers per unit of care delivered. Those two facts are connected. Reform produces winners and losers among the firms whose revenue depends on the existing structure; the losers have organized to delay reform since 1965, and they have been largely successful.

The insurance and healthcare cases belong together in one concept because they share the same structural logic. Private-sector risk mechanisms work when the risks are dispersed and priceable. They break down when the risks are catastrophic, correlated, or concentrated in populations that can't pay actuarially fair premiums. When the private mechanism breaks down, the options are: withdraw from the market (which the flood and wildfire insurers are doing), shift the cost to households (which is what the eighteen billion dollars in uninsured Harvey losses represents), or replace the private mechanism with a regulated or public one (which is what the NFIP did and what the ACA partially attempted). The debate about which of those options is correct is a policy debate. The chapter's observation is that the structural breakdown is predictable from the nature of the risk — and that practitioners who understand the structure understand what they're actually selling, and to whom, and at what cost to whom.

## What the three concepts share

Entrepreneurship, advertising, insurance and healthcare. Four sectors. Three structural pressures. The pressures don't look the same on the surface — founder culture is not the same thing as the dependence effect, and neither is the same thing as catastrophic-risk pooling. But they share something at the level of mechanism.

In each case, *the apparatus that is supposed to constrain the ethical failure either does not exist, does not function, or fails under specific conditions that are predictable in advance.* The Uber HR department existed and didn't function. The FTC advertising regulatory regime exists and handles some cases while leaving the vulnerability classes largely intact. The flood insurance system was absent until the federal government created it, and it is chronically under-resourced. The ACA made things better and left the structural cost problem largely intact.

The practitioners who navigate these structures are the ones who know which conditions make the apparatus fail. Founder-culture firms are high-risk during the transition from early-stage to scaled operations, and the apparatus is most likely to fail when the founder's personal authority is highest. Advertising is most likely to exploit vulnerability when the target audience has the least cognitive defense — children, the elderly, the financially anxious. Insurance structures are most likely to leave households exposed when the risk is catastrophic and correlated. Healthcare structures are most likely to perform worst when the pricing mechanism is unconstrained by competition or regulation.

None of this requires bad intentions. The Uber engineers who reported harassment had good intentions. The HR department that received the reports may have had good intentions. The system responded to the structural pressure, not to the intentions. The structural pressure is what the chapter is about. The apparatus is what you build to resist the pressure. And the apparatus has to be built before the external pressure arrives, because once it arrives, you are in Fowler-post territory — four months from the first intervention to the founder's exit, with the correction driven entirely from outside.

The firm that had built the apparatus earlier would have had a different story. It would also, on the available evidence, have been a better firm. Better retention, better talent, better outcomes. The ethical structure and the business structure point in the same direction. They usually do, on the right time horizon.

## Chapter summary

Three structural pressures, industry-specific but recognizable across sectors.

In entrepreneurial firms: the founder culture that produces early success scales into the firm's ethical defaults, and the formal bureaucracy that grows with scale does not, by itself, override those defaults. The apparatus exists; the apparatus doesn't function; the correction comes from outside, faster than the internal apparatus moved. The firms that install genuine override capacity early produce smoother corrections than the firms that don't.

In advertising: the asymmetry of information and persuasive resources between the firm and the audience creates vulnerability classes — children, the elderly, the self-conscious — where the persuasive apparatus operates on audiences that cannot adequately defend against it. The test is whether the audience, on full information, would choose to be persuaded by the appeal. Most advertising passes this test. The advertising that fails it is manipulation, and the vulnerability classes are where the failures concentrate.

In insurance and healthcare: private-sector risk mechanisms work for dispersed, priceable risks and break down for catastrophic, correlated, or concentrated ones. The breakdown is predictable from the nature of the risk. The policy responses — federal backstops, regulated multipayer frameworks, single-payer systems — are the responses to the structural failure of the private mechanism. The system that produces the highest per-capita healthcare cost in the peer-country comparison also delivers the most revenue to providers per unit of care. Those two facts are connected, and the connection is the structure.

The single idea that matters most: the pressures in each sector are predictable, and practitioners who know which pressures their sector concentrates will navigate them better than practitioners who don't. Good intentions are not sufficient. The structure responds to the pressure, not the intentions. The chapter's apparatus is for recognizing the pressure before it produces the Fowler-style disclosure, the Harvey-style coverage gap, or the Commonwealth Fund's last-place ranking. Recognition, in advance, is what preparation looks like.
