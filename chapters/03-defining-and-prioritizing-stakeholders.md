# Defining and Prioritizing Stakeholders

In a Starbucks at the corner of 18th and Spruce in Philadelphia, on the afternoon of April 12, 2018, two Black men in their early twenties — Rashon Nelson and Donte Robinson — sit at a table waiting for a third person, a friend, who has not yet arrived. They have not ordered anything. One of them asks to use the restroom and is told it is for paying customers only. A few minutes later, the store manager calls 911 to report two men trespassing. Six police officers arrive. Nelson and Robinson are arrested in handcuffs while their friend, who has just arrived, watches the scene unfold. They are detained for hours and released. They are not charged with a crime.

A bystander posts a video of the arrest. By the next morning, the video has been viewed more than ten million times.

Within six weeks, Starbucks announces it will close approximately eight thousand U.S. stores for one afternoon to provide racial-bias training to one hundred and seventy-five thousand employees. The company will also change its policy on store use: anyone may sit in a Starbucks, use the restroom, and remain on the premises without making a purchase. Nelson and Robinson settle with the company for an undisclosed sum.

Now ask the question this chapter is going to answer. *Who, exactly, did Starbucks owe a response to in those six weeks?*

The list is long. The two men who were arrested. The manager who placed the call. The police officers who answered it. The other customers who watched. The tens of millions of viewers of the video. The three hundred thousand baristas who would now be sitting in mandatory training instead of working. The sixteen hundred institutional investors who would absorb the cost of an afternoon of closed stores. The competitors who saw an opportunity. The suppliers — coffee growers in Ethiopia and Colombia, milk producers in upstate New York and Wisconsin, paper-cup manufacturers in Indiana — whose orders depended on those eight thousand stores running during the closed afternoon. The regulators who watched to see how a major American company would respond to a flagrant equity failure on national television.

Each of those parties is what Chapter 1 introduced as a *stakeholder*. The chapter you are about to read is the operating manual for working with them. It will introduce you to the categories stakeholders fall into, the procedures for figuring out which of them you owe what to, and the philosophical commitments that make those procedures non-arbitrary. It is the chapter where stakeholder analysis stops being a fashionable phrase and starts being a discipline.

Dotted lines connecting Nelson and Robinson, the manager, and the bystander to the rings — to make the point that the *specific* people involved are not abstractions, they are points in a network.]

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** internal from external stakeholders, primary from secondary, and explain why the law alone cannot tell you whom you owe what.
- **Categorize** any company's stakeholders using the four-type taxonomy (enabling, normative, functional, diffused) and the four-publics framework (nonpublic, latent, aware, active).
- **Apply** a power-interest matrix to prioritize stakeholders under genuine conflict and **defend** the choices the matrix produces.
- **Construct** a triple-bottom-line accounting of a real corporate decision, distinguishing genuine corporate social responsibility from greenwashing.
- **Identify** the warning signs that a company's stated stakeholder commitments and its actual operations have come apart.

## Prerequisites

Chapter 1's three lenses and the cashier's-discount intuition that small choices accumulate. Chapter 2's four traditions, particularly Rawls's veil of ignorance, which becomes operational here. The willingness to make explicit, defensible trade-offs in cases where every option is partly bad — because that is the case stakeholder analysis is built for.

## Why this chapter matters

Most failures of business ethics are not failures of intent. They are failures of *attention*. Someone affected by a decision was not noticed, or was noticed and weighted incorrectly, or was noticed in time but treated as an inconvenience. When you read about a corporate disaster — a recall, a lawsuit, a regulatory action, a viral video — and ask *how did the company not see this coming*, the answer is almost always that the affected party was outside the firm's stakeholder map. The map was incomplete. The chapter is about how to build a complete one and how to use it.

## Concept 1: The stakeholder map — who counts, by category

In late 2017, Samsung Electronics begins receiving complaints about a specific failure mode in its top-loading washing machines. During the spin cycle, the drum can become unbalanced and, in a small fraction of cases, the machine can tip or shake hard enough to injure someone standing nearby. The number of confirmed injuries is small — single digits — relative to the population of machines in use, which is about three million. The price range of the affected washers is roughly $450 to $1,500.

Samsung does the math two ways. The first way is the strict legal-compliance way: identify the specific failure mode, issue a service bulletin, repair affected machines under warranty, fight individual lawsuits as they arise. The total expected cost on this path is in the low hundreds of millions. The second way is the ethical-maximum way: voluntarily recall every affected machine, offer a full refund to every owner regardless of whether their specific unit had ever malfunctioned, and accept the cost. The total expected cost on this path is in the billions.

Samsung chooses the second path.

Notice the structure of the choice. Samsung's *legal* obligation was the first path. Samsung's *stakeholder* obligation — which the firm took seriously — was the second. The two paths were available; the firm picked the more expensive one because, in the firm's own analysis, the long-term cost to its relationship with its three million customers, its retailers, its regulators, and its brand was higher on path one than the multi-billion-dollar cost on path two.

### The mechanism: ethical minimum, ethical maximum, and the gap between

Three terms anchor this concept and they do not all mean what students intuitively assume.

**Ethical minimum** is the least a company can do and still claim to be acting ethically — typically, full compliance with applicable law. The ethical minimum is the floor. It is what a regulator can require. It is what a court can enforce. It is not, on its own, sufficient.

**Ethical maximum** is the strongest action a company can choose to take in a given situation while still operating as a viable for-profit firm. It is the ceiling — bounded not by law but by the firm's continued existence. Samsung's billion-dollar voluntary recall sat closer to the ethical maximum than to the ethical minimum.

**The social responsibility of business** — a phrase that has been argued over for half a century — is the view that stakeholders are not means to the firm's end of profit but ends in themselves. Customers who buy a Samsung washer are not vehicles for delivering revenue to Samsung; they are people whose households have to function and whose toes are not, on any reading of the implicit contract, supposed to be at risk during a spin cycle. Milton Friedman in 1970 famously argued the opposite — that the firm's only responsibility is to its shareholders. The Friedman position has been losing ground, in business education and in corporate practice, for the last twenty years. Lynn Stout, the Cornell law scholar, articulated one version of why in *The Shareholder Value Myth* (2013): shareholders *as a class* benefit when firms treat their stakeholders well, even though *individual* shareholders can sometimes profit by pressing firms to exploit stakeholders. The aggregate-level interest and the individual-level interest diverge, and the divergence creates predictable failure modes when boards listen only to the loudest individual shareholders.

### Categories: four types of stakeholders, four levels of awareness

The stakeholder population of a real firm is too large to manage as an undifferentiated list. Two complementary taxonomies make it tractable.

The first taxonomy comes from organizational theorists who classify stakeholders by *what kind of role they play in the firm's operation*:

**Enabling stakeholders** permit the firm to function within an economic and legal system at all — shareholders, legislatures, government regulators, the board of directors. Lose these and the firm is no longer a firm.

**Normative stakeholders** influence the norms and informal rules of an industry — competitors, peer firms, trade associations, professional bodies. They are the actors whose practices set the unwritten standard you will be measured against.

**Functional stakeholders** are involved in inputs (suppliers, employees, unions) and outputs (customers, distributors, retailers). They are the operating machinery of the business as a going concern.

**Diffused stakeholders** have an interest that may not be direct but can become decisive — NGOs, voters, the mass media, and increasingly, individuals on social platforms whose reach can rival a major newspaper. The Starbucks bystander with a phone is a diffused stakeholder. So was every viewer of the video.

The second taxonomy, from the public-relations theorists James Grunig and Todd Hunt, classifies stakeholders by *how aware they are of an issue*:

A **nonpublic** does not perceive a problem and therefore takes no action.
A **latent public** is affected by a problem but does not yet recognize it.
An **aware public** has recognized the problem.
An **active public** has organized to do something about it.

The two taxonomies stack productively. A *diffused* stakeholder who is currently a *nonpublic* is invisible. The same stakeholder, transitioned to *aware* through a viral video, can become an *active public* in a matter of hours. The Starbucks managers who placed the 911 call were not thinking about diffused stakeholders becoming an active public. They should have been.

### The trade-off: completeness versus tractability

The first taxonomy gives you completeness — every stakeholder fits somewhere. The trade-off is that a complete list is unmanageable. Starbucks could not, in any meaningful operational sense, attend simultaneously to every coffee grower, every barista, every regulator, every customer, every viewer of the bystander video. The second taxonomy gives you tractability — you can prioritize attention to the *active* row at any given moment without ignoring the others.

The deeper trade-off behind the categories is the one between *who counts in principle* and *who can be served in practice*. In principle, every party affected by a firm's decisions has a claim. In practice, claims compete and resources are finite. The choice of *which claims to honor first, when, and how* is the work of Concept 2.

### Worked example: Johnson & Johnson and the Tylenol crisis, fall 1982

In late September and early October of 1982, seven people in the Chicago area die after taking Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules that had been laced with cyanide. The tampering occurred on retail shelves, not in the Johnson & Johnson manufacturing process. The legal exposure of the company is, in the strict sense, limited — the firm did not introduce the poison.

The company's response is worth pausing on, because it became the textbook case for stakeholder primacy in corporate crisis management.

J&J voluntarily recalls every bottle of Tylenol from every U.S. retail shelf — approximately thirty-one million bottles, with a retail value at the time of more than one hundred million dollars (about three hundred and thirty-four million in 2025 dollars). The recall extends nationwide despite the deaths being confined to Cook County, Illinois. The company offers full retail-price refunds for returned product. It introduces tamper-evident packaging — the foil seal under the cap — that within months becomes the industry standard.

Tylenol's market share before the poisoning was approximately thirty-seven percent of the over-the-counter pain-reliever market. By the end of fall 1982, after the recall, it was approximately seven percent. By fall 1983, it was thirty percent and rising. The company recovered its market share within a year.

Apply the stakeholder map. *Enabling stakeholders* — shareholders and regulators — would, on a strict short-term reading, have preferred a narrower recall confined to Chicago. *Functional stakeholders* — retailers and customers nationwide — needed the assurance that any bottle they touched was safe. *Diffused stakeholders* — the public, the media, regulators of other industries watching how a major firm handled a crisis — were watching to see whether J&J would treat its customers as ends or as inconveniences. The decision to recall nationwide was unambiguous on the second and third categories. It cost the first category in the short run and rewarded them, by orders of magnitude, in the long run. The J&J credo — written by former chairman Robert Wood Johnson decades earlier — explicitly listed the stakeholder priority order: first, the consumers; second, the employees and their families; third, the communities; last, the stockholders. The recall was that priority order made operational under genuine pressure.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that stakeholder analysis is "soft" — that it dilutes the firm's focus. The Tylenol case is the standing rebuttal: the firm whose stakeholder commitments were most expensive to act on is the firm whose long-term shareholder return was most preserved.

The second is that stakeholder identification is a one-time exercise. It is not. Stakeholders shift over time, especially across the awareness levels — a *nonpublic* can become an *active public* in twenty-four hours. The firm that mapped its stakeholders five years ago and has not updated the map is operating, today, with a hallucination.

The third is that the law is a sufficient guide. It is not. The legal minimum is calibrated to the worst-behaved firm in the system, not to the obligations of an excellent one. Samsung's recall, the J&J recall, and the Starbucks closure were all responses to obligations the law could not have compelled and the firm chose to honor anyway.

## Concept 2: Prioritization under conflict

It is 2014 and Malaysia Airlines is in the worst year of its operational history. In March, Flight MH370 disappears en route from Kuala Lumpur to Beijing with two hundred and thirty-nine people on board. In July, Flight MH17 is shot down over eastern Ukraine, killing all two hundred and ninety-eight people on board. The airline is owned partly by individual investors and largely by the Malaysian government. Within a year of the second disaster, the Malaysian government effectively takes the company private to attempt a rescue.

By 2016, after the firm has lost three CEOs in two years, a new chief executive named Peter Bellew is installed. Bellew has a turnaround plan. The plan recognizes that more than sixty percent of the Malaysian population is Muslim and that, every year, hundreds of thousands of Malaysian Muslims travel to Mecca for the *hajj* — the pilgrimage that, for those who can afford it, is one of the five pillars of the faith. The hajj season is concentrated in a few weeks of the Islamic calendar each year and produces enormous, predictable demand for charter air service from Southeast Asia to Saudi Arabia.

Bellew proposes that Malaysia Airlines reorient a significant portion of its fleet around hajj charter service. Not as a sideline. As a strategic priority.

The decision is not an obvious one. The airline has *passengers* — every routine traveler from KL to Singapore, Bangkok, Tokyo. It has *the Malaysian government* as the controlling shareholder. It has *competitors* — Singapore Airlines, AirAsia, Emirates — who would happily fill any market gap MAS leaves open. It has *international partners*, *suppliers*, *unionized employees*, and *the families of the victims of MH370 and MH17*. Each of these is a stakeholder. Each has a claim on the firm's strategy. The claims do not all point in the same direction.

### The mechanism: the power-interest matrix and the exigency test

The standard tool for prioritization under conflict is the *power-interest matrix*, sometimes called the salience grid. Two dimensions: how much *power* the stakeholder has to affect the firm's outcomes, and how much *interest* the stakeholder has in the firm's specific decisions. Each dimension is collapsed to *high* or *low* for working purposes, producing four cells:

**High power, high interest** — the *key stakeholders*. These are the parties whose attention you cannot ignore and whose judgment will, if violated, produce immediate and severe consequences. For Malaysia Airlines, this cell includes the Malaysian government (controlling shareholder) and potentially the families of the crash victims (whose moral standing is enormous and whose response to firm decisions will be amplified by media).

**High power, low interest** — the *latent powers*. Parties who could become decisive if their attention is engaged but who currently have no reason to focus on the firm. International regulators are often in this cell. Stay below their threshold of attention and they remain quiet; cross it and they become high-interest very quickly.

**Low power, high interest** — the *interested observers*. Activists, NGOs, journalists, employee groups, and increasingly, social-media-organized publics. They cannot, individually, force the firm to act. They can amplify a story until the high-power cells move.

**Low power, low interest** — *background stakeholders*. Real but currently dormant.

A second dimension overlays the matrix: *exigency* — the level of urgency of the claim. A high-power, high-interest stakeholder with a non-urgent claim is a long-term constraint to design around. The same stakeholder with an urgent claim is a fire to put out today. Both call for attention; the *kind* of attention differs.

### The trade-off: the matrix is reductive but actionable

The power-interest matrix is, by design, a coarse instrument. Reducing every stakeholder to a binary high/low on two dimensions throws away most of the texture of any real situation. A skilled stakeholder analyst supplements the matrix with qualitative notes — what specifically does this stakeholder want, what would change their position, what is their realistic timeline.

The trade-off the matrix accepts is between *precision* and *usability*. A more elaborate model — say, the MITRE Corporation's five-step assessment, which scores each stakeholder along eight dimensions and produces a weighted ranking — is more precise. It is also too time-consuming to run during a crisis. The two-by-two matrix can be filled in on a whiteboard in fifteen minutes and produces an answer that is roughly right. *Roughly right delivered today* beats *exactly right delivered next quarter* in most stakeholder-management situations.

### Worked example: Bellew's decision

Run Malaysia Airlines through the matrix and see what falls out.

The Malaysian government — high power, high interest, high exigency. The airline cannot survive without state support; the government is publicly committed to the rescue and needs visible progress. *Key stakeholder, immediate priority.*

Hajj-bound Malaysian Muslims — low individual power, very high collective interest, seasonally high exigency. They are a potential *active public* every hajj season; collectively they represent a large fraction of the Malaysian flying population. *Functional stakeholder of strategic importance, even though no individual passenger has the power to move the airline alone.*

Routine passengers (the KL-Singapore-Bangkok-Tokyo network) — moderate power individually (frequent fliers can defect), low interest in the firm's strategy beyond their next flight. *Functional, attention-required, but lower priority than the hajj segment in this specific strategic move.*

The families of the MH370 and MH17 victims — low operational power (they cannot move the firm directly), high moral standing, intermittently high media exigency. *A category whose claims must be honored continuously and visibly, regardless of their position on the power axis.*

International competitors — high power to take market share, currently low interest in MAS's specific positioning. *Latent — to be monitored, not engaged.*

Bellew's hajj-charter strategy is a defensible answer to the matrix. It honors the high-power-high-interest *enabling stakeholder* (the government, by providing a strategy that produces revenue and visible progress). It honors a high-collective-interest *functional stakeholder* (hajj travelers) by treating them as a strategic priority rather than as a seasonal nuisance. It does so without ignoring the routine passenger network, which continues to operate. It is also not the obvious move that an outside management consultant would have produced — *focus on hajj* is not in the standard airline-restructuring playbook — and that is a feature: stakeholder-prioritized strategy often diverges from generic best-practice strategy because it is built around *these specific stakeholders*, not stakeholders in general.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that the matrix is a substitute for judgment. It is not. The matrix forces explicit categorization, which forces explicit reasoning, which makes the resulting decision auditable. It does not, on its own, decide. It is the worksheet that makes the decision visible.

The second misconception is that *power* in the matrix means money or position. It does not, at least not exclusively. Power is the capacity to affect the firm's outcomes — through purchase decisions, public statements, regulatory action, employment choices, media coverage. A bystander with a phone has power in this sense. The matrix needs to track that.

## Concept 3: Corporate social responsibility, the triple bottom line, and the cost of greenwashing

In Chiapas, in southern Mexico, a Coca-Cola bottling plant operates twenty-four hours a day. The plant draws water from a regional aquifer that also supplies the surrounding towns. According to public reporting and Coca-Cola's own sustainability disclosures, producing one liter of finished Coca-Cola product requires more than three liters of water — water for the syrup, water for the cleaning of bottles and equipment, water for the operation of the plant. The bottling plant in Chiapas consumes more than one billion liters of water daily. About fifty percent of the surrounding population has running water in their homes.

Mexico has the highest per-capita Coca-Cola consumption in the world.

Coca-Cola publishes detailed sustainability reports. The reports affirm a commitment to *long-term dialogue with diverse stakeholders*, to *strengthening the fabric of communities*, to *prosperity together*. The reports are well-written, measured, professionally edited, and substantially true at the level of corporate policy. They are also, in the specific case of the Chiapas plant, in tension with the operational reality of a community whose access to water is, by ordinary standards of fairness, being shaped by the firm's operational choices.

The chapter is going to put a name to this tension and to the more general phenomenon of which it is one example.

### The mechanism: corporate social responsibility, the triple bottom line, externalities, and greenwashing

**Corporate social responsibility** (CSR) is the philosophy that a company's expected actions extend beyond producing a reliable product at a fair price and paying fair wages — to include caring for the natural environment in which it operates and acting on the social concerns of the communities it touches. CSR was, in the second half of the twentieth century, the response to a growing recognition that the unaccounted side-effects of industrial activity — pollution, displacement, depletion of common resources — could not be left out of the firm's ledger forever.

The technical name for those unaccounted side-effects is **externalities** — the social and environmental impacts of operations that do not show up on the financial statements but are real costs nonetheless, paid by parties other than the firm. The Chiapas water draw is an externality. So is air pollution from a manufacturing plant. So is the displacement of a small farming community by a logistics warehouse.

**The triple bottom line** is the accounting move that brings externalities into the ledger. The phrase was coined in 1994 by John Elkington, a British sustainability consultant. The idea is that a firm should be measured on three simultaneous bottom lines: financial (the traditional profit-and-loss), social (the firm's effects on the people it employs and the communities it operates in), and environmental (the firm's effects on the natural systems it depends on and discharges into). *People, planet, profit* — the three Ps — is the popular shorthand. Triple-bottom-line accounting does not replace financial accounting; it adds two parallel ledgers that have to balance their own books.

**Sustainability** is the operational discipline of running a firm so that none of the three bottom lines is being depleted faster than it can be replenished. The Dow Jones Sustainability Indices, published annually since 1999, attempt to measure this for publicly-traded firms; they are imperfect but they have moved investor attention noticeably in the last twenty years.

**Greenwashing** is what happens when a firm performs the language of CSR without performing the substance — when the sustainability report is well-written and the operations remain extractive. Greenwashing is not the same as inauthentic CSR; it is more specific. It is the *strategic deployment of the appearance of CSR for public-relations purposes while the operational reality is unchanged*. The harm of greenwashing is double: it harms the parties the firm continues to harm operationally, and it crowds out the credit that should accrue to firms doing the actual work, by making the language of sustainability seem cheap.

### The trade-off: triple-bottom-line accounting is honest but expensive

The financial bottom line is calibrated to a familiar unit (currency) and can be summed across activities. The social and environmental bottom lines are not. *How much social benefit was produced by the company's training program?* is a harder question to answer than *how much revenue was produced by Q3 sales*. The social and environmental ledgers require careful operationalization — *what counts, measured how, weighted how* — and that operationalization is contested.

The trade-off the triple-bottom-line accepts is between *full honesty* and *operational simplicity*. A firm that tracks only the financial bottom line knows precisely what it is doing. A firm that tracks all three knows more and can communicate less precisely. The discipline rewards the more comprehensive accounting in the long run because the missing externalities, if not tracked, eventually appear in lawsuits, regulatory action, and brand damage.

### Worked example: Ben & Jerry's mission statement and its supply chain

Ben & Jerry's was founded in 1978 as a small ice-cream stand in Burlington, Vermont. From the beginning, the company took an explicit position on its three bottom lines. The stated mission has three parts: a *Product Mission* (making fantastic ice cream), an *Economic Mission* (managing the company for sustainable financial growth), and a *Social Mission* (using the company in innovative ways to make the world a better place). The triple framing predates Elkington's formal articulation. It was lived practice before it had a name.

Ben & Jerry's was acquired by Unilever — the international consumer-goods conglomerate, co-headquartered in Rotterdam and London — in 2000. The acquisition agreement preserved the Ben & Jerry's mission statement and the company's independent board. Operating as a Unilever subsidiary, Ben & Jerry's continues to publish sustainability reports, source dairy ingredients with documented standards, and donate a portion of profits to social causes.

There is also a tension in the supply chain that the mission statement does not resolve. Some of Ben & Jerry's dairy suppliers operate using *concentrated animal feeding operations* — CAFOs, the industrial-scale dairy and meat production systems whose environmental and animal-welfare records are, on most readings, in tension with the Social Mission's stated commitments. The company has policies regarding humane treatment of dairy cattle and has invested in a *Caring Dairy* program with documented standards. The CAFO problem has not, however, been resolved at the level of the supply chain.

This is a case where greenwashing is the *temptation*, not yet the *outcome*. A firm whose stated mission is sustainable production and whose supply chain still includes CAFO-sourced milk is in a place where a public-relations response (publish a glossy sustainability report) is cheaper than an operational response (rebuild the dairy supply chain). The company has, on the available evidence, done some of both — neither fully greenwashing nor fully aligned. Most CSR cases live in this middle territory. The honest move for the practitioner is to name the gap rather than to deny it, and to commit to closing it on a stated timeline.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that CSR is philanthropy by another name. It is not. Philanthropy is what the firm does with its profits; CSR is how the firm makes its profits. A firm can donate generously and still operate extractively; the donation does not cancel the extraction. Conversely, a firm can be highly profitable through operations that produce genuine social and environmental benefits — that is CSR in its strong form — without doing any traditional philanthropy at all.

The second misconception is that the triple bottom line is a way of pursuing profit. It is not, exactly. It is a way of *accounting* for profit honestly, including the costs that ordinary financial accounting hides. A firm using the triple bottom line might choose to make less money in order to operate more sustainably. The discipline is descriptive about the costs; the choice of what to do about them is the firm's.

The third misconception is that greenwashing is mostly intentional. It can be. More often it is structural — the result of a firm whose marketing department is operating on a different timeline than its operations department, with the marketing department free to make claims the operations department has not yet earned. The remedy is not better marketing language. It is operational alignment.

## Integration: stakeholder analysis on a single decision

A multinational footwear brand — let us call it Strider — discovers, through a journalist's investigation that is about to be published, that one of its second-tier suppliers in a Southeast Asian factory has been employing workers as young as fourteen, paying them below the regional minimum wage, and operating in a building whose fire-safety violations have been documented but unresolved. The supplier provides about eight percent of Strider's footwear by volume. The journalist's piece will run in seventy-two hours.

Strider has options. *Option A:* deny knowledge, blame the supplier, terminate the contract publicly, refuse comment beyond a press release. *Option B:* acknowledge, audit the entire supply chain, fund remediation at the affected factory, commit publicly to a multi-year program with measurable targets. *Option C:* something in between — limited acknowledgment, contract termination, no broader commitments.

Run the chapter's tools through the decision.

*Stakeholder map.* The directly affected stakeholders are the workers at the supplier (low individual power, high interest, very high exigency). The indirectly affected stakeholders include Strider's customers (medium power individually, becoming high power collectively in the era of social-media-organized response), Strider's competitors (latent power, watching), regulators in Strider's home market (high power, currently low interest, becoming high interest as soon as the story breaks), and Strider's investors (high power, currently mixed interest).

*Power-interest matrix.* The high-power-high-interest cell at this moment includes the journalist (whose story will move the other actors), the workers (through the journalist), and Strider's institutional investors (who will be making decisions within hours of publication based on Strider's response). The honest matrix is *centered* on the workers — they are the parties most directly harmed and the parties whose moral standing is highest.

*Triple-bottom-line accounting.* Option A is profit-bottom-line-positive in the short run (no remediation cost) and people-bottom-line-negative (the workers stay in the unsafe factory) and planet-bottom-line-neutral. Option B is profit-bottom-line-negative in the short run (remediation cost, potential supply disruption) and people-bottom-line-positive (the workers' situation is materially improved) and reputation-positive over the medium and long term. Option C is profit-bottom-line-mildly-negative and people-bottom-line-mixed-and-cosmetic.

*The temptation toward greenwashing.* Strider's communications team will, predictably, want to write Option B's *language* without committing to Option B's *operations*. The chapter has named this; the reader will now recognize it when it appears in the draft press release. The remedy is not to rewrite the press release. It is to commit operationally before writing the press release — and then to write the press release in language that the operations can actually fulfill.

Most stakeholder failures are not produced by malice. They are produced by an incomplete map, a mis-applied matrix, and a marketing department writing checks the operations cannot cash. The discipline of the chapter is to make the map complete, the matrix explicit, and the language honest. Doing this consistently, over years, is what corporate-social-responsibility looks like when it works.

### Synthesis

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can build a complete stakeholder map for a real firm, classifying the parties by category and by awareness level. You can apply the power-interest matrix to a decision under genuine conflict and produce a defensible prioritization. You can construct a triple-bottom-line accounting that makes externalities visible. You can recognize greenwashing in a public communication and articulate what operational evidence would distinguish it from substantive CSR. And you can identify, in a stakeholder analysis you encounter, the gaps that the analyst missed — because you know the categories the analyst should have covered.

The single idea that matters most is that *stakeholders are specific*. *The community* is not a stakeholder; *the residents within two kilometers of this plant* are. *Customers* is not a stakeholder; *the three million households that bought the affected washer* are. The discipline of stakeholder analysis is the discipline of forcing yourself to write down the specific names — individual, group, or institution — and to ask, of each, *what does this party want, what is their power to affect us, and what claim do they have on our decision?* Most ethical failures are failures to write down the specific names. The reader has, by now, no remaining excuse for that failure mode.

The common mistake to watch for is the assumption that more stakeholders means more attention divided. The opposite is closer to the truth: more *named* stakeholders means more *focused* attention, because the matrix tells you which to attend to first. A long list of stakeholders, properly prioritized, is easier to manage than a short list of vague ones.

You should now be able to teach the four-cell power-interest matrix and the triple bottom line to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in fifteen minutes, and to apply both to a current case the colleague brings you.
