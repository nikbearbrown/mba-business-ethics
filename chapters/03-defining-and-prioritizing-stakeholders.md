# Chapter 3 — Defining and Prioritizing Stakeholders

*On whom you owe what, and how to know before it becomes expensive to find out.*

---

There is a question hiding inside the Starbucks Philadelphia story that most people never ask.

On April 12, 2018, two Black men — Rashon Nelson and Donte Robinson — sit at a table in a Starbucks at 18th and Spruce, waiting for a friend who hasn't arrived yet. They haven't ordered anything. The manager calls the police. Six officers arrive. Nelson and Robinson are arrested in handcuffs, held for hours, and released without charge. A bystander records the arrest on a phone and posts the video. Within hours, it has been viewed more than ten million times.

Within six weeks, Starbucks closes approximately eight thousand U.S. stores for one afternoon to deliver racial-bias training to one hundred and seventy-five thousand employees. The company changes its open-door policy: anyone may sit in a Starbucks, use the restroom, and remain without purchasing anything.

Here is the question nobody asks: *Who, exactly, was Starbucks responding to?*

The obvious answer is Nelson and Robinson. But that can't be right, not fully. Two people were harmed. The company closed eight thousand stores and trained a hundred and seventy-five thousand employees. The response was orders of magnitude larger than the incident would seem to require if the only parties that counted were the two men who were arrested.

The actual answer is more interesting and harder. Starbucks was responding to a structure — a web of parties who had claims on its behavior, ranging from Nelson and Robinson themselves to the viewers of the video to the baristas who had to show up for training to the institutional investors who absorbed the cost of an afternoon of closed stores. Each of those parties is what this chapter calls a *stakeholder*. The discipline of figuring out who they are, what they want, and which of their competing claims you honor first — that is what this chapter teaches.

It is also, as we'll see, the discipline most companies fail at before their worst crises, not because the framework is complicated, but because working through it honestly is more expensive than not working through it at all.

## What a stakeholder actually is

The word gets used in two ways. The narrow way: anyone with a direct financial stake — shareholders, lenders, suppliers, employees. The broader way, and the more useful one: *anyone who is affected by what a firm does or doesn't do*.

The difference matters enormously in practice. Under the narrow definition, the viewers of the bystander video don't appear. Under the broader one, they're central. They're the reason the response had to be as large as it was.

Freeman's original formulation — and R. Edward Freeman coined the modern stakeholder concept in his 1984 book *Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach* — was deliberately broad. A stakeholder is any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization's objectives. That's almost everyone. And that's the point.

Now, broad doesn't mean unstructured. You need a way to sort the stakeholders into categories that tell you something about how to respond to each one. There are two taxonomies worth internalizing. Think of them as two different ways to cut the same data, each useful for different purposes.

### The first cut: what kind of role do they play?

The first taxonomy classifies stakeholders by the nature of their relationship to the firm.

**Enabling stakeholders** are the parties whose permission or participation allows the firm to exist at all — shareholders, boards, regulators, legislatures. Lose these and you don't have a firm in any meaningful sense.

**Normative stakeholders** shape the informal rules of the industry — competitors, trade associations, professional bodies. They set the unwritten standard you'll be measured against even when the law says nothing.

**Functional stakeholders** are the operating machinery — suppliers, employees, and unions on the input side; customers, distributors, and retailers on the output side. These are the people and organizations through whom the business physically happens.

**Diffused stakeholders** are the category most companies systematically undercount. NGOs, voters, journalists, and now — and this is not a small update — anyone with a phone and a social media account whose reach can rival a major newspaper. The bystander at 18th and Spruce was a diffused stakeholder. So, within hours, were the ten million people watching the video.

That last category is the one that surprises firms. The enabling, normative, and functional stakeholders are legible — they're in the contracts, the org charts, the supplier agreements. The diffused stakeholders are invisible until they're not.

### The second cut: how aware of the issue are they?

The public-relations researchers James Grunig and Todd Hunt introduced a complementary taxonomy that classifies stakeholders not by their role but by their awareness of a problem.

A **nonpublic** doesn't perceive a problem and takes no action.

A **latent public** is affected by a problem but doesn't yet recognize it.

An **aware public** has recognized the problem.

An **active public** has organized to do something about it.

The two taxonomies stack. A diffused stakeholder who is currently a nonpublic is, for practical purposes, invisible. The same stakeholder, reached by a video, can be an active public in an afternoon. This is not a theoretical possibility — it is what happened in Philadelphia, and it is what happens in every large-scale corporate crisis of the last decade.

The manager who called 911 wasn't thinking about diffused stakeholders becoming active publics. The discipline of stakeholder analysis is, in part, the discipline of thinking about that before you need to.

## The ethical minimum, the ethical maximum, and the gap between them

Before you can prioritize stakeholders, you need three reference points.

**The ethical minimum** is the least a company can do and still claim to be acting ethically — typically, full compliance with applicable law. It's the floor. A regulator can require it; a court can enforce it. But the minimum is calibrated to the worst-behaved firm in the system, not to the obligations of an excellent one. Following the law is not, by itself, a moral accomplishment.

**The ethical maximum** is the strongest action a company can choose while still operating as a viable going concern. It's the ceiling, bounded not by law but by the firm's continued existence. Nobody is obligated to go out of business to satisfy a stakeholder claim. But the space between the minimum and the maximum is large, and most of the interesting decisions in business ethics live there.

**The social responsibility of business** — a phrase that has been argued over for fifty years — is the view that stakeholders aren't means to the firm's end of profit; they're ends in themselves. Customers who buy a Samsung washer aren't vehicles for delivering revenue; they're people whose households need to work. Milton Friedman in 1970 argued the opposite: the firm's only responsibility is to its shareholders. The Friedman position has been losing ground for twenty years, partly because Lynn Stout and others showed that shareholders *as a class* benefit when firms treat their stakeholders well, even though individual shareholders can sometimes profit by pressing firms to exploit them. The aggregate interest and the individual interest diverge. Following the loudest individual shareholder produces predictable failure modes.

The Samsung washing-machine recall of 2017 puts all three concepts in the same story.

Samsung discovers a failure mode in its top-loading washers — during the spin cycle, the drum can become unbalanced and, in rare cases, the machine can tip far enough to injure someone nearby. The number of confirmed injuries is small relative to the installed base of about three million machines. The legal-minimum path: issue a service bulletin, repair under warranty, fight individual lawsuits. Expected cost: low hundreds of millions. The ethical-maximum path: voluntary recall of every affected machine, full refunds regardless of whether the specific unit ever malfunctioned. Expected cost: billions.

Samsung took the second path.

The interesting thing is not that Samsung did the right thing. The interesting thing is that Samsung's analysis concluded the second path was also, in the long run, the *less* expensive one — not financially in the short term, but in terms of its relationship with three million customers, its retailers, its regulators, and the brand that earned all of those relationships. The stakeholder-primacy view and the long-horizon self-interest view converged on the same answer. They often do. That's worth knowing.

## Prioritization: the power-interest matrix

Identifying stakeholders is the relatively easy part. The harder part is what you do when their claims conflict — which they always do when a decision actually matters.

The standard tool is the *power-interest matrix*, and it does what all good tools do: it forces explicit categorization, which forces explicit reasoning, which makes the resulting choice auditable.

Two dimensions: how much **power** a stakeholder has to affect the firm's outcomes, and how much **interest** they have in the firm's specific decisions. Each dimension is collapsed to high or low, producing four cells.

**High power, high interest** — the *key stakeholders*. These are the parties you cannot ignore, whose judgment, if violated, produces immediate and severe consequences. For Malaysia Airlines in 2016 — attempting a turnaround after losing two planes in the same calendar year — this cell included the Malaysian government as controlling shareholder and, in a different way, the families of the crash victims.

**High power, low interest** — the *latent powers*. These parties could become decisive if their attention is engaged, but currently have no reason to focus on you. International regulators often live here. Stay below their threshold and they remain quiet. Cross it and they become high-interest very quickly.

**Low power, high interest** — the *interested observers*. Activists, NGOs, journalists, social-media-organized publics. Individually, none of them can force you to act. Collectively, they can amplify a story until the high-power cells move.

**Low power, low interest** — *background stakeholders*. Real, but dormant.

A third dimension overlays the matrix: *exigency* — the urgency of the claim. A high-power, high-interest stakeholder with a non-urgent claim is a long-term constraint to design around. The same stakeholder with an urgent claim is a fire.

### The Malaysia Airlines case

In 2016, new chief executive Peter Bellew proposes that Malaysia Airlines reorient a significant portion of its fleet around hajj charter service — flying Malaysian Muslims to Mecca during the pilgrimage season. Not as a sideline; as a strategic priority.

The decision looks unusual from the outside. The airline has routine passengers — the KL-Singapore-Bangkok-Tokyo network. It has competitors — Singapore Airlines, AirAsia, Emirates — who would happily fill any market gap MAS abandons. It has unionized employees, international partners, suppliers, and the families of the MH370 and MH17 victims. These stakeholders do not all point in the same direction.

Run the matrix.

The Malaysian government — high power, high interest, high exigency. Without state support the airline doesn't survive; the government needs visible progress. Key stakeholder, immediate priority.

Hajj-bound Malaysian Muslims — low individual power, very high collective interest, seasonally concentrated exigency. They represent a large fraction of the Malaysian flying population; they're a potential active public every hajj season. Functional stakeholder of strategic importance, even though no single passenger can move the airline.

Routine passengers — moderate individual power through the option to defect, low interest in the firm's strategy beyond their next flight. Functional, attention-required, but lower strategic priority in this specific moment.

The families of the crash victims — low operational power, high moral standing, intermittently high media exigency. Their claims must be honored continuously and visibly, regardless of their position on the power axis. Moral standing is not the same as power; it still constrains.

Bellew's hajj-charter strategy is a defensible answer to this matrix. It honors the enabling stakeholder (the government) by producing a strategy that generates revenue. It honors a high-collective-interest functional stakeholder (hajj travelers) by treating them as strategic rather than seasonal. It doesn't abandon the routine network. The interesting thing is that this answer is not in the standard airline-restructuring playbook — the matrix produced a strategy *specific to these stakeholders*, not the generic prescription a consultant would deliver.

### What the matrix is not

The matrix is not a substitute for judgment. It's a worksheet that makes your judgment visible. Reducing every stakeholder to a binary high/low on two dimensions throws away texture; a skilled analyst supplements it with qualitative notes — what specifically does this party want, what would change their position, what is their realistic timeline.

It also isn't a guarantee of ethical correctness. A firm could run this matrix in 1960, place African-American customers correctly in the low-power-high-interest cell, and use that placement to justify ignoring them. The matrix tells you where the pressure is; it doesn't tell you whether the current distribution of power is just. Those are different questions, and both matter.

## Corporate social responsibility, the triple bottom line, and the cost of greenwashing

There is a Coca-Cola bottling plant in Chiapas, in southern Mexico. It operates twenty-four hours a day. It draws water from a regional aquifer that also supplies the surrounding towns. Producing one liter of finished Coca-Cola product requires more than three liters of water. The plant consumes more than one billion liters of water daily. About fifty percent of the surrounding population has running water in their homes.

Mexico has the highest per-capita Coca-Cola consumption in the world.

Coca-Cola publishes detailed sustainability reports affirming commitment to *long-term dialogue with diverse stakeholders*, to *strengthening the fabric of communities*, to *prosperity together*. The reports are well-written, measured, and substantially true at the level of corporate policy. They are also, in the specific case of the Chiapas plant, in tension with an operational reality in which the firm's water draw is shaping a community's access to a basic resource.

That tension has a name.

**Corporate social responsibility** is the view that a firm's obligations extend beyond making a reliable product at a fair price — to include the natural environment in which it operates and the social concerns of the communities it touches. It is the recognition that industrial activity produces unaccounted side-effects that don't show up on the financial statements but are real costs nonetheless, paid by parties other than the firm. The technical name for those costs is **externalities**.

**The triple bottom line** brings externalities into the ledger. John Elkington coined the phrase in 1994, but the idea is simple: measure the firm on three simultaneous bottom lines — financial (the traditional profit-and-loss), social (effects on the people it employs and the communities it operates in), and environmental (effects on the natural systems it depends on and discharges into). *People, planet, profit* — the three Ps. Triple-bottom-line accounting doesn't replace financial accounting; it adds two parallel ledgers that have to balance their own books.

**Greenwashing** is what happens when a firm performs the *language* of corporate social responsibility without performing the *substance*. The sustainability report is well-written. The operations are unchanged. The harm of greenwashing is double: it harms the parties the firm continues to harm operationally, and it cheapens the language of sustainability for the firms doing the actual work.

The Johnson & Johnson Tylenol case is the foil. In late September and early October of 1982, seven people in the Chicago area die after taking Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules laced with cyanide. The tampering occurred on retail shelves, not in J&J's manufacturing process. The company's legal exposure is limited. They voluntarily recall thirty-one million bottles from every U.S. retail shelf — a retail value at the time exceeding one hundred million dollars. Tylenol's market share drops from thirty-seven percent to seven percent. Within a year, it rebounds to thirty percent.

The J&J credo, written by former chairman Robert Wood Johnson decades before the crisis, listed stakeholder priority explicitly: consumers first, then employees and their families, then communities, then stockholders. The recall was that priority order made operational under genuine pressure. The credo was not a marketing document. It was a decision rule that produced a specific answer when the pressure was real.

Most firms in the Chiapas situation are not J&J. They are firms whose marketing department is operating on a different timeline than their operations department, with marketing free to make commitments the operations haven't yet earned. The remedy is not better marketing language. It is operational alignment — closing the gap between stated priority and actual practice, measurably, on a stated timeline.

The common failure mode is to assume that more comprehensive reporting *is* the alignment. It isn't. Ben & Jerry's has had a three-part mission — product, economic, social — since before Elkington named the triple bottom line. Since being acquired by Unilever in 2000, the company continues to publish sustainability commitments and to operate with CAFO-sourced dairy in parts of its supply chain. The honest position is to name the gap rather than to deny it. Most CSR cases live in that middle territory: neither full greenwashing nor full alignment. The discipline is to keep the distance between the two visible.

## Putting it together: the Strider case

A multinational footwear brand — call it Strider — learns, through a journalist who is about to publish, that one of its second-tier suppliers in Southeast Asia has employed workers as young as fourteen, paid below regional minimum wage, and operated in a building with documented fire-safety violations. The supplier provides eight percent of Strider's footwear by volume. The story runs in seventy-two hours.

Map the stakeholders. The workers — low individual power, very high interest, very high exigency. Strider's customers — medium power individually, potentially high power collectively through organized response. Regulators in Strider's home market — high power, currently low interest, becoming high interest when the story breaks. Institutional investors — high power, currently mixed interest.

Apply the matrix. The high-power-high-interest cell at this moment includes the journalist, the workers (through the journalist's amplification), and the institutional investors. The honest matrix centers on the workers — they're the parties most directly harmed and the parties whose moral standing is highest.

Run the triple bottom line. Denying knowledge and terminating the supplier contract is profit-bottom-line-positive in the short run, people-bottom-line-negative, and reputation-negative over the medium term. A full audit, remediation funding, and a multi-year commitment with measurable targets is profit-bottom-line-negative in the short run and people-bottom-line-positive, with reputation-positive effects over the medium and long term.

Watch for greenwashing. Strider's communications team will be tempted to write Option B's language without committing to Option B's operations. The matrix made the decision; now the press release needs to be constrained by what the operations can actually deliver.

Most stakeholder failures are not produced by malice. They're produced by an incomplete map, a mis-applied matrix, and a communications team writing checks the operations can't cash. The discipline of this chapter is to make the map complete, the matrix explicit, and the language honest. That combination, practiced consistently, is what corporate social responsibility looks like when it works.

## Chapter summary

A stakeholder is any party who can affect or be affected by the firm's decisions. The category is broader than most firms instinctively draw it, and the failure to draw it broadly enough is the proximate cause of most corporate ethics crises.

Stakeholders can be sorted by role — enabling, normative, functional, diffused — and by awareness level — nonpublic, latent, aware, active. A diffused stakeholder who is currently a nonpublic can become an active public in hours. The firm that hasn't mapped its diffused stakeholders hasn't mapped its risk.

The ethical minimum is legal compliance. The ethical maximum is the strongest action consistent with continued operation. The interesting decisions live in between.

The power-interest matrix — two-by-two, power versus interest — is a worksheet for prioritization under conflict. It doesn't decide; it makes the reasoning explicit. Supplement it with qualitative notes. Update it when the situation changes, because stakeholder positions change.

Corporate social responsibility means taking the full stakeholder map seriously, including externalities the financial statements hide. The triple bottom line — people, planet, profit — is how to make that accounting explicit. Greenwashing is the gap between stated commitment and operational reality. The discipline is to keep that gap visible and to close it.

The single idea that matters most: stakeholders are specific. *Customers* is not a stakeholder. The three million households that bought the affected washer are. *The community* is not a stakeholder. The residents within two kilometers of the plant are. Most failures of business ethics are failures to write down the specific names. The categories and frameworks in this chapter are the tools for doing that work before the crisis makes the cost of skipping it visible.
