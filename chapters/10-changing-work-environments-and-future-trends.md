# Changing Work Environments and Future Trends

In a McDonald's restaurant in suburban Chicago, on a Tuesday afternoon in 2017, a customer walks up to a touchscreen kiosk near the entrance, navigates a series of menu screens, customizes her order, swipes a credit card, and receives an order number. The transaction takes about ninety seconds. No human cashier is involved. The kiosk costs the restaurant approximately $35,000 to install. A human cashier — at the federal minimum wage of $7.25 per hour — would cost approximately $15,000 per year in direct wages, plus roughly thirty percent more in payroll taxes and benefits. The kiosk pays for itself in approximately eighteen months. After that it earns the restaurant pure margin.

McDonald's, by its own published accounting, has reduced its global workforce by approximately fifty percent since the 1960s while serving a substantially larger customer base. The reduction has come from automation in waves — the introduction of standardized food-preparation processes in the 1960s and 70s, the digitization of order management in the 1990s, the rise of mobile-app ordering in the 2010s, and now the kiosk wave. The restaurant in suburban Chicago is one expression of a process that has been running for sixty years and that the McKinsey Global Institute, in its widely-cited 2017 study, projected would accelerate substantially over the following decade — with approximately eight hundred million jobs globally, or about twenty percent of the global workforce, displaced by automation by 2030.

Eight hundred million is a number worth pausing on. It is roughly the entire workforce of China. It is approximately twice the entire population of the United States. The McKinsey study estimated, for the United States specifically, that between thirty-nine and seventy-three million U.S. workers — between one-quarter and one-half of current U.S. employment — would experience significant occupational disruption. Approximately twenty million would transition to comparable jobs without retraining. The remaining nineteen to fifty-three million would require retraining, redeployment, or extended periods of unemployment.

This chapter is about the work environments those workers will inhabit, the new forms of employment that are emerging to replace older forms, and the ethical questions the transitions raise. The chapter argues that the questions are not, primarily, questions about technology. They are questions about how the costs and benefits of the transitions are distributed across stakeholders — workers, firms, customers, taxpayers, communities — and about who bears responsibility for the parties on whom the costs concentrate.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Evaluate** the ethical case for and against telecommuting using documented productivity data and Marissa Mayer's 2013 reversal at Yahoo.
- **Analyze** the workplace-campus model (Apple Park, Facebook Willow Village) as a hybrid of historical company towns and contemporary work-life integration, and **identify** the structural ethical questions it raises.
- **Apply** the gig-economy framework to a contemporary case, including the Microsoft 2000 contract-worker settlement and the 2018 Freelancers Union estimate that thirty-five percent of U.S. workers operate in some gig-economy capacity.
- **Argue** for a defensible response to the McKinsey 2017 automation projections, including which sectors face the most acute displacement and which retraining, redeployment, or income-support frameworks are most likely to mitigate the transition costs.
- **Identify** the unresolved ethical questions raised by AI-driven workplace decisions, including disclosure to customers, liability assignment, and the developing regulatory framework in the EU AI Act of 2024.

## Why this chapter matters

Most readers of this book will work for thirty to fifty years across a labor market that will look substantially different at the end of their career than at the beginning. The chapter is about how to anticipate the changes the working world will go through and about the ethical posture to take toward those changes — both as an employee navigating them and as a manager whose decisions will shape how others navigate them.

## Concept 1: Flexibility as a double-bind — telecommuting, campuses, and what gets traded

In late February 2013, the recently-installed CEO of Yahoo, Marissa Mayer, announced an internal policy change requiring all Yahoo employees who had been working remotely to return to one of the company's offices by June 1. The memo, leaked within hours of its distribution, defended the policy on the grounds that *speed and quality are often sacrificed when we work from home* and that *some of the best decisions and insights come from hallway and cafeteria discussions*. The reaction was immediate. Industry commentators generally criticized the move; remote-work advocates noted the tension between the policy's framing and Mayer's own work-from-home accommodations during her then-recent maternity leave.

The Mayer memo was the high-profile early-2010s expression of a debate that has, in 2026, substantially reversed. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020-22 converted remote work from a niche arrangement into the baseline practice for most knowledge workers; the partial return-to-office push of 2022-25 has had mixed results, with many large employers settling on hybrid arrangements (two to three days in office, two to three days remote). The debate has not gone away. It has shifted from *whether to permit remote work* to *how to structure remote work in ways that produce the productivity gains without the cultural and equity costs*.

### The mechanism: documented productivity gains, real cultural costs

Several specific data anchors are worth carrying.

**Productivity.** The most-cited single study is Nicholas Bloom's 2014 *Quarterly Journal of Economics* paper on a Chinese travel-agency call center, in which a randomized trial of work-from-home arrangements produced a 13.5 percent increase in completed calls per worker, a 50 percent reduction in voluntary resignations, and statistically significant gains in worker satisfaction. Other documented studies (JD Edwards: 20-25 percent productivity gain; American Express: 43 percent gain on relevant tasks) support the same direction of effect. The productivity case for remote work, in the documented research, is positive.

**Cultural and equity costs.** The case against remote work is not, primarily, a productivity case. It is a culture and equity case. Workers who are not visible in the office are, on extensive HR research, less likely to be promoted than equally-productive in-office workers. The mechanism is *visibility bias* — the cognitive tendency of managers to weight what they observe more heavily than what they cannot. The bias produces the structural inequity remote-work advocates have been warning about since the 1990s. The mitigation — more deliberate documentation of remote workers' contributions, structured promotion-review processes that explicitly account for visibility-bias risk — is operationally demanding and many firms have not implemented it.

**The MIT pilot.** A 2016 pilot at MIT's executive education program structured a hybrid arrangement (Wednesday in office, the remaining days remote-eligible). The pilot reported 100 percent continuation rate after six months — every participating employee chose to maintain the arrangement. The MIT model has been widely cited as the template for hybrid arrangements that capture remote work's productivity gains while preserving the in-office collaboration the Mayer memo named.

### The campus model: Apple Park, Willow Village, and the company-town comparison

The opposite end of the spectrum from telecommuting is the workplace campus — the immersive corporate environment that the major U.S. technology firms have built or are building. Apple Park, the Cupertino headquarters that Apple opened in 2017, occupies approximately 2.8 million square feet at an estimated construction cost of $5 billion. The campus is circular ("the Ring"), surrounded by approximately 9,000 drought-resistant trees, and powered substantially by on-site solar generation. It accommodates approximately 12,000 Apple employees. The design has been criticized — by urbanists and by Apple-watchers alike — as inward-facing, isolated from the surrounding community, and accessible primarily by private car.

Facebook's Willow Village, in Menlo Park, is a more ambitious version of the same logic. The development plan includes approximately 1,500 housing units, retail space, a grocery store, a hotel, and parks, on a site adjacent to Facebook's existing campus. Approximately 15 percent of the housing units are reserved for low-income tenants under California's inclusionary-housing requirements. The development is, in effect, a small city that the firm both builds and partly inhabits.

The historical comparison is the *company town* of the late 19th and early 20th centuries — Pullman, Illinois (the railroad-car manufacturer's town outside Chicago, founded 1881); Hershey, Pennsylvania (the chocolate manufacturer, founded 1903); Kohler, Wisconsin (the bath-fixtures manufacturer); Ford's various Michigan factory towns. Company towns were paternalistic — the firm provided housing, schools, healthcare, recreation. They were also monopsonistic — the firm was effectively the only employer, which suppressed wages and limited workers' ability to leave. The Pullman Strike of 1894 was, in part, a worker rejection of the model. The model declined through the twentieth century as automobile ownership made commuting practical and as labor regulation constrained the monopsonistic dynamics. The contemporary tech-campus model is not a literal return to the company town — workers retain freedom of exit, the firms are not the only employers in their regions, the labor market is broadly competitive — but the structural similarities are real and the ethical questions transfer.

### The trade-off: convenience for capture

The campus model offers real benefits to workers. On-site amenities (gyms, dining, childcare, healthcare). Reduced commute friction. A degree of work-life integration that workers commuting from distant suburbs cannot match. The trade-offs are subtler. The amenities lock workers geographically — leaving the campus involves leaving the housing arrangement, the daily routines, the social network the campus has facilitated. The convenience produces a form of capture. The firm benefits from the reduced workforce mobility; the worker may or may not.

The chapter's framework: campus amenities are net-positive for workers when they are non-coercive — when the worker can decline to use them, or use them selectively, without consequence. They are net-negative when they become operational expectations. Acuity Insurance's $130 million Sheboygan campus — including a climbing wall, a community theater, an organic garden — is on the documented evidence operating in the non-coercive mode. Whether the Apple Park and Willow Village models produce the same outcome over time is, in 2026, contested.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that remote work produces lower productivity. The documented research generally supports the opposite conclusion for most knowledge work, with the cultural and equity costs being the more substantial concerns.

The second is that workplace campuses are unprecedented. They are not; the company-town comparison provides historical perspective on the structural dynamics.

The third is that hybrid arrangements are a compromise that captures neither the remote-work nor the in-office benefits. The MIT pilot and similar evidence suggest the opposite — well-structured hybrid arrangements often capture most of the productivity gains of remote work while preserving the in-office collaboration that purely-remote arrangements lose.

## Concept 2: Displacement at scale — the gig economy, automation, and the precariat

A graphic designer named Joseph — not his real name, but representative of a documented class of worker — assembles his weekly income from approximately five sources. Logo design for small businesses through an online platform. Per-project work for two regional advertising agencies. Album-cover design for two independent music labels. Occasional contract work for a former employer who calls when capacity is needed. Nights and weekends, a small e-commerce side project of his own. His total annual income is approximately $58,000, comparable to the median graphic-designer salary in his metropolitan area. He has no employer-provided health insurance; he buys a high-deductible plan through the state ACA exchange. He has no retirement plan; he contributes to a personal IRA when his cash flow permits. He pays both halves of FICA — approximately 15.3 percent of his gross income — because he is, for tax purposes, self-employed. His monthly income varies by approximately 40 percent from highest to lowest month.

Joseph is one of approximately fifty-five million U.S. workers — about thirty-five percent of the U.S. workforce, by the Freelancers Union's 2018 estimate — who participate in some form of gig-economy work. The figure has continued to grow; current 2026 estimates place the share at approximately forty percent of the U.S. workforce, with substantial regional variation.

The gig economy is the second of three displacement forces this concept is built around.

### The mechanism: gig-economy classification, automation, and the *precariat*

Several specific concepts anchor the displacement discussion.

**The gig economy** is the labor-market arrangement in which workers are engaged through short-term contracts, project-based assignments, or platform-mediated tasks rather than through long-term employment. Gig work has existed for as long as freelance professional services have existed; the contemporary version's distinguishing features are platform mediation (Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, Upwork, Fiverr) and the corresponding scaling of what was historically a niche labor-market segment. Approximately one-third of gig workers, by Freelancers Union surveys, prefer the gig arrangement to traditional employment. Approximately two-thirds did not choose gig work as their preferred arrangement; they chose it because traditional employment was not available on terms they could accept.

**Worker classification** is the legal question that determines whether a gig worker is, for purposes of employment law, an *employee* (entitled to minimum-wage protection, overtime, employer-provided health insurance, employer FICA contribution, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation) or an *independent contractor* (entitled to none of the above). The classification has been one of the most contested workplace-policy questions of the past decade. California's 2019 *AB 5* legislation, modeled on the *Dynamex* decision, attempted to reclassify many gig workers as employees; the 2020 Proposition 22, supported by approximately $200 million in industry spending, partially reversed the legislation for app-based drivers. The federal *Department of Labor* under multiple administrations has issued conflicting rules on classification. The U.K. Supreme Court's 2021 *Uber B.V. v. Aslam* decision classified Uber drivers as workers under U.K. employment law. The European Union's *Platform Work Directive* of 2024 establishes a presumption of employment status for platform workers in EU member states.

**The precariat** is the term coined by the British labor economist Guy Standing for the new social class of workers whose employment relationship is, in his framing, *precarious* — short-term, low-benefit, geographically mobile, lacking the stability that the post-1945 *standard employment relationship* provided. The precariat is not a niche phenomenon; Standing's analysis estimates that approximately 25 to 30 percent of the workforce in advanced economies operates in conditions that fit the description.

### Automation: McKinsey 2017 and the displacement projections

The McKinsey Global Institute's December 2017 report *Jobs lost, jobs gained: Workforce transitions in a time of automation* is the most-cited single analysis of the contemporary automation question. The report's headline projection — that approximately 800 million workers globally, or about 20 percent of the global workforce, would face significant occupational disruption from automation by 2030 — has been the reference point for subsequent policy discussion.

The report's U.S.-specific projection: between 39 and 73 million U.S. workers (between one-quarter and one-half of current U.S. employment) face significant disruption. Approximately 20 million would transition to comparable jobs with limited retraining; 19 to 53 million would require substantial retraining or extended unemployment.

The sectors at highest risk, on the McKinsey analysis: machine operators, factory workers, food workers (especially in fast food), bookkeepers and paralegals, cashiers, toll operators, and most categories of driver — taxi, truck, delivery — as autonomous-vehicle technology matures. The Bureau of Labor Statistics' independent projections converge on similar figures: 80,000 fast-food jobs disappearing by 2024, 7.5 million retail jobs at risk from self-checkout, 5 million driving jobs at risk in the early 2020s.

The sectors at lowest risk are sectors where the work involves substantial human-to-human interaction with cognitive and emotional content that current AI cannot reliably replicate: nurses, physicians, lawyers, teachers, social workers (Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 19 percent growth in social-work employment through 2024), bartenders, hairstylists, sports coaches, songwriters. The pattern is recognizable. Work that requires the recognition of nuanced human emotional state and the response to it is, on current AI capability, substantially safer than work that involves pattern-matching, classification, and routinized response.

The developing-nation comparison is worth noting. McKinsey's projections for India estimate approximately 9 percent of the Indian workforce facing significant disruption — substantially below the U.S. figure. The reason is not that India is more technologically advanced or more humanely organized. The reason is that Indian labor is, in many sectors, sufficiently inexpensive that automation is not yet cost-justified. The displacement pattern in advanced economies reflects, in part, the relative cost of labor versus capital.

### The Microsoft 2000 settlement: a structural precedent

The clearest legal precedent for the gig-classification question is the Microsoft contract-worker settlement of 2000. From approximately 1986 through the late 1990s, Microsoft had retained large numbers of workers — sometimes for years at a time — through staffing agencies, classifying them as independent contractors. The contractors performed work substantially identical to Microsoft employees', but received no employer-provided health insurance, retirement plan contributions, or stock options. A class-action lawsuit (*Vizcaino v. Microsoft Corp.*) was filed in 1992. The case settled in December 2000 for approximately $97 million, with the plaintiffs receiving back-payment for stock-option benefits that the courts had found they were entitled to under the legal definition of *employee* applicable at the time. Microsoft subsequently restructured its contractor relationships and now requires its staffing partners to provide benefits to long-tenured contractors.

The case is the precedent the contemporary gig-classification fights are working off. The legal definitions have shifted; the underlying structural pressure — firms hiring through staffing arrangements to avoid employer obligations — has not. The chapter's framework is to recognize the pressure and to evaluate specific cases on the substantive questions: how much control does the firm exercise over the worker's daily activity, how exclusively does the worker work for the firm, how integral is the worker to the firm's core operation?

## Concept 3: AI, accountability, and the personhood question

In December 2022, OpenAI released ChatGPT, a conversational interface to a large language model. Within five days the system had passed one million registered users; within two months, one hundred million. The release was the first mass-market introduction of generative AI capable of producing human-grade prose, code, summaries, analyses, and creative content on demand. The subsequent three years (2023-2025) produced rapid deployment of similar systems by Anthropic, Google, Meta, and others, and the integration of AI capabilities into most major productivity software, customer-service systems, hiring platforms, and creative tools.

The contemporary version of the workplace-AI question is not whether AI will affect work — it is affecting work, in measurable ways, in 2026. The questions are about how the affected workers are treated, how the affected customers are informed, how the affected legal systems assign liability, and how the affected societies adapt to the rapid pace of change.

### The mechanism: disclosure, liability, and the personhood question

Several specific ethical questions anchor the AI-workplace discussion.

**Disclosure to customers.** When a customer interacts with a customer-service system, must the firm disclose whether the system is human or AI? The U.S. Federal Trade Commission has issued guidance on the question without enacting binding rules; California's *B.O.T. Act* of 2018 requires disclosure for certain commercial interactions; the EU AI Act of 2024 requires disclosure for AI systems in many commercial contexts. The U.S. national framework is in development.

**Liability assignment.** When an AI-driven system makes a consequential decision — denying a loan, recommending a hire, diagnosing a patient — and the decision turns out to be wrong, who is liable? The firm that deployed the system, the firm that built the model, the data providers whose data was used to train it, the engineers who specified the model's parameters? The legal answer is, in 2026, not settled in the U.S. The EU AI Act establishes a tiered liability regime for high-risk AI applications; U.S. case law is being constructed through specific cases working through the courts.

**The personhood question.** As AI systems become more capable of producing human-grade output, the question arises whether they have, or should have, some form of moral or legal standing. The contemporary philosophical consensus is that current AI systems do not have consciousness or moral patient status in the traditional sense. The technical question — *what would count as evidence that an AI system had consciousness, and how would we recognize it* — remains contested. The legal question — *should AI systems have any independent standing in any legal context* — has not been answered affirmatively in any major jurisdiction. The chapter's framework is that the questions are open and that the operational ethics for AI deployment should not depend on their resolution. The treatment of AI systems as instruments, with humans bearing the responsibility for their use, is the safer and more defensible position pending answers to the deeper questions.

### KPMG's 50 percent: what is automatable, what is not

KPMG's 2017 analysis estimated that approximately 50 percent of current workplace activities are technically automatable using existing technology. The figure is widely cited and was used as the basis for some of the McKinsey projections discussed in Concept 2. It is also worth specifying carefully. *Activities*, in the KPMG analysis, are not the same as *jobs*. Most jobs consist of bundles of activities, some of which are automatable and some of which are not. A job whose activities are 50 percent automatable is not a job that disappears; it is a job that, post-automation, consists primarily of the activities that were not automated, often performed by fewer workers. The displacement is real; the framing should be precise.

The activities most resistant to automation, on the available evidence, are activities that involve unstructured human-to-human interaction (counseling, complex negotiation, education that responds to individual student needs), activities that involve manual dexterity in unstructured environments (much of skilled-trades work), and activities that involve novel creative judgment (some forms of artistic creation, complex strategic decision-making). The activities most susceptible to automation are activities that involve pattern-matching against well-defined inputs, classification of standardized cases, and execution of well-specified procedures.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that AI displacement is unprecedented. It is not. The agricultural transition of the 18th and 19th centuries, the manufacturing transition of the late 20th, and the contemporary services transition follow recognizably similar patterns: a technology emerges, it displaces workers in one set of occupations, it creates new occupations in others, the transition produces concentrated short-term pain and dispersed long-term benefit. The pattern is not new. The specific technology and the speed of the transition are.

The second is that the displaced workers will retrain into the new occupations. The available evidence on retraining outcomes is mixed. Retraining works for some workers (typically younger, with prior education, in regions with active labor demand); it does not work for others (typically older, with deep specialization in displaced occupations, in regions where the new occupations are not located). The aggregate retraining figure obscures the distributional reality.

The third is that AI is the same kind of technology as previous waves. Several features of the current AI wave — the speed of capability advance, the breadth of activities affected, the difficulty of specifying when an AI system is or is not appropriate to deploy — are different from previous waves and may produce qualitatively different outcomes. The framework for the previous waves may not, on these dimensions, generalize.

## Integration: a single firm through the chapter's apparatus

A mid-sized U.S. financial-services firm — call it Northbeam — has approximately 2,800 employees and is contemplating three simultaneous workplace transformations: (a) full transition to a hybrid work model, with two days in-office mandated and three days remote-eligible; (b) deployment of an AI-driven customer-service system that would handle approximately 60 percent of routine customer inquiries; (c) restructuring of approximately 400 customer-service positions through a combination of attrition, voluntary buyouts, and redeployment to roles emphasizing complex case handling.

Run the chapter's apparatus.

*Concept 1 — flexibility.* The hybrid model is, on the documented evidence, likely to produce productivity gains and improved retention. The structural risk is visibility bias in promotion decisions for the workers who choose the more-remote end of the eligible-arrangement spectrum. The mitigation is operational: structured promotion-review processes that explicitly account for visibility-bias risk.

*Concept 2 — displacement.* The 400-position restructuring is the displacement event for this firm. The question is how the costs are distributed. Voluntary buyouts on generous terms shift costs from displaced workers to the firm. Aggressive attrition without redeployment shifts costs onto the displaced workers and the regional labor market. The mid-path — voluntary buyouts plus structured redeployment plus retraining support for those willing — is the path consistent with the chapter's apparatus.

*Concept 3 — AI accountability.* The customer-service AI deployment requires answers to specific questions. Will customers be informed when they are interacting with the AI? Will customers have an option to escalate to a human agent? Who is responsible when the AI provides incorrect information that causes customer harm? What records will be maintained of AI decisions, and for how long? The questions are not optional; they will be answered, either deliberately by the firm or by the regulatory framework that is, in 2026, rapidly closing the gap.

The integration verdict is the one the chapter has been working toward. *The transitions of the contemporary work environment can be handled well or badly by specific firms making specific decisions.* The firms that anticipate the structural pressures handle them better. The firms that handle them better produce better outcomes for workers, customers, and shareholders alike. The chapter's apparatus is the recognition that the pressures are predictable and the appropriate responses are known.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can evaluate the productivity, cultural, and equity arguments for and against telecommuting, hybrid work, and full-return-to-office models, using documented research rather than ideology. You can analyze the workplace-campus model as a hybrid of the historical company town and the contemporary work-life-integration trend. You can apply the gig-economy classification framework to specific cases, including the Microsoft 2000 settlement and the contemporary Uber/Lyft fights. You can articulate the McKinsey 2017 automation projections with the specificity they require, including both the aggregate figures and the sector-specific patterns. You can identify the unresolved ethical questions raised by AI-driven workplace decisions and the developing regulatory framework that is beginning to address them.

The single idea that matters most is that the work environments of the next thirty years will be different from the work environments of the last thirty in ways that are predictable in their direction and uncertain in their pace. The reader's career will navigate the transitions. The chapter's apparatus is the recognition that the transitions are not random; they are responses to documented technological and economic forces operating in patterns that previous transitions illuminate.

The common mistake to watch for is the assumption that the contemporary moment is unprecedented. It is not, in most respects. The agricultural-to-industrial transition, the industrial-to-services transition, and the contemporary services-to-AI-augmented-services transition follow recognizable patterns. The specific technology is new; the structural dynamics are familiar.

You should now be able to teach the productivity case for remote work, the gig-economy classification framework, and the McKinsey automation projections to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in twenty minutes, and to apply each to a current case the colleague brings you.
