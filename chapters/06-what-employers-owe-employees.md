# Chapter 6 — What Employers Owe Employees

*On the distance between what the law extracts and what a firm actually owes.*

---

Sharon Dunn worked at Ford's Chicago-area assembly plant for more than a decade. In the late 1990s, she was part of a group of women — Black women, mostly — who reported what was happening on the assembly line: being groped, having sex acts simulated in their presence, men masturbating where they could be seen, racial slurs woven through the daily work. The EEOC investigated. Ford settled for roughly $22 million. The settlement included policy commitments, consent decrees, the whole apparatus of formal remedy.

By 2014, when a *New York Times* reporter sat down with Dunn to talk about a *second* settlement Ford had just reached — the same plant, substantially the same allegations, about $10 million this time — she said something the chapter cannot easily set aside.

*For all the good that was supposed to come out of what happened to us, it seems like Ford did nothing. If I had that choice today, I wouldn't say a damn word.*

Two settlements. Twenty-five years apart. Thirty million dollars in aggregate. A federal anti-discrimination regime that has been in continuous operation since 1964. And one of the people whose complaints had built the legal apparatus recommending, in retrospect, that anyone in her position stay quiet.

That is the opening problem. The law produced money damages and public consent decrees and written corporate-policy commitments. None of it changed the operative culture of the assembly line enough that the woman whose suit had set the precedent would make the same choice twice. So the question this chapter answers is not *what does the law require*. The law is just the floor, and everyone can see it. The question is *what does the floor leave out* — and what is an employer actually obligated to provide in the space above it?

---

## The legal floor: four pillars and what they cover

Before you can reason about what sits above the floor, you need to know exactly where the floor is. Four federal statutes do most of the work, and a treatment that doesn't name them precisely isn't operating with the actual apparatus.

**The Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970** requires employers to provide a workplace free from recognized hazards. It created the agency of the same name, with inspection authority and the power to cite and fine. The reference case is the Imperial Sugar refinery explosion in Port Wentworth, Georgia, on February 7, 2008. Sugar dust — accumulated over years on the inside of the plant's processing equipment, documented in internal audits, remediation deferred for cost reasons — ignited at a single point and propagated through the building in seconds. Fourteen workers died. About forty more were injured, many with severe burns. The OSHA fine was $8.7 million. The company had known about the dust-explosion risk. The remediation — better ventilation, regular cleaning, dust-suppression systems — had been treated as optional.

**Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964** prohibits employment discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. It created the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, which investigates and litigates complaints. The EEOC also enforces the Equal Pay Act of 1963, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, and the Pregnancy Discrimination Act of 1978. The framework is layered and consequential: roughly two-thirds of EEOC complaints filed in 2017 alleged sexual or racial harassment.

**The Worker Adjustment and Retraining Notification Act of 1989** (WARN) requires employers with 100 or more employees to give 60 days' advance notice of mass layoffs or plant closures. Before the Act, only about 20 percent of workers facing displacement received any advance notice; only 7 percent received two months. The Act addresses a predictable failure mode: workers losing income before they have time to find alternative employment. It mandates the runway.

**The Electronic Communications Privacy Act of 1986** regulates the interception of communications, including in workplace settings. It has two large exceptions — the business-purpose exception and the consent exception — that effectively give employers wide discretion to monitor employees. More on this shortly.

Here is the thing to notice about the four pillars: they cover safety, discrimination, layoff notice, and privacy. No pillar covers wage adequacy, work-life balance, or operative culture. Those are not regulated floors. They are the space above the floor, and that space is where the interesting ethical questions live.

## What the law cannot reach: harassment and operative culture

The Ford case is, legally, a Title VII case. Title VII prohibits harassment in two forms. *Quid pro quo* harassment — conditioning employment benefits on sexual favors — has been illegal since the Supreme Court's 1986 decision in *Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson*. *Hostile work environment* harassment — conduct frequent or severe enough to alter the conditions of employment — has been illegal since the same decision. The legal framework is settled. Has been for forty years.

The operative culture at the Ford Chicago plant was not.

Roughly thirty thousand harassment complaints are filed with the EEOC every year. Survey research suggests that for every formal complaint there are several incidents that never become formal complaints — because the worker fears retaliation, doesn't believe the system will produce remedy, or has watched colleagues file and be retaliated against.

The firm whose harassment policy is *we comply with Title VII* is operating at the floor. The firm whose *operative culture* makes harassment unthinkable — through hiring, through promotion criteria, through prompt and credible response to first reports, through the visible accountability of senior leadership when reports are made — is operating above the floor. The Ford case is a case where the legal regime produced two large settlements and the operative culture, by Sharon Dunn's direct account, did not change.

The post-2017 wave of public revelations makes the same point from the other direction. Harvey Weinstein, John Lasseter, Matt Lauer, Charlie Rose — these were firms and industries that had been operating at the legal floor and hoping the floor was the ceiling. When public expectation shifted faster than the legal regime, the firms that had been operating above the floor were better positioned. The ones that had invested only in compliance faced the bills of the operative culture they had never bothered to build.

This is the structure of compliance failures generally: compliance is auditable. Operative culture is not. Written policies, mandatory training modules, and certified records produce an audit trail that protects the firm in litigation. They do not produce the culture. The Ford case is the operative-culture failure inside a successful legal-compliance facade.

There is a misconception worth naming directly: that training solves the problem. The industry-level data on harassment training shows little reduction in incident rates and small increases in formal complaint rates — which is all training can produce. More reports, with the underlying behavior unchanged. Training is part of the solution. The rest of the solution is compensation, promotion, and accountability structures that make the operative culture actually move. The firms that understood this before being forced to are the ones that didn't spend 2017 in emergency board meetings.

## What the law doesn't set: wages and four gaps

In April of 2015, Dan Price — the thirty-year-old founder and CEO of Gravity Payments, a Seattle credit-card-processing company — announced that the firm's minimum salary would be raised to $70,000 per year, phased in over three years, and that he would cut his own salary from approximately one million dollars to the same $70,000 to fund part of the increase. An employee had told him directly that $35,000 was insufficient to live on in Seattle. Price did the math. The math required the change.

The aftermath was contested. Some predicted Gravity Payments would fail. Some predicted resentment and exits. What actually happened, by the published accounts through 2017, was that the firm received approximately five thousand job applications in the week following the announcement, retained more than ninety percent of its workforce, and grew its revenue substantially as the story became national news. Two related experiments followed: Pharmalogics Recruiting raised its starting base pay 33 percent and grew from $6.7 million in revenue and 46 employees to $15 million and 72 employees within a year. Tower Paddleboards cut its workday from ten hours to five with full salary preservation and reported productivity gains.

The wave was small. Most U.S. firms in 2015 did not raise their salary floors. To understand why the experiments matter, you need the four gaps.

**The minimum-wage gap.** The U.S. federal minimum wage has been $7.25 per hour since July 2009. Adjusted for inflation, the federal minimum's purchasing power has declined about 23 percent since its 1968 peak. The floor has not been adjusted in real terms in a generation.

**The productivity-pay gap.** Between 1973 and 2016, U.S. productivity — output per hour worked — rose by approximately 74 percent, according to Economic Policy Institute calculations using Bureau of Labor Statistics data. Median worker compensation over the same forty-three years rose by approximately 12 percent in real terms. Productivity grew about six times faster than worker pay. The gains from productivity growth that, in the post-1945 era, were broadly shared between firms and workers have, since the early 1970s, accrued largely to capital.

**The CEO-to-worker pay ratio.** Between 1978 and 2014, inflation-adjusted CEO compensation at large U.S. firms rose by approximately 1,000 percent. Worker pay over the same period rose 11 percent. The ratio of CEO pay to median-worker pay, roughly 20-to-1 in 1965, reached approximately 300-to-1 by 2014. The United Kingdom operates at roughly 22-to-1, France at 15-to-1, Germany at 12-to-1 — without observable detriment to those economies' competitiveness.

**The gender pay gap.** Women in the United States earned approximately 60 percent of men's pay in 1960. By 2016, the figure was approximately 80 percent. The trajectory of closure has slowed since the 1990s; current projections suggest pay equity will not be reached until somewhere between 2059 and 2119 if the recent rate continues. A $5,000 starting-salary gap in early career magnifies, at typical 5 percent annual raises, to more than $600,000 over a 40-year career.

All four gaps tell related parts of the same story: the post-1973 decoupling of worker compensation from productivity gains. The firms running compensation experiments — Gravity Payments, Costco, which has paid above its retail-sector wage benchmark for decades — have, on the available data, performed at or above their sector-comparable competitors. The mechanism is partly composition (higher-quality applicants, longer retention), partly culture (workers who understand the rationale for the firm's existence), partly demand (consumers in many segments increasingly prefer firms with visible labor commitments). Pay above the legal minimum is more expensive in the present quarter and less expensive in the long run. The firm whose horizon is the next quarter is optimizing the wrong horizon.

Iceland's *Equal Pay Standard*, which took effect on January 1, 2018, is the boldest legal experiment in this area. Employers with 25 or more employees must certify, every three years, that they pay men and women equally for the same work. Compliance is verified by independent audit; non-compliance produces escalating fines. The U.S. federal regime — Equal Pay Act, Title VII, the Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act of 2009 — produces enforcement through individual complaints. The Icelandic regime produces enforcement through certification. The two differ in burden of proof, administrative cost, and pace of effect. The Icelandic regime is faster. It is also more administratively intensive. The contemporary U.S. policy debate about whether to move toward certification has not resolved.

## What the law permits but ethics constrains: privacy, voice, due process

In 2010, the U.S. Supreme Court decided *Ontario v. Quon*, a case about a California police sergeant whose text messages on a department-issued pager were reviewed by a new chief after a change in administration. The messages included several hundred personal texts and several sexually explicit ones. The department's written policy had been *no expectation of privacy on department-issued equipment*; the operative practice had been more permissive, with supervisors collecting overage fees without otherwise reviewing content. Quon sued, alleging a Fourth Amendment violation.

The Court ruled 9-0 for Ontario. Justice Kennedy's opinion: *Because the search was motivated by a legitimate work-related purpose and because it was not excessive in scope, the search was reasonable.* The decision applied to government employers and specific facts, but it became the reference point for the broader question of how much workplace privacy employees can expect.

The answer, on present U.S. law, is not very much. American Management Association surveys in the late 2010s found approximately 48 percent of employers using some form of video monitoring, 67 percent monitoring employee internet use, and 30 percent reporting that monitoring had directly led to terminations. The infrastructure is in place. The legal regime permits it. The ethical question is what the firm should do *given that it can*.

Three claims constitute the employee's interest in autonomy, and they are worth distinguishing.

**Privacy.** The interest in not being monitored beyond what is necessary for the firm's legitimate operations. The interest is bounded — no employee credibly claims privacy in their work outputs, in firm-issued equipment used for personal purposes, or in conduct affecting safety or security. The interest is also substantive: constant surveillance of employees in daily work, beyond what is necessary, produces measurable increases in workplace stress, decreases in trust, and in many studies decreases in productivity. The monitoring that was supposed to improve performance tends to impair it.

**Voice.** The interest in having input into the conditions of one's work. The U.S. has the lowest unionization rate of any major OECD economy: about 10 percent of workers in 2016, against 26 percent in Germany, 29 percent in the United Kingdom, 35 percent in Italy, 82 percent in Sweden. The private-sector rate was about 6.5 percent — an all-time low. The decline has been continuous since the early 1980s, accompanied by the productivity-pay gap that opened during exactly the same period. Correlation is not causation. But the timing is worth noticing.

German *Mitbestimmung* — codetermination — has, since the postwar period, given workers seats on supervisory boards of large firms. It is credited by analysts with the German economy's 12-to-1 CEO-to-worker pay ratio and with the durability of skilled-trades employment in German manufacturing. The U.S. has no comparable framework. The business case against importing it is weaker than the conventional U.S. discussion suggests.

**Due process.** The interest in being fairly treated when adverse employment decisions are made. The U.S. doctrine of employment at will — under which approximately 85 percent of private-sector workers operate, meaning either party can dissolve the employment relationship at any time without cause — substantially limits due-process protections in practice. Germany, France, Italy, the United Kingdom, and Canada all operate with substantially stronger employee protections against dismissal. Their economies are not worse for it.

*Figure 6.3 — Three-column layout, one column per autonomy claim *

The *Quon* test is a useful frame even in purely private-sector settings: is the monitoring *motivated by a legitimate work-related purpose, and is it not excessive in scope*? The first prong asks why the monitoring is happening. The second asks how broad it is. A specific investigation of a specific employee who has triggered a specific complaint passes the test. Keystroke logging and screen capture deployed across an entire workforce fails it — the legitimate purposes (catching shirking, securing IP) do not require comprehensive surveillance to achieve. The available longitudinal evidence on comprehensive surveillance — workplace culture surveys, retention data, absenteeism rates — supports the view that the cumulative costs outweigh the gains for most firms in most settings.

## Putting it together

A mid-sized technology firm — call it Northstar — has just been acquired by a private-equity sponsor with a stated three-to-five-year operating horizon. The proposal: a 15 percent headcount reduction; comprehensive monitoring software (keystroke logging, screen capture, application telemetry) for "productivity optimization"; a two-year freeze on cost-of-living wage adjustments; elimination of the firm's existing pay-transparency disclosures because they create "unnecessary internal friction"; and a renewed commitment, in the revised employee handbook, to *full compliance with all applicable employment laws*.

The proposal complies with the WARN Act — the layoffs are 60 days out and within the threshold. It complies with Title VII non-discrimination requirements. It complies with OSHA safety requirements. It is legal.

On the chapter's framework, it fails on every concept.

The two-year wage freeze, against ongoing inflation, is a real wage decrease of roughly 5 to 7 percent over two years. The elimination of pay-transparency disclosure removes the mechanism by which the firm's gender and racial pay gaps could be detected and addressed; the change predictably enlarges those gaps. The monitoring software fails the *Quon* test on the *not excessive in scope* prong — the firm's legitimate interests do not require continuous surveillance of every employee. And a workforce told it will be comprehensively monitored is a workforce whose retention rates, voluntary engagement, and informal cooperation will deteriorate in measurable ways.

The firm's most-talented employees — the ones with options elsewhere — will leave first. The firm's productivity will decline rather than improve. The private-equity sponsor's three-to-five-year horizon may be short enough that the costs don't appear within the holding period; they will appear at the point of the next sale, in the form of weakened business fundamentals and lower exit multiples.

This is the pattern Chapter 1 named: short-horizon-rational, long-horizon-irrational. The proposal treats legal compliance as the ceiling of obligation rather than the floor. It treats workers as a cost to be minimized rather than as the operating machinery through whom the business physically happens. It will, on the available evidence, perform exactly as the framework predicts.

Sharon Dunn's testimony is still the clearest statement of what the floor fails to provide. The legal regime produced money and consent decrees and written policy commitments. What it could not produce — what it cannot produce, because it is not designed to — is the operative culture in which people like her are treated as ends rather than as a line item. That is what an employer actually owes. And the firms that have figured this out are not doing it because it feels good. They are doing it because it works.

## Chapter summary

The legal floor of employer obligation — OSHA, Title VII and the EEOC framework, WARN, ECPA — covers safety, discrimination, layoff notice, and privacy. It does not cover wage adequacy, work-life balance, or operative culture. Those are the space above the floor, and that space is where an employer's ethical obligations live.

Operative culture is not produced by written policy. It is produced by compensation, promotion, accountability, and the visible behavior of senior leadership. The Ford case is the standing rebuttal to anyone who confuses the two.

Four pay gaps — minimum wage, productivity-pay, CEO-to-worker, gender — tell the same story: the post-1973 decoupling of worker compensation from productivity gains. Compensation experiments that move above the legal minimum have, on the available evidence, produced better firm performance. Pay above the floor is more expensive in the short run and less expensive in the long run.

The employee's interests in privacy, voice, and due process are real claims, not sentimental ones. Monitoring beyond what legitimate business purposes require fails the *Quon* test. Unionization and codetermination structures that give workers input into the conditions of their work correlate with higher productivity and lower pay inequality in the economies that have them.

The single idea that matters most: the legal regime is the floor, not the ceiling. The firms that operate at the floor are the firms whose long-run performance is, on the available evidence, weakest. The firms that operate above it — with operative culture, with compensation that shares productivity gains, with monitoring that stays proportional — are the ones with the strongest workforces and the most durable returns. The argument is not that ethics costs profit. The argument is that ethics, on the right time horizon, *is* profit.
