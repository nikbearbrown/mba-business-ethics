# What Employees Owe Employers

In August of 2001, an Enron officer named Sherron Watkins finished reading a set of internal accounting documents and understood, for the first time clearly, that the company she worked for was hiding several billion dollars in losses inside off-balance-sheet entities her own department had helped construct. Watkins composed a memo addressed to Kenneth Lay, Enron's chairman and founder, identifying the accounting irregularities by name, naming the people responsible, and predicting — accurately — that the company would, when the irregularities became public, "implode in a wave of accounting scandals." She delivered the memo. Lay convened a meeting. The meeting produced an investigation conducted by the same law firm that had structured the questioned transactions, which produced a report concluding that the transactions were defensible.

By December 2001, Enron had filed for bankruptcy. Approximately sixty billion dollars of shareholder value evaporated. Tens of thousands of employees lost their jobs, their retirement savings, or both. The chief financial officer who had structured the off-balance-sheet entities, Andrew Fastow, eventually pleaded guilty and served prison time. Lay was convicted of conspiracy and securities fraud and died of a heart attack in July 2006 before sentencing. Jeffrey Skilling, the former CEO who had resigned two weeks after Watkins discovered the fraud — *the captain had just taken a helicopter home*, as Watkins later put it — served twelve years in federal prison. Watkins testified before Congress, gave press interviews, and became one of the most-cited examples of the modern American whistleblower.

She also said, in subsequent interviews, that she would never again hold a job in corporate America. *The minute you speak truth to power and you're not heard*, she said, *your career is never the same again.*

The chapter you are about to read is about what employees owe their employers — the symmetric question to the previous chapter's. It is also about what happens when the duty of loyalty and the duty to a wider audience pull in opposite directions. The Watkins case is the cleanest illustration of why the chapter is harder than its symmetric framing suggests. Loyalty is a real obligation. So is honesty. So is the obligation owed to shareholders and the public when an employee learns of an active fraud. When the obligations conflict, the employee has to choose. The chapter is about how to choose well.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Define** the common-law duty of loyalty and **identify** its modern erosion through at-will employment, defined-contribution retirement plans, and worker mobility.
- **Distinguish** non-compete, nondisclosure, nonsolicitation, and nondisparagement clauses, and **evaluate** their enforceability under specific state regimes.
- **Apply** the brand-and-customer duty to a contemporary social-media-era case and **explain** why employee-side conduct now operates as part of the firm's external face.
- **Recognize** insider trading, bribery, and Foreign Corrupt Practices Act violations and **identify** the specific legal frameworks (Section 10(b)5 of the 1934 Act; FCPA 1977/1988/1998) that govern them.
- **Analyze** a whistleblowing decision using the False Claims Act framework, the available evidence threshold, and the documented career consequences for whistleblowers from Watkins through Birkenfeld.

## Prerequisites

Chapter 6's framework on what employers owe employees. Chapter 1's three lenses. The willingness to take seriously that the symmetric question — *what do employees owe employers* — does not have the same shape from the other side of the table.

## Why this chapter matters

Most readers of this book are or will soon be employees. The chapter is about the daily ethical demands of that role — the demands that begin the moment you sign your offer letter and continue through every decision in which the firm's interest and some other interest pull in different directions. The chapter is also about the rare moments when those decisions become genuinely consequential. Most working lives never produce a Watkins-style choice. A small number of working lives turn on one. The reader should know what the choice looks like before encountering it.

## Concept 1: The duty of loyalty and its modern erosion

The common-law duty of loyalty in employment is, in all fifty U.S. states, derived from the legal doctrine of agency. The employee is an *agent* of the employer; the employer is the *principal*. The agent owes the principal a duty of loyal and faithful service rendered in good faith. The duty is structurally similar to the duty a fiduciary owes a beneficiary, though typically narrower in scope. Concretely, the duty requires the employee to refrain from acting contrary to the employer's interest during the term of employment — not to compete on the side, not to take bribes, not to leak confidential information, not to use the employer's authority for personal gain.

The doctrine is settled. Its operation, in modern American employment, is contested. The contestation is the substance of this concept.

### The mechanism: loyalty in two halves and the structural shifts of the last forty years

The Wharton management scholar Matthew Bidwell described the modern employee's loyalty as having *two halves*. The first half is the part the agency doctrine names — having the employer's interest at heart while at work, performing the duties of the role honestly, not damaging the firm. The second half — *staying with the same employer rather than moving on* — is not in the doctrine. It is the cultural practice of long-tenure employment that Americans associated with the post-1945 corporate landscape: thirty-year careers at IBM, GE, Ford. The first half remains a legal obligation. The second half has substantially eroded.

Three structural shifts produced the erosion.

**At-will employment.** Approximately eighty-five percent of U.S. private-sector workers operate under the doctrine of *employment at will*, under which the employer can terminate the employment relationship at any time without cause. The doctrine signals to the employee that the firm's commitment is itself short-term and conditional. Employees, unsurprisingly, respond in kind.

**The shift from defined-benefit to defined-contribution retirement.** From the 1940s through the 1970s, most large U.S. employers offered defined-benefit pension plans — guaranteed lifetime income calculated as a function of years of service and final salary. The plans rewarded long tenure mechanically. Beginning in the 1980s, U.S. employers began the shift to defined-contribution plans (401(k) for private employers, 403(b) for nonprofits and certain public employers), in which the employee's retirement assets travel with the employee across jobs. The shift was not, primarily, an attack on employee retirement security; it was a transfer of investment risk from employer to employee. The side effect was the elimination of the financial reward for long tenure.

**Worker mobility as norm.** Recent surveys — including a *PayScale* compensation report and analyses by *Glassdoor* — document that workers under thirty-five expect to change jobs frequently; one Forbes HR Council survey found ninety-one percent of millennial workers do not expect to stay with any employer more than three years. Job-changers earn measurably higher salary increases (Glassdoor data showed roughly five percent higher annual increases for movers vs. stayers); a ten percent pay increase was found to reduce voluntary turnover. The data do not say workers are disloyal in the legal sense. They say workers operate inside a labor market in which mobility is the rational strategy.

The combined effect is that the *first half* of Bidwell's loyalty — the legal duty — remains. The *second half* — the cultural expectation of long tenure — has substantially eroded, as a result of structural changes the employer initiated. The doctrine of loyalty is unchanged. The texture of the relationship inside which the doctrine operates is different.

### Restrictive covenants: non-compete, NDA, nonsolicitation, nondisparagement

The legal apparatus for protecting employer interests during and after employment relies on four standard contractual clauses. Each is enforceable under specific conditions, and the conditions vary by state.

**Non-compete agreements** prohibit the employee, during a specified time after departure, from competing with the firm in a specified geographic area or specified market. Enforceability is uneven. About one in five U.S. private-sector workers is covered by a non-compete agreement; about one in six earning under forty thousand dollars per year is, including in industries where the trade-secret rationale for non-competes is weak. The 2016 Jimmy John's case — in which a sandwich-shop chain operating about twenty-five hundred franchises across forty-six states required its hourly workers to sign two-year prohibitions on working at any other sandwich shop within two miles of any Jimmy John's — became the standing example of non-compete overreach. The New York Attorney General Eric Schneiderman and the Illinois Attorney General Lisa Madigan separately challenged the practice. California, in 2017 legislation, voided most post-employment non-competes outright. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission, in subsequent years, attempted a national rule against non-competes; the rule was struck down in court in the early 2020s, leaving the question to state legislation.

**Nondisclosure agreements** prohibit the employee from disclosing the firm's confidential information — trade secrets, customer lists, business plans, technical specifications. Trade secrets are protected by *secrecy itself*, not by federal intellectual-property law; the firm's protection survives only as long as the secret is kept. NDAs are widely enforceable.

**Nonsolicitation clauses** prohibit a departing employee from luring the firm's customers or colleagues to a new venture. Enforceability is generally stronger than for non-competes because the harm being prevented is more specific and the burden on the departing employee less broad.

**Nondisparagement clauses** prohibit the departing employee from publicly criticizing the firm. Enforceability is contested. Some jurisdictions have begun to recognize that nondisparagement clauses can conflict with whistleblower-protection statutes, with public-interest disclosure obligations, and with the basic First Amendment-adjacent interest in honest commentary about workplace experience.

### Worked example: the patent assignment clause

A typical employment agreement contains a *patent assignment clause* — a provision that any invention the employee creates during the term of employment, within the scope of the employment, is owned by the firm rather than the employee. The doctrine extends to copyrightable works under the *works-for-hire* doctrine. The duty of loyalty is the underlying legal basis: the employee is using the firm's resources, time, and context to develop the invention; the firm is, in the legal frame, the agent's principal in respect of the work.

The doctrine is settled but its operation produces hard cases. An engineer who works on a project at the firm during business hours and then, on weekends and evenings, develops a related invention from home using personal equipment — does the firm own the weekend work? The answer depends on the specific scope of the assignment clause, the factual record of the work's origin, and the state in which the dispute arises. California, by statute, limits patent assignments to inventions developed *in the scope of employment using employer resources*; outside that scope, the invention belongs to the employee. Several other states have similar statutes. In states without such statutes, the contract language controls, and the contract language typically favors the employer.

The chapter's framework: the employee should know, before signing, what the assignment clause covers. The conditions of the clause are negotiable in many cases; once the clause is signed, the conditions are not.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that loyalty is a one-way duty. It is not, in any non-cynical reading. The agency doctrine creates obligations on both sides; the employer's obligations to the agent (Chapter 6's substance) are part of the same legal structure.

The second is that mobility is disloyalty. It is not. The legal duty of loyalty governs conduct *during* the employment relationship and a defined post-employment period. Departure for a better opportunity, after honoring those obligations, is the employee's contractual right.

The third is that non-competes are universally enforceable. They are not, and the jurisdictional variation is substantial. The employee should know the state-specific regime before signing and before considering departure.

## Concept 2: Daily conduct — brand, customers, coworkers, codes of conduct

In April 2009, two employees at a Domino's Pizza franchise in North Carolina recorded a video of themselves spitting on pizza ingredients, picking their noses over food, and inserting cheese into their nostrils before placing it on a sandwich. The employees uploaded the video to YouTube. By the end of the day, the video had been viewed approximately one million times. By the end of the week, it had been viewed many millions of times. The food in the video was, on the franchise's later confirmation, never delivered to a customer. The employees were fired. Two were charged with felonies and Domino's filed civil suits against them.

The financial damage to Domino's brand was much larger than the cost of the firings. The company's stock price declined; its public reputation took a year to substantially recover; the parent company introduced a multi-million-dollar response that included rebuilding its product line and rebranding around the slogan *Oh yes we did*, an open admission that the prior product had been deficient. The rebranding worked. The damage from one prank by two employees, however, was visible on the company's financials for two full quarters.

The Domino's case is a clean illustration of a fact about modern employment that the legal duty of loyalty did not fully anticipate. *Every employee is, in the social-media era, a possible spokesperson for the firm.* What employees do during work hours, what they post outside work hours, what they say on a podcast or in a tweet — all of it can become, with no warning, a public statement attributed to the firm. The duty of loyalty has expanded, in operational practice, to include a duty of brand-consistent conduct that extends past the firm's gates.

### The mechanism: brand, internal marketing, the employee as ambassador

Several specific concepts anchor the daily-conduct concept.

**Brand** is the cluster of associations a customer holds about a product, service, or firm — the felt sense of what the firm stands for. Branding is the work of creating, maintaining, and modifying that cluster of associations. Most large firms invest substantially in branding. Few firms invest comparably in the *internal* version of the same work — the project of getting their own employees to believe in, and behave consistently with, the brand. The McDonald's executive Colin Mitchell, writing in *Harvard Business Review* in 2002 in a piece titled *Selling the Brand Inside*, made the case that the most effective advertising a firm runs is, in many cases, the daily behavior of its frontline employees. The argument has held up.

**Internal marketing** is the discipline of communicating the firm's brand promise to its employees with the same care it communicates the same promise to its customers. Done well, internal marketing produces a workforce whose daily decisions align with the brand without continuous supervision. Done badly, it produces the gap between the firm's stated values and the operative culture that Chapter 6 identified as the structural risk.

**The employee as ambassador** is the simple but consequential observation that, in social-media-era operations, the firm's customer-facing reputation is mediated by the conduct of every employee who interacts with a customer or who posts about the firm. The Cisco social-media branding program — encouraging employee creative posts about working at the firm and using employee voices as part of the recruiting message — is one positive example. The Domino's case is the negative example. Both show the same underlying mechanism.

### The trade-off: the ambassador role is unpaid extra labor

The expansion of the loyalty duty into brand-ambassadorship introduces a real trade-off. The firm gets more of the employee's social presence as part of its operational footprint. The employee is asked to absorb behavior constraints that used to apply only at the workplace. The trade-off is in the employee's favor when the firm has an authentic culture worth being an ambassador for. The trade-off is against the employee when the firm is asking for ambassadorship in exchange for terms (compensation, dignity, voice) the firm has not earned.

The chapter's framework: the firm whose employees are willing brand ambassadors has typically earned the ambassadorship by treating employees the way Chapter 6 specified. The firm that has not, but expects ambassadorship anyway, is asking for unpaid additional labor and will receive disgruntled compliance at best.

### Codes of conduct: the enumerated specifics

Most firms of meaningful size publish an explicit *code of conduct* — a written enumeration of the daily behavioral expectations the firm imposes on employees. The Exxon Mobil Code of Conduct is widely cited as a representative example. A typical code covers: compliance with applicable laws (anti-bribery, fraud, securities, environmental, safety, employment); conflicts of interest (disclosure of personal or financial interests); company property (care, protection, no personal misuse); cybersecurity and digital devices (no offensive or pirated material; protection of confidential information); social-media policy (the rules for personal-account behavior that names the firm); workplace respect (prohibition of harassment, sexual misconduct, discrimination).

Codes of conduct are enforceable through internal disciplinary processes, including dismissal. They are also, in many cases, references for the firm's defense in litigation — the firm that can produce a violated code-of-conduct provision against an accused employee is in a stronger litigation position than the firm that cannot.

### Workplace violence as a coworker duty

The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health classifies workplace violence into four types. *Type 1*: traditional criminal intent (robbery of a workplace). *Type 2*: customer or client violence against employees (about one-fifth of U.S. workplace homicides). *Type 3*: worker-on-worker violence (about fifteen percent of U.S. workplace homicides). *Type 4*: personal-relationship violence that follows the victim into the workplace (about ten percent, with women disproportionately affected).

Type 3 — the worker-on-worker form — is the type the duty of conduct most directly addresses. The structural prevention is in part screening (background checks, reference verification), in part culture (a workplace in which warning signs are reported and addressed rather than ignored), and in part response (security, evacuation procedures, post-incident support). The 2018 YouTube headquarters shooting, the recurring U.S. workplace shootings of the past two decades, and the global pattern of similar incidents make the question more than abstract. The employee duty includes the duty to report warning signs and to participate in the firm's prevention apparatus.

## Concept 3: The breaking point — financial temptations, whistleblowing, and the duty owed beyond the firm

In April 1986, Ivan Boesky — a forty-nine-year-old arbitrageur whose Wall Street firm had become one of the most successful merger-arbitrage operations in the country — was approached by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission with evidence that he had been buying stocks in advance of merger announcements based on tipped non-public information. The tipper was Dennis Levine, an investment banker at Drexel Burnham Lambert. Levine had been routing payments to Boesky through a Swiss bank account in exchange for the tips. Levine's Swiss account had been discovered through a routine audit of a foreign bank.

Boesky settled with the SEC. The settlement was conditional on Boesky cooperating with the ongoing investigation, which by 1988 had reached the head of Drexel Burnham Lambert's high-yield-bond department, Michael Milken. Milken — who had, at his peak, earned approximately five hundred and fifty million dollars in a single year — was indicted on ninety-eight counts including securities fraud, mail fraud, and racketeering. He pleaded guilty to six felonies in April 1990. His total monetary penalties exceeded $1.1 billion (approximately $200 million in government fines, $400 million in restitution to injured investors, and $500 million to Drexel Burnham Lambert clients). He served twenty-two months of a ten-year sentence and was banned for life from the securities industry. Drexel Burnham Lambert, weakened by the prosecutions and a related civil RICO case, filed for bankruptcy in February 1990. The firm's collapse eliminated approximately five thousand jobs.

The Milken-Boesky case sits at the intersection of every concept in this chapter. The duty of loyalty was violated (the employees used non-public information for personal gain). The duty of confidentiality was violated (the non-public information was sold). The duty of brand-conduct was violated (the firm's reputation was destroyed). And the financial penalties were structured to make the violation, in expected-value terms, irrational — the pre-tax gains were dwarfed by the eventual fines. The case is the clean version of the breaking point this concept is built around.

### The mechanism: insider trading, bribery, the FCPA, and the whistleblowing alternative

Three legal frameworks anchor the financial-ethics concept.

**Insider trading.** Section 10(b)5 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 prohibits the use of material non-public information in securities transactions. The Section is enforced by the SEC. *Insider* is defined broadly: officers, directors, employees, *temporary insiders* (bankers, brokers, attorneys, accountants, consultants who acquire non-public information through their professional roles), and the *tippees* of any of the above. The seminal case — *SEC v. Texas Gulf Sulphur Co.* (1968) — established that employees who knew of a major mineral discovery before public disclosure violated the law by trading on the information; the resulting forfeiture of profits set the modern enforcement template.

**Bribery and the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act.** The FCPA, enacted in 1977 and substantially amended in 1988 and 1998, prohibits U.S. persons and U.S.-listed firms from paying or offering anything of value to foreign government officials to obtain or retain business. The Act is enforced by both the SEC and the Department of Justice. Its jurisdictional reach is broad: U.S. citizens and residents, U.S.-incorporated firms, foreign firms whose stock is listed on U.S. exchanges, and any of the above acting through agents. The Act includes a narrow exception for *facilitating* (or *grease*) payments — small sums for routine non-discretionary government services like processing visas — and an affirmative defense for payments lawful under the foreign country's written law. Recent FCPA cases include the 2014 BHP Billiton settlement (Australian-American mining firm, twenty-five-million-dollar fine for hospitality and tickets at the 2008 Beijing Olympics), the GlaxoSmithKline 2014 case (twenty-five-million-dollar fine for similar activity), and the Telia 2017 settlement (Swedish telecom, $965 million for Uzbekistan bribery).

**Domestic bribery and gift policies.** State and federal law prohibit bribery of domestic government officials. Most large firms operate gift policies that specify maximum values and acceptable circumstances for gifts to and from business contacts. The U.S. federal government's gift-acceptance rules — gifts under $20 from any one source per occasion, under $50 in aggregate per source per year, with categorical exceptions for prohibited sources — are widely used as a benchmark for corporate gift policies. The point of an explicit policy is not to enable gift-giving; it is to make the line between acceptable hospitality and inappropriate exchange visible enough that employees can stay on the right side of it.

### The whistleblowing decision

The whistleblower's question is what to do when the employee has discovered, in the course of the employment, conduct by the employer that violates law, harms third parties, or both — and when the internal reporting channels have either failed or been compromised.

The legal regime is complex. Approximately twenty federal statutes contain whistleblower-protection provisions, including the Clean Air Act, the Water Pollution Control Act, the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (passed partly in response to the Enron and WorldCom failures), and the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010. Each statute applies to a specific industry or type of violation. There is no comprehensive federal whistleblower statute.

The most consequential single statute for whistleblower rewards is the *False Claims Act* of 1863, originally passed to address fraud against the Union government during the Civil War. The Act allows private individuals — *relators* — to file lawsuits on behalf of the government against parties who have submitted false claims for federal payment. Civil penalties range from $5,500 to $11,000 per claim, plus treble damages. The relator receives between fifteen and thirty percent of the recovery, depending on whether the government joins the case. The Act's *qui tam* provision has produced the largest whistleblower rewards on record. Bradley Birkenfeld received $104 million from the IRS in 2012 for his disclosures about UBS's Swiss tax-evasion services to U.S. clients. John Kopchinski and nine other former Pfizer employees shared a $102 million whistleblower award in 2009 for exposing illegal off-label drug promotion; Kopchinski's individual share was approximately $50 million. Multiple HCA employees split a $100 million combined award for Medicare fraud disclosures.

### The trade-off: whistleblower rewards are large; the career consequences are also large

The financial rewards available under the FCA and similar statutes are substantial. The career consequences are also substantial. Sherron Watkins's experience — public testimony, press interviews, and a subsequent career as an ethics speaker rather than a return to corporate finance — is representative rather than exceptional. The whistleblower research literature consistently shows that the modal post-disclosure career outcome involves substantial reduction in industry employability, regardless of whether the disclosed conduct was confirmed and regardless of whether the whistleblower received a financial award.

The trade-off the employee facing a possible whistleblower decision must weigh: the legal protections available, the evidence threshold required to be credible, the likelihood that the disclosure will produce remediation rather than retaliation, and the personal capacity to absorb the career consequences. The chapter's recommendation is the operational sequence the whistleblower research literature consistently endorses: internal reporting first (with documentation), followed by external reporting only after the internal channel has either failed or been compromised. The sequence is not about procedural courtesy; it is about producing the documentary record that the legal protections require.

### The Krawcheck case: the variant where the firm backed the whistleblower

Sallie Krawcheck — formerly the head of Smith Barney at Citigroup, then later the head of Bank of America's wealth-management division, and currently the CEO of *Ellevest*, a financial-services platform she co-founded for women clients — provides a useful counter-case. While leading Smith Barney, Krawcheck pushed Citigroup to reimburse retail clients who had been damaged by certain proprietary investment products. She was, by her own subsequent account, terminated for taking the position. She moved to Bank of America. There, she discovered that Bank of America's *Stable Value Fund* — a 401(k) investment product widely held by retail investors including a large concentration of Walmart employees — was structured in ways that placed retail savers at greater risk than the marketing materials disclosed. She advocated bailing out the fund at the firm's expense. The CEO at the time backed her decision. The fund was bailed out. Krawcheck was, several years later, *reorganized out* of Bank of America when a new CEO took over.

The Krawcheck variant differs from the Watkins variant in one crucial respect: at Bank of America, the *internal* report to the CEO produced the ethical action. The employee did not have to go external. The career consequences were nonetheless real (the eventual *reorganized out* end of her tenure), but they were delayed and partial. The variant illustrates that internal reporting can work when the internal channel is not itself compromised. It also illustrates that even successful internal reporting often produces career consequences in the long run, because the firm whose senior management acted on the report typically does not survive intact through the next leadership transition.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that whistleblowing is heroic. It often is, but the framing tends to elide the operational reality: most whistleblowing is slow, evidence-intensive, and career-damaging, and the financial rewards (when they appear at all) take years to arrive.

The second is that the First Amendment protects employee speech. It does not, in private workplaces. The First Amendment limits *government* action against speech. Private employers can, in most circumstances, discipline or terminate employees for speech outside the workplace, including social-media posts about the firm. Some specific protections exist — Section 7 of the National Labor Relations Act protects two-or-more employees discussing wages or working conditions, in person or online — but the protections are narrower than the lay reading of *free speech* suggests.

The third is that pay secrecy is enforceable. It is, in most U.S. states, no longer enforceable after the 2012 NLRB rulings holding most pay-secrecy policies in violation of NLRA Section 7, the 2014 Obama executive order banning federal-contractor pay-secrecy rules, and a wave of state legislation (California, Colorado, Illinois, Louisiana, Maine, Michigan, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Vermont) banning pay-secrecy outright. Employees have substantially more latitude to discuss compensation than the pre-2012 norm suggested.

## Integration: a single decision through the chapter's apparatus

A senior finance manager at a publicly-traded U.S. firm — call her Anna — discovers, through an internal audit she is supervising, that the firm has been misclassifying a significant fraction of its overseas operating expenses as research-and-development tax credits. The misclassification, on her preliminary count, has reduced the firm's effective tax bill by roughly $40 million over the past three years. The misclassification appears, on her review, to be deliberate rather than the result of error. The CFO who oversaw the classification is the person to whom Anna reports. Anna has no internal audit-committee channel that does not pass through the CFO. The firm's outside auditor has signed off on each year's tax positions. Anna has approximately three weeks before the firm's next annual report is due to be filed.

Run the chapter's apparatus.

*Concept 1 — duty of loyalty.* Anna owes the firm a duty of loyal and faithful service. The duty does not require her to facilitate continued tax fraud. The duty does require her, before any external disclosure, to give the firm an opportunity to correct the matter internally. Her first move is internal escalation — to the CFO directly, with documentation, in writing.

*Concept 2 — daily conduct.* The conduct expected of her, while pursuing the internal escalation, includes continued attention to her ordinary duties, no public discussion of the matter, no posting on social media, no conversation with colleagues outside the audit team. The brand and the firm's external face are, while the internal process plays out, hers to protect.

*Concept 3 — the breaking point.* If the CFO refuses to correct the matter, or directs Anna to suppress the audit finding, the breaking point arrives. The legal frameworks engage. The False Claims Act is potentially applicable (the misclassification produced reduced federal tax revenue, the central case for which the FCA was designed). The Sarbanes-Oxley Act provides specific anti-retaliation protections for accounting-related whistleblower disclosures by public-company employees. The IRS Whistleblower Office, through the *qui tam*-equivalent IRS whistleblower-reward program, offers awards of fifteen to thirty percent of recovered funds for credible disclosures of tax fraud over $2 million. Anna's disclosure of a $40 million misclassification, if confirmed, would qualify.

*The career consequences.* Anna's likely outcome, even on the most favorable scenario in which the disclosure produces remediation and a substantial financial reward, includes career disruption. The Watkins-Krawcheck-Birkenfeld pattern is consistent. Anna should know this before she chooses, and her choice should be made with the consequences understood rather than assumed away.

The integration verdict is the same one the chapter has been working toward. *The duty of loyalty is real. The duty owed beyond the firm — to investors, to the public, to the integrity of the tax system — is also real. When the two duties pull in opposite directions, the employee has to choose, and the choice carries personal cost regardless of which direction it goes.* The chapter has equipped Anna to make the choice with the legal frameworks, the evidence-threshold expectations, and the career-consequence landscape understood. The chapter has not made the choice easier. Some choices in working life are not supposed to be easy.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can articulate the common-law duty of loyalty as a continuing legal obligation while recognizing the structural erosion of long-tenure norms. You can distinguish the four standard restrictive covenants and evaluate their enforceability in specific jurisdictions. You can recognize how the brand-and-customer duty has expanded into social-media-era ambassadorship and assess specific cases on its terms. You can identify insider trading, bribery, and FCPA violations and produce defensible verdicts on the legal frameworks that govern each. You can analyze a whistleblowing decision using the False Claims Act framework, the available evidence threshold, and the documented career-consequence landscape from Watkins through Birkenfeld.

The single idea that matters most is that the duty of loyalty is real but not unlimited. It binds the employee to honest, faithful service to the firm. It does not bind the employee to participate in fraud, to suppress disclosure of harm to third parties, or to remain silent when the firm's conduct violates the law. The chapter's central argument is that the working-life situations in which loyalty and broader ethical obligation pull in opposite directions are rare, but when they arrive they are decisive. The employee should know the legal frameworks, the evidence thresholds, and the career consequences before the situation arrives, not after.

The common mistake to watch for is the assumption that the legal duty of loyalty is also a moral duty of silence. It is not. The duty of loyalty constrains the employee's conduct *during* the employment relationship; it does not extinguish the employee's standing as a moral agent with obligations beyond the firm. Watkins, Krawcheck, and Birkenfeld each acted on a duty owed beyond the firm. None violated their duty of loyalty in doing so. The distinction is the chapter's most important takeaway.

You should now be able to teach the four restrictive covenants, the SEC and FCPA frameworks, and the False Claims Act *qui tam* mechanism to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in twenty-five minutes, and to apply each to a current case the colleague brings you.
