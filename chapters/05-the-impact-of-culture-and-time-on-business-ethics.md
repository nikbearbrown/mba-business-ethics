# The Impact of Culture and Time on Business Ethics

In May of 1626, on the southern tip of an island that the local Lenape people called *Mannahatta* — *the place of many hills* — a Dutch West India Company representative named Pieter Minuit conducted what European history would record for the next four hundred years as the *purchase of Manhattan*. The price, in the version every American schoolchild has heard, was twenty-four dollars in beads and trinkets. The actual amount was sixty Dutch guilders, equivalent to perhaps a thousand U.S. dollars in modern purchasing power, paid in farming tools, muskets, gunpowder, kettles, axes, knives, and clothing. The transaction was witnessed and signed.

Both parties walked away believing they had a deal. Both parties were wrong about what the deal was.

The Dutch believed they had purchased outright ownership of the island — title in fee simple, the kind of ownership that European law had been refining for several hundred years. The Lenape believed they had granted a right of usage — fishing rights, hunting rights, perhaps shared access to certain lands during certain seasons. The category *outright permanent ownership of land* was not an idea their legal vocabulary contained. The category *temporary shared usage rights* was not an idea Pieter Minuit was tracking.

Within a generation, the misunderstanding produced violent conflict. Within a century, the Lenape had been displaced from their homeland and the Dutch West India Company had become the New York Stock Exchange.

This chapter is about why ethical business practice is not the same in May 1626 in lower Manhattan as it is in May 2026 in lower Manhattan. The differences are not merely cosmetic. They are structural. Two parties operating in good faith under different cultural assumptions about *what is being exchanged* can produce a transaction that is, by each party's lights, fully ethical — and that nonetheless harms one of the parties severely. The honest treatment of business ethics has to grapple with that asymmetry. Universal principles, when they exist, exist on top of it. They do not erase it.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** enculturation (the process by which a person is shaped by their birth culture) from acculturation (the cultural change that occurs when two cultures meet) and **identify** how each affects business ethics in cross-cultural transactions.
- **Trace** the major historical eras (mercantilist, industrial, postindustrial, information, globalized) and **explain** how each era produced its own characteristic ethical questions and responses.
- **Analyze** the role of geography, religion, and language in shaping the ethical expectations a firm walks into when operating in a new market.
- **Evaluate** the claim that there are universal business-ethics standards that survive cultural translation — and **identify** which claims survive and which do not.
- **Apply** the humanistic business model and **evaluate** its specific claims about the relationship between profitability and human flourishing.

## Prerequisites

Chapter 2's four traditions (especially Aristotelian and Confucian virtue ethics, whose cross-cultural parallel makes the universal-vs-local question concrete). Chapter 3's stakeholder map. Chapter 4's three special stakeholders. The willingness to take seriously that *what counts as right behavior* has changed within living memory and will change again.

## Why this chapter matters

Most contemporary business is cross-cultural in ways the chapter's author would not have predicted thirty years ago. A logistics manager in Dallas is coordinating with a contractor in Hanoi who is sourcing components from a supplier in Suzhou whose CFO trained in London. A customer-service representative in Manila handles complaints from customers in Berlin and Lima in the same hour. The premise that *business ethics is one thing, the same in every office on the planet* is a premise that fails in operation. The premise that *business ethics is whatever the local culture says it is* fails too, more dangerously, because it produces the kind of cultural-relativist evasion Chapter 1 already warned against. The chapter has to find the middle path. The middle path is the discipline of identifying what is universal, what is local, and how to operate honestly across the difference.

## Concept 1: How culture gets inside business — enculturation, acculturation, and the Manhattan problem

A child born in Tokyo in 2010 learns to bow at a specific angle, with specific timing, when greeting a person of specific seniority. The child does not learn this from a manual; she learns it by watching her grandmother greet her grandfather's business associate at a New Year's gathering in 2014. By the time she is twelve, the bowing is automatic — the muscle memory of a cultural practice that has been encoded in her body before she can name what she is doing. When she joins a Tokyo firm at twenty-two, the bowing is part of her professional vocabulary. It does not feel cultural to her. It feels like *what one does*.

The technical name for this process is *enculturation*. It is the process by which a person is shaped, mostly invisibly, by the culture they are born into — the language, the customs, the implicit hierarchies, the unstated rules about who speaks first and who waits, who initiates a transaction and who responds. By adulthood, the rules have been internalized so deeply that they feel like features of reality rather than features of one specific culture's way of operating.

The companion concept is *acculturation* — what happens when a person formed in one culture encounters and is changed by another. Acculturation is rarely symmetric. The smaller, less powerful, or more recently arrived culture is typically more changed by the encounter than the larger or longer-established one. The Lenape in 1626 were acculturated by the Dutch — they adopted European tools and traded materials and eventually European disease — far more than the Dutch were acculturated by the Lenape. The asymmetry is the structural feature of cross-cultural business that the universal-principle theorists most often miss.

### The mechanism: enculturation and acculturation as the substrate of business norms

Several specific mechanisms are worth tracking.

**Language carries values.** The aphorism *English is the language of money* is not entirely a joke. The dominance of English in international finance, technology, and corporate governance has carried with it specific cultural assumptions: about contracts (they are written, comprehensive, binding), about time (it is linear, scarce, optimizable), about persons (they are autonomous individuals with property rights), about disputes (they go to court). A firm whose executives all operate in English is operating, partly invisibly, in the cultural assumptions of English-speaking societies. The assumptions are not wrong. They are also not universal.

**Consumerism is one cultural product among many.** The lifestyle organized around the acquisition of goods and services — the cultural form that American economists in 1950 called the *consumer society* — is now globally dominant in measured terms but is not therefore *the natural form of human economic life*. Other cultural forms (collective consumption, religiously-mediated restraint, environmentally-mediated reduction) exist and have existed. The sense that consumerism is normal is itself a product of enculturation. A firm whose marketing assumes the consumer-society default is, often, exporting that default to markets where it will produce specific cultural and ecological consequences.

**Business beliefs are stated explicitly and operate implicitly.** IBM's stated beliefs — dedication to client success, innovation that matters, trust and personal responsibility — are an example of the explicit form. The beliefs are codified, displayed in lobbies, taught at orientation. The implicit form is what the firm actually does when those beliefs come under pressure. Merck & Co.'s founder George W. Merck, in a 1950 speech at the Medical College of Virginia, stated the implicit form sharply: *We try to remember that medicine is for the patient, not for the profits. The profits follow, and if we have remembered that, they have never failed to appear.* Whether the firm honored that commitment in subsequent decades is a question the firm's actual operations answer better than its stated beliefs do.

### The trade-off: cultural sensitivity is essential but can become evasion

The careful cross-cultural operator gives weight to the receiving culture's norms. The careless one imposes the home culture's norms as if they were universal. So far, so good. The trade-off is harder than that.

Cultural sensitivity, taken as far as some practitioners take it, becomes ethical relativism — *whatever is normal here is acceptable here*, with no ground for criticizing local practice from outside. The position is sometimes presented as humility; it is more accurately a refusal to evaluate. It is also, in practice, a way of explaining why a firm's local operations include practices the firm would never permit at headquarters: longer working hours, lower wages, weaker environmental standards, looser anti-corruption norms, fewer protections against discrimination. *That is just how things are done here* is a sentence that does specific work in such conversations, and the work is the displacement of moral responsibility from the firm to the receiving culture.

The trade-off the chapter is going to develop is between *thin universal standards* (which cross cultures and cannot be locally suspended) and *thick local norms* (which vary genuinely and where the firm should respect the variation). Honest cross-cultural practice operates inside both — applying the thin universals as non-negotiable and respecting the thick locals as legitimate variation. The discipline is in distinguishing one from the other. Concept 4 will return to this distinction.

### Worked example: the Petrobras Lava Jato investigation

Petróleo Brasileiro — Petrobras, the Brazilian state-controlled oil company — became, between 2003 and 2014, one of the largest corporate corruption cases in commercial history. The mechanism was simple and large-scale. Construction and engineering contractors competing for Petrobras contracts paid kickbacks to senior Petrobras executives, typically in the range of two to five percent of the contract value. The kickbacks funded executive lifestyles (luxury cars, jewelry, art, yachts) and political party operations (financing of the Workers Party, Partido dos Trabalhadores, including campaign expenditures of multiple federal officials). The total value of fraudulent contracts has been estimated, in subsequent prosecutorial findings, in the tens of billions of U.S. dollars.

The investigation began in 2014 — an accounting case at a small money-laundering operation at a gas station and car wash in Brasília that prosecutors codenamed *Lava Jato* (Operation Car Wash) — and expanded into one of the largest anti-corruption investigations in any country's history. By 2017, more than two hundred individuals had been convicted, including senators, federal officials, former president Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, and the country's largest construction firm Odebrecht. Lula's successor, Dilma Rousseff, was impeached in 2016, partly in connection with related investigations. The federal Supreme Court justice originally overseeing the investigation died in early 2017 in a small-plane crash whose circumstances remain contested.

Apply the chapter's apparatus to the case. *Was the corruption a local cultural feature that outsiders should not judge?* The defense was attempted by some commentators in Brazil and elsewhere. The case for the defense relied on the argument that *jeitinho* — the Brazilian cultural practice of finding informal workarounds — has long been a feature of business and political life. The case fails, on the chapter's framework, on three grounds. First, the practices in question were illegal under Brazilian law itself; they did not represent *the local norm*, they represented violations of it. Second, the harms were borne disproportionately by Brazilian citizens — through the inflated public-works costs, through the political instability the scandal produced, through the eventual displacement of the political coalition that had been the major beneficiary of the corruption — making the *outsiders should not judge* defense difficult to sustain. Third, the corrupt practices were universally rejected by every major ethical tradition Chapter 2 covered. *Jeitinho* as everyday social lubricant is one thing; multi-billion-dollar systematic kickbacks are another. Cultural sensitivity does not require equating them.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that *cultural sensitivity* and *ethical universalism* are opposed. They are not. The honest practitioner treats thin universal standards as binding and thick local norms as legitimate. The discipline is in the distinction.

The second is that the firm exporting its culture to a new market is *imposing*. Sometimes it is. More often the influence flows in both directions — the firm changes the market and is changed by it. The Manhattan case is the catastrophic version of asymmetric influence; most cross-cultural business is closer to symmetric, with both parties acculturating to one another over time.

The third is that *English as the language of money* is permanent. The pattern is more recent than the framing suggests, traceable to the post-1945 American economic dominance, with antecedents in nineteenth-century British commercial empire. There is no structural reason it must continue.

## Concept 2: How time shapes business ethics — the historical eras and the ethical questions they produced

A photograph from a New York office in 1957 shows a typical scene. Half the people in the room are smoking. The smoke fills the air to about chest height. There is an ashtray on every desk, several on the conference table. The pregnant woman at the back of the room, taking minutes, has a cigarette in her hand. None of this strikes anyone in the room as worth noting. It is what offices look like.

Sixty years later, most of those office workers are dead, several from lung cancer or related diseases. The ashtrays are gone. The pregnant woman is, in any contemporary U.S. office, smoking nowhere — and in many countries, smoking nowhere at all in any indoor public space. The norm has shifted so completely that the 1957 photograph reads as historical evidence of a different culture, not a recognizable office.

Tobacco's transformation from acceptable workplace presence to publicly contested industry took, by most accounts, about four decades — from the U.S. Surgeon General's January 1964 report on *Smoking and Health* to the 2006 federal racketeering judgment in *United States v. Philip Morris*. The shift was not a change in tobacco's biology. The shift was a change in what the surrounding society was willing to allow, regulate, or tolerate. Time changed business ethics around tobacco. It did not change tobacco.

The same kind of shift, on different timelines and around different industries, is the engine of the historical changes in business ethics this concept tracks.

### The mechanism: five eras and the ethical questions each produced

Business-ethics historians sometimes organize the long arc into five eras. Each era's characteristic ethical question reflects the technology, scale, and social structure of the period.

**The mercantilist era** (roughly 1500 to 1750). Trade is mediated by chartered companies — the Dutch West India Company, the British East India Company, the various Compagnies des Indes — operating under explicit royal grants of monopoly. The era's characteristic ethical question is the morality of the chartered monopoly itself. The era's defining transactions — the Manhattan transaction is one — typically involve cross-cultural exchanges in which the European parties and their indigenous counterparts had radically different assumptions about what was being exchanged.

**The industrial era** (roughly 1750 to 1920). Steam, coal, mechanized factories, the transformation of agrarian economies into urban-industrial ones. The era's characteristic ethical question is what employers owe workers under conditions of unprecedented production scale and unprecedented labor intensity. Charles Dickens (1812-1870) and Upton Sinclair (1878-1968) wrote the literary indictments. Ida Tarbell (1857-1944) wrote the journalistic ones, including the lacerating analysis of John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil that contained one of the era's most quoted lines: *Would you ask for scruples in an electric dynamo?*

**The postindustrial era** (roughly 1920 to 1970). Mass markets, mass consumption, the corporate form, the rise of advertising and public relations. Edward Bernays — the Austrian-American who is sometimes called the father of public relations — engineered, in 1929, a parade in which young women smoked Lucky Strike cigarettes as *torches of freedom*, marketing tobacco to women as an act of feminist liberation. The era's characteristic ethical question is what the firm owes the consumer when the firm has become large and information-asymmetric enough to shape the consumer's preferences before the consumer gets to choose.

**The information era** (roughly 1970 to 2010). The personal computer, the internet, the mobile phone, the rise of platforms whose primary product is attention. The era's characteristic ethical question is the management of personal data — the firm's access to information about its customers becoming, increasingly, more comprehensive than the customers' access to information about themselves. The Cambridge Analytica revelations in 2018 and the multiple Equifax-style breaches in the 2010s are the era's defining cases.

**The globalization era and what comes after** (2010 onward). The era's characteristic ethical question is shifting in real time. The early-2000s confidence that globalization would produce convergence has been complicated by the populist reactions of the 2010s — Brexit, the U.S. tariff wars, the partial withdrawal of supply chains from China, the resurgence of national-economic-sovereignty movements in multiple countries. The era's defining ethical question may turn out to be the management of obligations across jurisdictions whose legal-political alignment with one another can no longer be assumed.

[FIGURE: A horizontal timeline running from 1500 to 2030, with the five eras as labeled bands, each containing one defining case study (Manhattan 1626, Dickensian factory 1840, Bernays Lucky Strike 1929, Cambridge Analytica 2018, Brexit/tariff regime 2018-2025). The student should notice that ethical questions are *era-specific* — what was contested in one era was settled in the next, and what was unimagined in one era became central in the next.]

### The trade-off: ethical change is slow but inexorable

The change of business-ethics norms over time is slow on the scale of a single career and inexorable on the scale of a generation. The trade-off the firm faces is between staying ahead of the change (which costs in the present, before the change has materialized as regulatory or market pressure) and staying behind the change (which costs more in the future, when the change has materialized and the firm has to scramble to catch up).

Tobacco firms in the 1990s faced this trade-off acutely. The U.S. firms whose response to the changing public-health consensus was litigation and denial paid the largest costs. The international firms whose response was diversification and gradual exit from the U.S. cigarette market paid lower long-run costs. The shift took thirty years; the firms whose horizons were shorter than thirty years made decisions that the longer-horizon firms regretted having made.

The trade-off generalizes. Pharmaceutical firms in 2026 are now facing the equivalent question on opioids. Fossil-fuel firms are facing the equivalent question on climate. Social-media firms are facing the equivalent question on attention extraction. In each case, the trade-off is between defensive operation under the current regime (cheaper now, more expensive later) and proactive transformation toward the regime that is coming (more expensive now, cheaper later). The chapter's working argument is that the proactive option is, on the long-horizon evidence, almost always the cheaper one.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that ethical norms change because the underlying ethics changes. They typically do not. The underlying claims — that workers should be safe, that customers should not be defrauded, that pollution harms — were articulable in every era. What changes is what societies are willing to enforce.

The second is that the change is always toward stricter standards. It is not. Some changes go the other way (the relaxation of certain monopoly-pricing prohibitions in the 1980s, the rollback of certain financial-sector regulations in the 1990s and again in the 2010s). The direction of change is contestable in each era.

The third is that historical knowledge is decorative. It is not. The firm whose strategic horizon does not include the lessons of the previous historical era is operating, as Chapter 1 put it, on the wrong time scale.

## Concept 3: How geography, religion, and conscience shape standards — the search for the universal core

In a Tuareg encampment in the southern Sahara, before any business is discussed, three glasses of tea are served. The first is bitter. The second is sweet. The third is gentle. The Tuareg proverb that names the sequence — *the first cup is bitter like life, the second sweet like love, the third gentle like death* — is also the social engine of the conversation that follows. By the third glass, perhaps two hours into the meeting, the parties have established who they are, who their families are, where they are from, what they hope from the encounter. Only then does the substantive negotiation begin.

A New York or Tokyo or London businessperson, parachuted into the same encampment with a 9 AM-to-5 PM schedule and a deal to close by Friday, will find this practice incomprehensible at first and then, perhaps, frustrating. The frustration is the firm's enculturation showing. A culture that has organized its business around scarce, optimizable time will find a culture organized around relational, ceremonial time difficult to operate inside. The Tuareg, for their part, will find the time-pressed visitor's haste rude — and, more practically, untrustworthy. *A person who cannot sit through three glasses of tea is a person whose long-term reliability is not yet established.*

The chapter's third concept is about the ways geography, religion, and individual conscience interact to produce the specific ethical landscape of a place. The frameworks of the previous concepts — enculturation, the historical eras — operate inside the geography of where the business actually happens.

### The mechanism: time, space, contact, and stewardship

Several specific dimensions of cultural variation are worth tracking because they affect business ethics most directly.

**Time.** The variation in time-orientation across cultures is more than a stylistic preference. It is a different theory of what business is. The German *sofort* (at once) and the Italian *subito* (at once) — both nominally translating to immediate action — produce different actual response times in practice; the cultural calibration of *immediate* differs. The Kenyan *tutaonana baadaye* (see you later) leaves the *later* genuinely open. The U.S. *a New York minute* compresses urgency further. None of these is wrong. Each produces different transactional rhythms and different expectations of what counts as good faith.

**Space.** Cultures vary on physical proximity in ways that affect business interactions every day. *Contact cultures* — much of South America, the Middle East, parts of southern Europe — use closer physical proximity, more touch, more sustained eye contact. *Non-contact cultures* — much of northern Europe, the Far East, the United States — maintain more distance and less touch. A Nigerian business meeting in which the participants are seated too far apart will register as unfriendly. The same distance in a Helsinki meeting will register as appropriate.

**Religion.** Until the seventeenth century, in most of the world, religion and ethics were inseparable. The Enlightenment in Europe produced an attempted separation that has been incomplete and is still contested. Most contemporary business ethics in the West operates as if the separation is complete; in practice, the underlying ethical commitments often trace to specific religious traditions whose vocabulary has been secularized. The Protestant work ethic that Max Weber identified in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905) — frugality, hard work, the linking of material success to divine favor — remains operative in many U.S. corporate cultures even where the religious vocabulary has dropped away. John D. Rockefeller's lifelong Baptist faith and his conviction that *God had given him money for mankind's benefit* were not eccentric features of his career; they were structural to his understanding of the philanthropy that funded the University of Chicago, Rockefeller University, and the Rockefeller Foundation. Whether one accepts the theological premise or not, the cultural inheritance shapes the present.

**Stewardship.** Several major religious traditions converge on a concept the chapter will name as important. *Stewardship* — the idea that wealth and resources are held by their possessor on behalf of someone else, with a duty of care attached — appears in Jewish, Christian, Islamic, Hindu, Buddhist, and indigenous African and American religious frameworks, with different theological underpinnings and considerable overlap in practical content. The convergence is suggestive. It is one of the candidate items for Concept 4's universal core.

### The trade-off: cultural literacy is essential and costly

The cross-cultural firm that operates without cultural literacy makes specific avoidable mistakes — the rude 9 AM-to-5 PM behavior in the Tuareg encampment, the too-distant seating in Lagos, the dismissive response to a Pakistani team's Ramadan fast. The mistakes accumulate. Reputations form. Doors close.

The cross-cultural firm that operates with deep cultural literacy avoids those mistakes but pays the price of slower decision-making, longer relationship-formation, and the genuine difficulty of training executives whose careers will require operation across many cultures.

The trade-off is structural. The firm operating in many markets faces the choice of a thin, universal corporate culture (cheaper, more consistent, less culturally adaptive) or a thick, locally-adaptive corporate culture (more expensive, more culturally appropriate, harder to coordinate). Most large firms attempt some hybrid. The hybrid is hard. The firms that manage it well are typically distinguished by long-tenure executives who have lived in multiple cultures rather than by training programs.

### Worked example: Ramadan in Pakistan

Imagine an American auditor — call her Jillian — leading a team at the Islamabad branch of an investment bank during Ramadan. The bank's local employees are observing the fast: no food, water, or other consumables from sunrise to sunset. Jillian, on her first day, suggests a midmorning coffee break, is met with awkward silence, and realizes the cultural error. She is now uncertain about lunch.

Three options. *Option A:* eat lunch in her office, out of sight. *Option B:* leave the building and eat at a Western restaurant. *Option C:* fast with the team during the workday and break the fast at sunset together. Each option has costs.

*Option A* is the lowest-cost in immediate operational terms — Jillian's energy is preserved for the audit. The cultural cost is potential, not certain; the team may notice and read the choice as a refusal to share their experience.

*Option B* is similar in immediate terms but adds the visible signal of physical separation from the team. In Pakistani business culture, the signal is more visible than its analogue would be in U.S. culture.

*Option C* is the most costly to Jillian — fasting for a month while leading an audit is genuinely difficult — and is the option most aligned with cross-cultural respect. It also signals, more strongly than any verbal statement could, that Jillian takes the team's practices as serious enough to share. The signal compounds; in subsequent business with the same team, the relationship she has built by fasting alongside them is the asset her American competitors do not have.

There is no required answer. Any of the three is defensible depending on the specific context — Jillian's health, the audit's intensity, the team's specific expectations. The chapter's point is that the choice *is* a choice and that the firm whose executives know how to make it well are operating with an asset their less-culturally-literate competitors lack.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that religion is *outside* the firm's concern. In Western corporate cultures it is often treated that way. In most of the world it is not. A firm operating in India whose calendar ignores Diwali, in Saudi Arabia whose calendar ignores Ramadan, in Israel whose calendar ignores the High Holidays, is operating without a tool its competitors are using.

The second is that *cultural difference* is mostly about etiquette. It is not. The deeper differences are about time, space, the locus of moral responsibility (individual or family or community), and the boundary between religious and secular life. Etiquette is downstream of those.

The third is that the conscience of the individual practitioner is irrelevant in the cross-cultural setting because the local culture is the relevant standard. It is not. The individual's conscience — the residue of their own enculturation, modified by their encounter with the new culture — remains the operating instrument. Cultural literacy informs it. It does not replace it.

## Concept 4: The universal core and the local variation — what survives translation

The Manhattan case opens the chapter with a transaction that fails because the parties had different cultural assumptions about *what was being exchanged*. The Petrobras case shows a failure where local cultural defenses (*jeitinho*) were used to excuse practices that violated the local culture's own norms. The Ramadan case shows a context where cultural literacy and conscience must work together to produce the ethical action. These examples sit on a single continuum. The chapter's most useful synthesis is the distinction between the universal core that survives translation and the local variation that does not.

### What survives translation: the thin core

Across the major ethical traditions Chapter 2 covered — Aristotelian and Confucian virtue ethics, Benthamite-Millian utilitarianism, Kantian deontology, Rawlsian contractualism — and across the major religious traditions, a small number of ethical commitments recur with sufficient consistency that they constitute a *thin universal core*. The list is contested but its central items are not.

*Honesty.* Every major tradition prohibits the deliberate misrepresentation of fact in transactions. The form varies — the Confucian *zhōngxìn* (wholehearted sincerity), the Kantian duty of truth-telling, the Christian *thou shalt not bear false witness*, the Islamic *amana* (trustworthiness) — but the prohibition is constant.

*Non-coercion.* No tradition endorses the use of force or fraud to extract value from a non-consenting party. The Confucian relational ethics, the Kantian humanity formulation, the Rawlsian original position, and the major religious traditions converge here.

*Fair dealing.* The duty to honor commitments and to deliver what was agreed to, on the terms agreed to. *Pacta sunt servanda* (agreements are to be kept) is the Roman-law version; analogous principles exist in every commercial code.

*Stewardship.* The duty to manage entrusted resources with care, not as the steward's property. Recurs in every major religious tradition and in the secular fiduciary duty.

*Treating persons as ends.* The Kantian formulation is the sharpest statement, but the underlying claim — that human persons are not merely instruments of someone else's purpose — is present in every tradition that takes ethics seriously.

These five items survive translation. They are the floor below which no tradition allows commerce to operate.

### What varies legitimately: the thick local

The thick local norms vary across cultures without violating the thin core. *How* honesty is signaled (direct statement vs. indirect implication), *how* commitments are made (written contract vs. oral promise), *what* counts as appropriate gift-giving (Kenyan business courtesy vs. U.S. anti-corruption rules), *how* time is allocated in negotiation (Tuareg three-glass ceremony vs. New York minute), *what* religious practices are accommodated, *how* close to stand, *whether* to tuck in a shirt — these vary, and the variation is legitimate. The thick local does not contradict the thin core; it specifies how the thin core is operationalized in a particular cultural setting.

### What does not survive: the thin core with local pretense

The hardest cases are the ones where the firm operates in a way that violates the thin core and *defends the violation by appeal to local norms*. The Côte d'Ivoire toxic-waste case from Chapter 4 is the cleanest example. The argument *Ivorian disposal regulations permitted the offload* is, on inspection, an appeal to a regulatory gap rather than to a genuine local norm. No Ivorian community would, asked under the Rawlsian veil of ignorance, agree to a regime in which European industrial waste flowed onto their lagoon at three dollars per ton. The *local norm* in such cases is a fiction constructed by the firm to displace responsibility from itself to a culture that did not, in fact, sanction the practice.

The discipline of cross-cultural ethics is, in part, the discipline of refusing this displacement. *Local culture* is not a synonym for *whatever the firm can get away with*. The thin core constrains. The thick local does not provide cover.

### The humanistic business model — and its limits

The chapter ends on the philosophical question that the source material raises and that contemporary business education is still working out. *What is the relationship between the firm's profitability and the human flourishing of the people it employs and serves?*

The *humanistic business model* — articulated by figures including Richard T. DeGeorge of the University of Kansas and several thinkers at the Humanistic Management Center — proposes that the firm should be designed to produce sustainable human flourishing as well as profit, with the two seen as mutually reinforcing rather than opposed. The model points to operational features: investment in employee training, support for family responsibilities (parental leave, dependent care), accommodation of religious and cultural practices, attention to the community in which the firm operates.

The model has substantial empirical support. Firms that score high on humanistic measures often outperform comparators on retention, customer loyalty, and long-run financial returns. The model also has its critics. Aristotelian virtue ethicists note that *happy employees* and *flourishing employees* are not the same thing — flourishing requires the development of character and the exercise of practical wisdom over a life, which a single employer cannot guarantee even with generous benefits. The humanistic model risks treating the firm's instrumental support for employee well-being as if it were the firm's contribution to human flourishing in the deeper Aristotelian sense.

The honest reading is that the humanistic model is a partial answer to a real question. Firms that operate in its spirit produce better outcomes than firms that do not. The question of what *full human flourishing* requires is a question larger than any single firm can answer, and the firm should not pretend otherwise. The humanistic firm contributes to the conditions of flourishing without claiming to be its sufficient cause.

## Integration: a single decision through the chapter's apparatus

A U.S. consulting firm — call it Northpoint — has been engaged by a Saudi Arabian ministry to advise on a major infrastructure project. The engagement is multi-year, multi-million-dollar, and involves a team of approximately fifteen Northpoint consultants relocating to Riyadh for the duration. The project requires close coordination with Saudi government counterparts during Ramadan; with male and female consultants on the team and Saudi norms requiring gender-segregated working environments in many official contexts; with the expectation of evening hospitality dinners that a U.S. firm might consider gift-giving violations under its anti-bribery policies; and with the gradual evolution of Saudi norms under the country's *Vision 2030* economic-modernization program, which is changing some of these expectations during the engagement itself.

Apply the chapter's apparatus.

*Enculturation and acculturation.* The Northpoint consultants will arrive shaped by U.S. corporate enculturation — the time-pressed, individualistic, contract-mediated work culture. They will encounter Saudi work culture — relational, family-mediated, hospitality-intensive. Both cultures will change one another during the engagement. The firm should plan for that, not against it.

*Historical context.* The engagement is happening during a specific moment in Saudi modernization (Vision 2030, ongoing since 2016) and in a particular phase of U.S.-Saudi diplomatic relations. The ethical questions of an engagement five years earlier or later would be different. The firm should track the historical specificity of *this* moment.

*Geography and religion.* Ramadan will affect the project rhythm. Gender-segregation norms will affect team composition for specific meetings. The Friday-Saturday weekend will affect the project calendar. The firm's planning should accommodate these from the beginning, not as exceptions.

*The thin universal core.* The U.S. anti-bribery commitments — the honesty, non-coercion, fair-dealing, persons-as-ends commitments that travel with the firm — must be honored regardless of local hospitality practices. *The hospitality dinner is normal here* is not an answer to *did this dinner cross the line into something the firm could not defend at headquarters*. The firm should pre-define the line, in writing, before the engagement begins, with specific dollar thresholds, specific reportable activities, and a clear escalation path.

*The thick local variation.* The hospitality dinners are not categorically forbidden. The shape and timing of the workday during Ramadan is appropriately adjusted. The gender-segregated meeting structures, where they apply, are accommodated. The firm operates inside the thick local while staying inside the thin universal.

*The humanistic dimension.* The fifteen consultants will be living in Riyadh for several years. Their families' welfare, their professional development during the engagement, the cultural training they receive before and during the assignment — these are humanistic-model questions that affect the firm's long-run capacity to operate in international assignments. A firm that treats the consultants as instruments of the engagement and not as flourishing people will, over time, lose the consultants worth keeping.

The integration is the central claim of the chapter. *Cross-cultural business ethics is the discipline of operating inside both the thin universal core and the thick local variation simultaneously.* Done well, it produces durable cross-cultural relationships, lower long-run costs, and better outcomes for all parties. Done badly — at either pole — it produces the Manhattan failure (universal pretense imposing on local context) or the Petrobras failure (local pretense excusing universal violation). The middle path is the honest one.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can distinguish enculturation from acculturation and identify how each operates in a specific cross-cultural business setting. You can trace the major historical eras of business ethics and identify how each era's characteristic question was shaped by its technology and social structure. You can analyze the role of geography, religion, and individual conscience in producing the ethical landscape of a specific place. You can distinguish the thin universal core of ethical commitments from the thick local variations and apply the distinction to a specific case. And you can evaluate the humanistic business model and recognize both its operational claims and its philosophical limits.

The single idea that matters most is that ethical universalism and cultural sensitivity are not opposed. They are layered. The thin universal core constrains; the thick local variation specifies. The careless practitioner collapses the distinction in one of two directions — either imposing the home culture as if it were universal or accepting the local culture as if no universal core existed. The honest practitioner operates inside both.

The common mistake to watch for is the displacement of moral responsibility from the firm to a vaguely-specified *local culture*. The defense *that is just how things are done here* is sometimes accurate; more often, it is a fiction the firm has constructed to justify a practice it could not defend at home. The discipline of the chapter is to refuse the displacement and to specify, in concrete terms, which norms are universal and which are genuinely local.

You should now be able to teach the thin-core-thick-local distinction and the five historical eras to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in twenty minutes, with one specific case for each.
