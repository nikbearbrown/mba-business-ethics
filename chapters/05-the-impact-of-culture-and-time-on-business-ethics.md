# Chapter 5 — The Impact of Culture and Time on Business Ethics

*What counts as right behavior has changed before. It is changing now. The honest practitioner plans for that.*

---

In May of 1626, on the southern tip of a forested island the Lenape people called *Mannahatta*, a Dutch West India Company representative named Pieter Minuit handed over sixty guilders' worth of farming tools, muskets, kettles, axes, and clothing, and walked away believing he owned an island.

The Lenape walked away believing they had granted a right of shared use.

Both parties had signed. Both parties believed they had a deal. Neither party was lying. Within a generation, the misunderstanding produced violent conflict. Within a century, the Lenape had been displaced from their homeland and the Dutch West India Company had become the New York Stock Exchange.

This is the chapter's central puzzle: two parties operating in complete good faith, each using a coherent ethical framework, each believing the transaction honored their obligations — and the transaction harming one of them severely. Not because someone cheated. Because the two parties had different cultural assumptions about *what was being exchanged*. The category *outright permanent ownership of land* was not in the Lenape's legal vocabulary. The category *temporary shared usage rights* was not in Pieter Minuit's.

That gap is still open. It is open in every cross-cultural transaction conducted today. The discipline of this chapter is to see it clearly enough to stop falling into it.

## How culture gets inside business

There is a child born in Tokyo in 2010. At a New Year's gathering in 2014, she watches her grandmother greet her grandfather's business associate. She sees the angle of the bow, the timing, the particular quality of the pause before and after. She does not read this in a manual. She absorbs it — the way a child absorbs language, through repetition and observation and the thousand small corrections of the surrounding world. By the time she joins a Tokyo firm at twenty-two, the bowing is not a rule she follows. It is something she simply does. It no longer feels cultural to her. It feels like *what one does*.

The technical name for this process is enculturation. It is how a culture installs itself in a person — invisibly, before the person has the vocabulary to name what is being installed. Language, customs, implicit hierarchies, unstated rules about who speaks first and who waits, who initiates and who defers: by adulthood these have been internalized so deeply that they feel like features of reality rather than features of one specific culture's way of operating.

The companion concept is acculturation — what happens when a person formed in one culture encounters and is changed by another. Acculturation is rarely symmetric. The smaller, less powerful, or more recently arrived culture is typically changed more by the encounter than the larger or longer-established one. The Lenape in 1626 were acculturated by the Dutch — adopting European tools, materials, eventually European disease — far more than the Dutch were acculturated by the Lenape. That asymmetry is the structural feature of cross-cultural business that most universalist ethical theories quietly skip over.

Now: these processes do not stop at the firm's front door. The executive who walks into a Bangkok negotiation has been enculturated into a specific theory of what business is, how time works, what a contract means, and who speaks first. The counterparty across the table has been enculturated into a different theory on every one of those points. Neither theory is written down. Neither feels like a theory to the people holding it. Both feel like *reality*.

Several specific mechanisms are worth naming because they show up in business ethics most directly.

**Language carries assumptions.** The dominance of English in international finance carries with it a set of assumptions the language has been encoding for centuries: contracts are written and comprehensive; time is scarce and optimizable; persons are autonomous individuals with property rights; disputes go to court. A firm whose executives all operate in English is operating, partly invisibly, inside those assumptions. The assumptions are not wrong. They are not universal either.

**Stated beliefs and operative beliefs are different things.** IBM's stated beliefs — client success, innovation, trust — are the explicit form. What the firm actually does when those beliefs come under financial pressure is the operative form. George W. Merck said it directly in 1950, at the Medical College of Virginia: *We try to remember that medicine is for the patient, not for the profits. The profits follow, and if we have remembered that, they have never failed to appear.* The operative test is whether the behavior under pressure matches the stated belief. Most firms' stated and operative cultures diverge more than their communications suggest.

Example rows: Merck's 1950 statement vs. Merck's opioid-era decisions; IBM's stated innovation values vs. workforce-reduction patterns; a generic bank's customer-first mission statement vs. its incentive-compensation structure. Caption: "The right column is the operative culture. The left column is, at best, an aspiration."] -->

**Consumerism is not the natural form of economic life.** The lifestyle organized around the acquisition of goods and services — what American economists in 1950 called the consumer society — is globally dominant by measured share but is one cultural form among several that have existed and that still exist. Other forms (collective consumption, religiously-mediated restraint, subsistence with cultural wealth) are real and, in some circumstances, viable. A firm whose marketing assumes the consumer-society default is, often, exporting that default to markets where it will produce specific consequences the firm has not been asked about.

Here is what all of this means for ethics, stated simply: when two parties to a business transaction have been enculturated into different frameworks, a transaction can be fully ethical by *both* parties' lights and still harm one of them. That is not a paradox. It is what happened in lower Manhattan in 1626. The ethical response is not to pretend one framework is universal. It is to develop enough cultural literacy to see *both* frameworks clearly enough to negotiate honestly across them.

But — and this is the chapter's first major trade-off — cultural sensitivity, taken far enough, becomes evasion. *Whatever is normal here is acceptable here* sounds like humility. In practice it is often a way of explaining why a firm's operations in a foreign market include practices it would never permit at headquarters. Longer working hours. Looser anti-corruption norms. Weaker environmental standards. *That is just how things are done here* is a sentence that does specific work in those conversations. The work is the displacement of moral responsibility from the firm to the receiving culture. The chapter will come back to this when the apparatus is built.

## How time shapes ethics

A photograph from a New York office in 1957 shows a typical scene. Half the people in the room are smoking. The smoke fills the air to about chest height. There is an ashtray on every desk. The pregnant woman at the back of the room, taking minutes, has a cigarette in her hand. None of this strikes anyone in the room as worth noting. It is what offices look like.

Sixty years later, most of those office workers are dead, several from lung cancer. The ashtrays are gone. The 1957 photograph reads as historical evidence of a different culture — not a recognizable office.

<!-- → [IMAGE: A period photograph of a 1950s American office interior showing multiple people smoking at desks — ideally a press or stock photograph from that era. Caption: "1957: normal. The ethics did not change. What society was willing to enforce did."] -->

Tobacco's transformation from acceptable workplace presence to publicly contested industry took about four decades — from the U.S. Surgeon General's January 1964 report to the 2006 federal racketeering judgment in *United States v. Philip Morris*. The shift was not a change in tobacco's biology. The shift was a change in what the surrounding society was willing to allow, regulate, or tolerate. Time changed business ethics around tobacco. It did not change tobacco.

That engine — what society is willing to enforce, not what is intrinsically right — has been running across the full history of commercial life, producing eras with characteristic ethical questions.

**The mercantilist era** (roughly 1500 to 1750). Trade is mediated by chartered companies — the Dutch West India Company, the British East India Company — operating under explicit royal grants of monopoly. The era's defining ethical question is the morality of the chartered monopoly itself. The Manhattan transaction is a mercantilist-era event; its cultural-misunderstanding structure is the era's characteristic failure mode.

**The industrial era** (roughly 1750 to 1920). Steam, coal, mechanized factories, the transformation of agrarian economies into urban-industrial ones. The era's characteristic ethical question is what employers owe workers under conditions of unprecedented scale and unprecedented labor intensity. Dickens wrote the literary indictment. Ida Tarbell wrote the journalistic one — her analysis of John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil contained the line: *Would you ask for scruples in an electric dynamo?* The question was rhetorical. The subsequent century suggests the answer had better be yes.

**The postindustrial era** (roughly 1920 to 1970). Mass markets, mass consumption, the rise of advertising and public relations. Edward Bernays — the Austrian-American who is sometimes called the father of public relations — engineered a 1929 parade in which young women smoked Lucky Strike cigarettes as *torches of freedom*, marketing tobacco to women as an act of feminist liberation. The era's characteristic ethical question is what the firm owes the consumer when the firm is large and information-asymmetric enough to shape the consumer's preferences before the consumer gets to choose.

**The information era** (roughly 1970 to 2010). The personal computer, the internet, the mobile phone, the rise of platforms whose primary product is attention. The era's characteristic ethical question is the management of personal data — the firm's access to information about its customers becoming more comprehensive than the customers' access to information about themselves. Cambridge Analytica and the Equifax breach are the era's defining cases.

**The globalization era and what comes next** (2010 onward). The early-2000s confidence that globalization would produce convergence has been complicated by the populist reactions of the 2010s — Brexit, the U.S. tariff regimes, the partial withdrawal of supply chains from China, the resurgence of national-economic-sovereignty movements across multiple countries. The era's characteristic ethical question may turn out to be the management of obligations across jurisdictions whose legal-political alignment with one another can no longer be assumed.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: A horizontal timeline from 1500 to 2030 with the five eras as labeled bands. Each band contains its characteristic ethical question as a one-line callout and one defining case (Manhattan 1626 / factory conditions 1840 / Lucky Strike 1929 / Cambridge Analytica 2018 / supply-chain jurisdiction 2020s). Student should notice: each era's defining question was invisible in the previous era, and each era's settled answers were contested in the one before.] -->

The key insight here is not the list. The key insight is that ethical questions are era-specific. What was contested in one era became settled in the next. What was unimagined in one era became central in the next. The firm that does not know which era it is operating in — and what questions *that era* is raising — is, in the plainest sense, historically illiterate. And historical illiteracy in business ethics produces the same result as any other kind: you repeat mistakes that a faster learner would have avoided.

One more thing about the timing. Tobacco firms in the 1990s faced the trade-off starkly. The firms whose response to the changing public-health consensus was litigation and denial paid the largest costs. The firms whose response was diversification and gradual exit from the U.S. cigarette market paid lower long-run costs. The shift took thirty years; the firms whose horizons were shorter than thirty years made decisions the longer-horizon firms were glad they had not made. Pharmaceutical firms in 2026 face the equivalent question on opioids. Fossil-fuel firms face it on climate. Social-media firms face it on attention extraction. The structure of the trade-off is the same in each case: defensive operation under the current regime costs less now and more later; proactive transformation toward the coming regime costs more now and less later.

The chapter's working argument, based on the long-horizon evidence, is that the proactive option is almost always the cheaper one. The firms that learned from tobacco's history made different decisions than the ones that did not.

## Geography, religion, and what they carry

In a Tuareg encampment in the southern Sahara, before any business is discussed, three glasses of tea are served. The first is bitter. The second is sweet. The third is gentle. The Tuareg proverb that names the sequence — *the first cup is bitter like life, the second sweet like love, the third gentle like death* — is also the social engine of what follows. By the third glass, perhaps two hours in, the parties have established who they are, who their families are, where they are from, what they hope from the encounter. Only then does the substantive negotiation begin.

A New York businessperson parachuted into the same encampment with a deal to close by Friday will find this incomprehensible and then frustrating. That frustration is the firm's enculturation showing. And the Tuareg, for their part, will find the time-pressed visitor's haste rude — and, more practically, untrustworthy. *A person who cannot sit through three glasses of tea is a person whose long-term reliability is not yet established.*

This is a real difference, and it is not primarily about etiquette. It is a different theory of what business is. The U.S. version says business is a transaction, and relationship is something that emerges from a history of successful transactions. The Tuareg version says business is a relationship, and transactions are events within an established relationship. Neither theory is wrong. They are genuinely different frameworks, producing different expectations of good faith.

Several specific dimensions of cultural variation affect business ethics most directly.

**Time orientation.** Cultures vary on this in ways that go deeper than scheduling. The German *sofort* and the Italian *subito* both nominally mean *at once* and produce different actual response times. The Kenyan *tutaonana baadaye* (see you later) leaves the *later* genuinely open. The U.S. *a New York minute* compresses urgency further than any of them. None of these is wrong. Each produces different transactional rhythms and different expectations of what counts as good faith.

**Space and contact.** Cultures vary on physical proximity in ways that affect business interactions every day. Contact cultures — much of South America, the Middle East, parts of southern Europe — use closer physical proximity, more touch, more sustained eye contact. Non-contact cultures — much of northern Europe, the Far East, the United States — maintain more distance. A Nigerian business meeting in which participants are seated too far apart registers as unfriendly. The same distance in Helsinki registers as appropriate. The norm is not arbitrary; it is functional. The cross-cultural error is treating your own norm as reality.

**Religion.** Until the seventeenth century, in most of the world, religion and ethics were inseparable. The Enlightenment in Europe produced an attempted separation that has been incomplete and is still contested. Most contemporary business ethics in the West operates as if the separation is complete; in practice, the underlying ethical commitments often trace to specific religious traditions whose vocabulary has been secularized without being abandoned. John D. Rockefeller's lifelong Baptist faith and his conviction that *God had given him money for mankind's benefit* were not eccentric features of his career; they were structural to his understanding of the philanthropy that funded the University of Chicago, Rockefeller University, and the Rockefeller Foundation. Whether one accepts the theological premise or not, the cultural inheritance shapes the present.

One concept worth carrying forward: *stewardship* — the idea that wealth and resources are held by their possessor on behalf of someone else, with a duty of care attached — appears in Jewish, Christian, Islamic, Hindu, Buddhist, and indigenous African and American religious frameworks. Different theological underpinnings, considerable practical overlap. The convergence is not coincidental. It points toward something that every major tradition that has thought hard about wealth has arrived at independently: the person with resources is not simply the owner. They are also a trustee.

## What survives translation — and what doesn't

Now the chapter's central claim, and the most useful synthesis of what has come before.

Across every tradition covered in Chapter 2 — Aristotelian and Confucian virtue ethics, Benthamite-Millian utilitarianism, Kantian deontology, Rawlsian contractualism — and across the major religious traditions, a small number of ethical commitments recur with sufficient consistency that they constitute a *thin universal core*. These items survive cultural translation. They are the floor below which no tradition allows commerce to operate.

*Honesty.* Every major tradition prohibits the deliberate misrepresentation of fact in transactions. The Confucian *zhōngxìn* (wholehearted sincerity), the Kantian duty of truth-telling, the Christian prohibition on false witness, the Islamic *amana* (trustworthiness): the form varies, the prohibition is constant.

*Non-coercion.* No tradition endorses the use of force or fraud to extract value from a non-consenting party.

*Fair dealing.* The duty to honor commitments and deliver what was agreed to, on the terms agreed to. *Pacta sunt servanda* — agreements are to be kept — is the Roman-law version; the analogous principle exists in every commercial code.

*Stewardship.* The duty to manage entrusted resources with care, not as the steward's personal property.

*Treating persons as ends.* The Kantian formulation is the sharpest, but the underlying claim — that human persons are not merely instruments of someone else's purpose — is present in every tradition that takes ethics seriously.

These five items are the thin universal core. They travel. They cannot be locally suspended.

Below the thin core, the thick local norms vary without violating it. *How* honesty is signaled (direct statement versus indirect implication). *How* commitments are made (written contract versus oral promise). *What* counts as appropriate gift-giving. *How* time is allocated in negotiation. *What* religious practices are accommodated. *How* close to stand. These vary legitimately. The thick local does not contradict the thin core; it specifies how the thin core is operationalized in a particular cultural setting.

The hardest cases are the ones where the firm violates the thin core and *defends the violation by appeal to local norms*. This is where the chapter's machinery reveals its purpose.

Between 2003 and 2014, Petróleo Brasileiro — Petrobras, the Brazilian state-controlled oil company — became one of the largest corporate corruption cases in commercial history. The mechanism was simple and large-scale: construction and engineering contractors paying kickbacks to senior Petrobras executives, typically two to five percent of contract value. The kickbacks funded executive lifestyles and political party operations — including campaign expenditures of multiple federal officials. The total value of fraudulent contracts has been estimated in the tens of billions of U.S. dollars. The investigation — codenamed *Lava Jato*, Operation Car Wash, after the small money-laundering operation at a Brasília gas station where it began in 2014 — eventually resulted in more than two hundred convictions, including senators, federal officials, former president Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, and the country's largest construction firm, Odebrecht.

The cultural defense was attempted by some commentators. The argument relied on *jeitinho* — the Brazilian cultural practice of finding informal workarounds — as a long-standing feature of business and political life. The defense fails on three grounds. First, the practices were illegal under Brazilian law itself — not a case of foreign norms being imposed, but a case of Brazil's own norms being violated. Second, the harms fell disproportionately on Brazilian citizens through inflated public-works costs and political instability. Third, the corrupt practices are rejected by every major ethical tradition Chapter 2 covered. *Jeitinho* as everyday social lubrication is one thing. Multi-billion-dollar systematic kickbacks are another. Cultural sensitivity does not require equating them.

The discipline of cross-cultural ethics is, in part, the discipline of refusing this displacement. *Local culture* is not a synonym for *whatever the firm can get away with*. The thin core constrains regardless of geography. The thick local does not provide cover.

## The honest middle path

Consider one more case. An American auditor — call her Jillian — is leading a team at an Islamabad investment bank during Ramadan. The local employees are fasting: no food, water, or other consumables from sunrise to sunset. On her first morning, she suggests a coffee break, gets awkward silence, and realizes the error. Now she is uncertain about lunch.

She has three options. Eat in her office, out of sight. Leave the building and eat at a Western restaurant. Fast with the team during the workday and break fast at sunset together.

The first two options preserve Jillian's energy. The third costs her something real — fasting for a month while leading an audit is genuinely difficult. It also signals, more strongly than any verbal statement, that she takes the team's practices as serious enough to share. The relationship she builds by choosing the third option is an asset her American competitors — the ones who took the coffee break — do not have. Over a career of cross-cultural work, that asset compounds.

There is no required answer. The right choice depends on Jillian's health, the audit's intensity, the team's specific expectations. The chapter's point is that the choice *is* a choice, not an inevitability, and that the practitioner who makes it deliberately — with cultural literacy and practical wisdom — is operating at a level the one who stumbles into it is not.

<!-- → [TABLE: Three-column decision matrix for Jillian's Ramadan choice — rows: Option A (eat in office), Option B (leave building), Option C (fast with team). Columns: immediate cost to Jillian, signal sent to team, long-run relationship asset. Student should notice that the option with the highest immediate cost produces the highest long-run asset — and that none of the options violates the thin universal core, making this genuinely a thick-local judgment call.] -->

That practical wisdom is what the chapter has been building toward. Ethical universalism and cultural sensitivity are not opposed. They are layered. The thin universal core constrains. The thick local variation specifies. The careless practitioner collapses the distinction in one of two directions — either imposing the home culture as if it were universal (Pieter Minuit in 1626) or accepting the local culture as if no universal core existed (the Petrobras apologist in 2015). The honest practitioner operates inside both.

The Manhattan failure and the Petrobras failure are mirror images. Manhattan: a universal framework (European property law) imposed on a different framework (Lenape usage rights) without enough cultural literacy to see the gap. Petrobras: a local cultural defense constructed to excuse a violation that no local culture actually sanctioned. Both failures are failures of the same discipline — the discipline of seeing which norms travel and which do not, and operating honestly across the difference.

That discipline is what this chapter is for.
