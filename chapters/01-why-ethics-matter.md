# Why Ethics Matter

In late July of 2017, somewhere inside the headquarters of Equifax — a credit-reporting agency whose servers held the financial fingerprints of most adults in the United States — a small group of executives received an unwelcome briefing. The servers had been breached. Names, Social Security numbers, addresses, in many cases credit card details, had been quietly leaving the building since the middle of May. By the time the briefing happened, almost half the population of the country had been exposed.

The public did not learn this until September 7.

In the six weeks between the discovery and the announcement, three Equifax executives sold off nearly two million dollars of company stock. When the announcement came, the share price fell about fourteen percent. By September 15, the chief information officer and the chief of security had retired. By September 26, the chief executive had resigned, days before he was scheduled to testify before Congress.

Pause on those six weeks. Two questions sit inside them, and the questions are not the same question. The first: was selling that stock *legal*? The second: was it *right*? You can hold an opinion on the first that has nothing to do with your opinion on the second. You can hold an opinion on the second that has nothing to do with your opinion on the first.

This chapter is about that gap. The space between what the law lets you do and what your stakeholders, your colleagues, and the version of yourself you can sleep next to actually need you to do. Most of business ethics — almost all of it — happens in that gap. The argument of this chapter is that operating inside that gap as if no one is watching is, over any long enough time horizon, the most expensive bet you can make.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** ethical conduct from legal compliance and **identify** decisions where the two diverge.
- **Apply** three normative ethical lenses — consequences, duties, and character — to a specific business decision and explain what each lens reveals.
- **Diagnose** the difference between a stockholder and a stakeholder, and **construct** a stakeholder analysis for a given action.
- **Argue** the case for a single ethical standard against the case for ethical relativism.
- **Explain** why long-term goodwill is an asset that does not appear on the balance sheet and how short-term thinking destroys it.

Note the verbs: *distinguish, apply, diagnose, construct, argue, explain*. Not *understand*, not *appreciate*. By the end of the chapter, you should be able to do these things in front of someone else.

## Prerequisites

You need only one piece of equipment for this chapter: the ability to take seriously the possibility that the right thing to do and the smart thing to do are the same thing. Most arguments to the contrary — *ethics is for monks; business is war; nice guys finish last* — turn out, on inspection, to be either bumper stickers or self-serving stories told by people whose own track records do not flatter the slogan. We will inspect them.

## Why this chapter matters

Every other chapter in this book is, in some sense, a special case of this one. Chapter 2 examines where ethical reasoning came from. Chapters 3 and 4 examine *who* is owed ethical conduct — the map of stakeholders. Chapters 6 and 7 split the question along employer-employee lines. Chapter 9 narrows it to specific professions. The framing of this chapter — that ethics is a system of decisions made by named people in named situations under named pressures — is what those later chapters specialize.

There is also a working assumption in this book worth saying out loud. We use the words *ethics* and *morals* interchangeably. Some philosophers distinguish them — ethics as the rules a profession agrees to, morals as the rules a person agrees to with themselves — but the distinction does not earn its keep in business analysis. If a sentence is true with one word, it stays true if you swap in the other.

## Concept 1: What ethics is, and what it is not

A clothing-store cashier — call her J. — stands behind the counter on a quiet Tuesday afternoon. Her cousin walks in, picks out a hundred dollars' worth of merchandise, sets it on the counter, and asks for her twenty-five-percent employee discount.

The store's written policy permits employee discounts on personal purchases. There is no policy explicitly forbidding the use of the discount on a relative's purchase. There is no auditor in the store. There is no surveillance footage anyone will ever review. The transaction will save her cousin twenty-five dollars. It will cost the store the same twenty-five dollars in foregone margin.

What should J. do?

This is the cleanest possible illustration of why business ethics exists as its own subject. Notice what is *not* the question. The question is not "is it legal" — it is. The question is not "will she be punished" — she will not. The question is not "does anyone notice" — no one does. Strip those away and what is left is the only question ethics has ever really asked: *given that you can, should you?*

### The mechanism: ethics as a self-imposed standard

Ethics, at its root, is the set of standards of behavior that a person — or a company, treated as a moral agent — *holds themselves to* in the absence of external enforcement. Notice the direction of force. Law presses on you from outside. Ethics is the code you press onto yourself from inside. When the two agree, you cannot tell them apart from your behavior alone — you do the right thing whether the auditor is watching or not. When they disagree, you find out which one is actually running you.

Three terms do most of the load in this section. Each is worth specifying carefully because each gets misused as cover for fuzzy thinking.

**Ethics**: the standards of behavior to which we hold ourselves in our personal and professional lives. The keyword is *we hold ourselves*. The standards are chosen and maintained from inside, not imposed from outside.

**Compliance**: the extent to which a company conducts its business operations in accordance with applicable regulations, statutes, and laws. Compliance is the floor. The law is the floor. Ethics is what you build on top of the floor — voluntarily, because you have decided that the floor is not enough.

**Integrity**: from the same root as *integer*, meaning whole, undivided. Integrity is the unity between what you say and what you do. A person of integrity is the same person on Monday and Saturday, with the auditor and without, in the boardroom and at the dinner table. The opposite of integrity is not deceit — it is fragmentation. Different ethics for different rooms.

**Stakeholders** are the named parties who can be affected by the business's decisions: clients, customers, suppliers, investors, retailers, employees, the media, the government, members of the surrounding community, competitors, and the environment itself. The list is concrete, not vague. *Stakeholder* is not a mood. It is a roster you can write down.

A simple test of whether you are doing ethics or doing public relations: a stakeholder analysis names individuals or groups in writing and notes how each would be affected. If you cannot name them, you are not analyzing.

### The trade-off: legal compliance is cheaper but does not buy what ethics buys

Why not just comply with the law and stop there? It is faster, it is auditable, it is defensible.

The trade-off is that compliance is a floor designed for the worst-behaved actor in the system. The law specifies what no actor is permitted to fall below. It says nothing about what excellent actors should aspire to. Two firms can both be in full legal compliance, and one can be the place you would entrust your retirement savings to and the other can be the place you would not let near a parking meter. The difference between them lives entirely above the floor.

There is also a temporal asymmetry, which Concept 3 will return to. Legal compliance protects you from punishment *today*. Ethical conduct protects you from your customers and employees walking out the door *over the next decade*. A firm that confuses the two — that treats not-being-sued as the goal — is optimizing the wrong horizon.

The Hippocratic Oath is a useful contrast. Doctors and nurses operate under a code that traces back to ancient Greece, embraced by most healthcare professionals today as a baseline of duty owed to patients regardless of what the law of any particular country says. Lawyers have an analogous code. Business, historically, has not had one. Over the past century the gap has begun to close — Johnson & Johnson's *Our Credo*, written by former chair Robert Wood Johnson, is one well-known example, and *Forbes* now publishes an annual list of companies it considers most ethical — but business ethics remains, in part, a code each firm and each professional has to commit to itself.

### Worked example: the cousin's discount

Back to J. behind the counter.

Run the question through each of the three terms above.

*Ethics.* What standard does J. hold herself to? If her standard is "do nothing the audit would catch," she swipes the discount. If her standard is "act in a way I could explain to my manager without flinching," she does not. The standard chosen is the answer.

*Compliance.* The store's written policy is silent on relatives. There is no legal violation. Compliance does not resolve the question.

*Integrity.* If J. swipes the discount for her cousin and the next day asks her own customers to honor the no-return policy on a discounted item, the two stances are inconsistent — she is asking customers to follow a written policy in spirit while she has just declined to. The fragmentation may be invisible to the customers, but it is not invisible to J.

The discount is worth twenty-five dollars. The integrity, if she trades it, is worth substantially more than that — to her, to her relationship with her manager, to her view of her own character. The math is not close. It only looks close because the twenty-five dollars is on the counter and the integrity is not.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that ethics is *the same thing* as religion. It is not. Most religious traditions teach kindness, honesty, and respect for others, and those teachings are useful raw material. But the ethical claim — *treat people fairly because they are people* — does not require a religious premise. The teachings can run on a secular battery. You can be an atheist and a person of integrity. You can be devout and corrupt. The two questions, *what do you believe about the universe* and *how do you treat the cashier*, do not have to track each other.

The second misconception is that ethics and law are the same thing differently named. They are not. The law lags ethics in almost every domain where the question is alive. Slavery was legal long after it was widely understood to be wrong. Smoking advertising on children's television was legal long after the harms were known. Insider trading was legal in the United States until Congress made it illegal in stages between 1934 and 1988. *Legal* and *ethical* are two axes, not one. A four-celled diagram — legal/ethical, legal/unethical, illegal/ethical, illegal/unethical — would be useful here, and you should sketch it on paper before reading further. Most of the interesting business cases live in the upper-right cell: legal but unethical.

The third misconception is that ethics is for soft people in calm times — that when the pressure is on, the realist drops the ethics. It is the reverse. Ethics is the discipline of behavior under pressure. Pressure is when it is *needed*; the rest of the time it is decoration.

## Concept 2: Three lenses for examining a decision

A patient is wheeled into a surgical theater. The surgeon, before scrubbing in, recites — silently or audibly, but consistently — a small piece of an oath traceable to the fifth century BCE: *first, do no harm*. The oath is not a contract. The patient cannot hold the surgeon to it in court the way one party holds another to a contract. Yet the oath does work. It pre-commits the surgeon to a standard before the surgical decisions begin, so that when complications arrive in the next ninety minutes — and they will — the surgeon does not have to *decide* her ethics in real time. She decided them when she took the oath.

Business decisions, even the small ones, are like that surgical theater. They arrive faster than you can reason about them. You need a pre-loaded framework. You need *lenses*.

Philosophy has worked on this problem for a long time and has produced three lenses worth carrying. Each one stares at the same decision from a different angle. Each one sees something the others miss.

### The mechanism: three lenses that examine different parts of the same act

A decision has three parts. There is the *end* it produces (who is helped, who is harmed, in what amounts). There is the *means* by which it gets carried out (which duties are honored, which are violated). And there is the *character* of the person making it (what kind of person they are becoming by making this kind of decision). Three lenses, one for each part.

**Consequentialism — the lens of ends.** Sometimes called utilitarianism, sometimes teleology. The claim: an action is ethical if its consequences produce the greatest good for the greatest number of people. To apply the lens, ask, *who is helped, who is harmed, by how much, with what probability?* and add it up. Consequentialism is the language most business school decision-making courses already speak — net present value, expected utility, return on investment — extended to non-monetary outcomes. The trade-off it makes is that it does not ask whether the means used to reach the good outcome were themselves acceptable. A surgeon who saves five lives by harvesting one healthy patient's organs would, on a strict utilitarian count, have done good. Most people have a strong intuition that the surgeon has done evil. That intuition is where the second lens enters.

**Deontology — the lens of means.** From the Greek *deon*, meaning duty. Most associated with the eighteenth-century German philosopher Immanuel Kant. The claim: certain duties — tell the truth, keep promises, do not use a person merely as an instrument of your own ends — bind regardless of the consequences. The means lend nobility to the ends. To apply the lens, ask: *what duties are owed to each person in this transaction, and does my proposed action honor or violate them?* The trade-off here is the mirror of consequentialism: a strict deontologist will refuse to lie even when the lie would prevent enormous suffering. The duty wins; the math loses. Deontology is the language of contracts, of professional codes, and of fiduciary duty. When a financial adviser tells you "I owe you a duty of loyalty regardless of whether disclosing this conflict costs me your business," the adviser is speaking deontology.

**Virtue ethics — the lens of character.** Roots in the Greek philosophical tradition, in Socrates as a role model, in the cardinal virtues — courage, self-control, justice, wisdom — practiced as habits. The claim: ethical action flows from ethical character, and character is built by the routine practice of virtuous habits over a lifetime. To apply the lens, ask: *what kind of person — and by extension what kind of organization — does this decision make me?* The trade-off is that virtue ethics is slow. You cannot install courage in a bonus cycle. Character is built the way a cathedral is built — one stone at a time, over a longer time horizon than any single quarterly meeting. Its strength is also that slowness: a person of strong virtue does not have to *decide* to behave well in a crisis. The behavior is already there, pre-loaded.

[FIGURE: A diagram showing one decision (a box in the center) with three arrows pointing into it from the left, right, and bottom — labeled "ENDS (consequences)," "MEANS (duties)," and "CHARACTER (virtues)." The student should notice that all three lenses examine the same decision and that disagreement among them is the signal that the decision is genuinely difficult.]

### The trade-off: each lens reveals what the others miss

The honest move is to use all three. Together they form a triangulation. A decision that looks good from one lens but ugly from the other two is a decision worth re-examining. A decision that all three lenses approve is defensible from every angle ethics has to offer. A decision that all three lenses reject is the easy case — most business ethics failures are *not* like this. Most of them are the cases where one lens approves and the others squirm.

Consider a fourth move that sharpens all three. Aristotle called it *phronesis* — fro-NEE-sis — practical wisdom. The capacity to take a general principle and apply it well to a specific situation. *Phronesis* is what makes the difference between a person who knows what utilitarianism says and a person who can actually *use* utilitarianism to make a decision under uncertainty in a meeting that is running late. Ikujiro Nonaka and Hirotaka Takeuchi, writing in *Harvard Business Review* in May 2011, argued that practical wisdom is the often-forgotten third kind of knowledge — beyond explicit knowledge and tacit knowledge — that distinguishes wise leaders. They listed five qualities: making moral judgments for the common good in specific situations, quickly assessing situations and envisioning the consequences of possible actions, creating shared purpose among employees, communicating through stories and metaphors anyone can understand, and encouraging the development of practical wisdom in others. The list is operational — each item is something you can be observed to do or fail to do.

### Worked example: a layoff decision through three lenses

A company is twelve months into a downturn. The CFO presents two options to the executive team. Option A: lay off three hundred people across the company by attrition and one round of voluntary buyouts, taking eighteen months. Option B: lay off five hundred people in a single announced round next month. Option B saves more money sooner. Option A is gentler.

*Through the consequentialist lens.* Option A: three hundred people are displaced over eighteen months, with two months of severance each, and most likely outcomes assume sixty percent re-employment within a year. Option B: five hundred people displaced in one month, one month of severance each, with the labor market suddenly flooded by ex-colleagues competing for the same jobs. The total *suffering*, summed across affected people, is plausibly larger under B even though B helps the firm's near-term profit more. The consequentialist lens, applied honestly, often does not say what people think it says. The greatest good for the greatest number is rarely the option that saves the firm the most money in the next quarter.

*Through the deontological lens.* What duties does the firm owe its employees? At minimum, the duties of advance notice, fair severance, and non-deception. Option B provides less advance notice and less severance per person. The duty owed to each individual employee is more fully discharged under A.

*Through the virtue lens.* What kind of company is the executive team becoming by choosing each option? Option B is the choice of an organization that, when the pressure is on, treats employees as costs to be cut. Option A is the choice of an organization that, when the pressure is on, treats employees as people to whom obligations are owed. Five years from now, when the company is recruiting again, the answer to *which kind of company are we* will affect who applies.

Three lenses, applied to one decision. They converge here, as they often do, on the option that is also the slower and more expensive option in the short term. That is not a coincidence. That is part of what makes Concept 3 the chapter's hardest concept.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that you must pick *one* lens and stay loyal to it. You need not. The lenses are tools. A good practitioner reaches for whichever one most illuminates the case at hand and uses the others as checks. Pretending to be a strict utilitarian or a strict deontologist tends to be a way of avoiding the cases where your chosen lens delivers an answer you would not actually live with.

The second misconception is that virtue ethics is a polite name for "be a nice person." It is not. Virtue ethics is a specific and demanding claim about the relationship between habit and character, about courage and self-control as *practiced* skills, and about the cumulative effect of small daily choices on who you become over a working life. Aristotle's *cardinal virtues* are not synonymous with niceness — courage is sometimes the willingness to be unpopular, justice is sometimes the willingness to disappoint a friend.

The third misconception is that the three lenses are mainly philosophy-classroom abstractions. They are not. They are already in use, every day, under different names. Cost-benefit analysis is consequentialism in a suit. Fiduciary duty is deontology in a suit. *Our values* statements in employee handbooks are virtue ethics in a suit. The lenses are not academic; the academic vocabulary is a sharper version of what experienced operators are already doing.

## Concept 3: The long game, goodwill, and the cost of a double standard

Toyota begins selling cars in the United States in 1957. The cars are not initially profitable. For a long stretch — measured in years, not quarters — Toyota is content to lose a small amount of money on each vehicle sold in America.

A Wall Street analyst, looking at the U.S. P&L in 1962, would have asked the obvious question: why is this company tolerating losses? The answer the analyst would have rejected as soft is the answer Toyota was actually following: the company was buying two things that were not yet on the balance sheet. It was buying a relationship of trust with what would become its U.S. customer base, and it was buying the gradual dismantling of an inherited belief that Japanese manufactured goods were cheap and unreliable. Both of those purchases paid out — slowly, and then enormously — over the next several decades. *The Toyota Way*, Jeffrey Liker's 2005 account of the firm's operating philosophy, describes long-term thinking as one of its load-bearing principles, including the explicit willingness to sacrifice short-term financial goals to do so.

This is the move Concept 3 is built around. The thing you cannot put on the balance sheet is the thing whose presence or absence will, over a long enough horizon, determine the fate of the business.

### The mechanism: time, goodwill, and the social contract

Three terms, again. Each names something specific.

**Long-term perspective**: a way of evaluating profit that recognizes that the impact of a business decision may not manifest for a long time. Quarterly earnings are real, but they are also a slice — the most recent ninety days. A decision whose costs land in this quarter and whose benefits arrive over the next forty quarters will look bad on every horizon shorter than ten years and good on every horizon longer than ten years. The horizon you choose determines the answer you get.

**Goodwill**: the value of a business beyond its tangible assets — its reputation, the strength of its brand, the attitude of its workforce, the loyalty of its customers. Goodwill is intangible in the accounting sense (you cannot point to a machine that holds it) but it is observable in customer behavior, employee retention, and pricing power. Goodwill cannot be earned in a quarter. It also cannot be entirely destroyed in a day, but it can be deeply damaged in one — the Equifax announcement was such a day.

**Social contract**: an implicit agreement among societal members to cooperate for mutual benefit. Applied to a business, the social contract says: in exchange for the privileges society grants the firm — limited liability, the right to incorporate, access to courts and infrastructure — the firm owes society an obligation to act in ways that benefit the broader community, not only the shareholders. The Nobel laureate Milton Friedman, in a 1970 *New York Times Magazine* article, argued the opposing position: the only social responsibility of a business is to increase its profits. The position was sharpened in 1976 by the finance professors Michael Jensen and William Meckling, whose "Theory of the Firm" gave the Friedman view a quantitative engine — agency theory and the maximization of shareholder value — that business schools could teach and corporations could measure. From the early 1980s through the mid-2000s, that engine ran American boardrooms. The 2008 financial crisis began the erosion. In August 2019, the Business Roundtable — the trade association of large American CEOs, whose own *Principles of Corporate Governance* had endorsed shareholder primacy in every version since 1997 — formally moved away from the doctrine, with 181 chief executives signing a statement committing to the interests of customers, employees, suppliers, communities, *and* shareholders. Whether that signature changed corporate behavior is a fair question. That it changed the public language of corporate purpose is not.

### The trade-off: short-term margin vs. long-term goodwill

The honest version of the trade-off is uncomfortable. In any given quarter, the firm that cuts a corner — under-discloses a defect, under-pays a vendor, under-disclaims a risk — will probably show a higher reported margin than the firm that does not. The deception works for a while. The trade-off is that goodwill, once damaged, takes much longer to rebuild than it took to spend. And in industries where information now travels instantly — where a single Yelp review can be seen by thousands within an hour, where Twitter and Facebook surface a misstep in a minute — the rebuild horizon has gotten longer, not shorter, in the last twenty years.

The trade-off explains, in part, what happened to many of the firms that pushed the most aggressively into the cut-corners strategies of the mid-2000s. During 2008 and 2009, a wave of firms whose model depended on opacity went bankrupt or were absorbed in distressed sales. Firms that had built genuine long-term goodwill — by treating customers fairly, by keeping employees through downturns, by giving back to local communities — disproportionately survived and, in some cases, used the downturn to acquire competitors at a discount. The same is true on a smaller scale year after year: *Fortune*'s list of most-admired companies, year over year, correlates with above-average long-term margins. Correlation is not causation; one cannot rule out that being profitable makes you admirable rather than the reverse. But the data is at least consistent with the claim that ethical business practices and durable profitability share an underlying cause: the discipline to think past the next quarter.

### Worked example: Equifax through the long game

Return to the executives in late July 2017. The internal briefing has just happened. Imagine a very different conversation in that room.

*Option A (what was done).* Quietly sell stock. Disclose the breach in early September. Offer free credit monitoring after the fact. Watch the share price fall about fourteen percent on disclosure. Lose the chief executive within three weeks. Face hundreds of private lawsuits and multiple government investigations. Spend years of management attention, and roughly a billion dollars in eventual costs, on the consequences.

*Option B (the long-game alternative).* Hold all insider stock sales. Disclose immediately. Take the share-price hit on Tuesday rather than in September — which would have been roughly the same hit, because the underlying breach was the same — but accompanied by a credible narrative of *we found this, we told you within forty-eight hours, here is what we are doing.* Offer free credit monitoring proactively, before the lawsuits forced it. Replace the chief information officer voluntarily as a signal of accountability. The financial cost of Option B is similar in the first ninety days. The cost trajectory diverges sharply afterward. Option A's costs continue to compound through litigation, regulatory scrutiny, and lost trust. Option B's costs flatten earlier because the narrative — *they handled it like adults* — is partly recoverable.

The point is not that Option B is costless. It is not. The point is that, viewed from a horizon of years rather than quarters, Option B is *cheaper*, even on a strictly financial accounting. The executives who chose Option A were not being economically rational. They were being economically rational on the wrong time scale — the next two months. On the time scale that actually mattered to the firm and its shareholders, Option A was the more expensive choice, and it lost the firm both money and reputation.

This generalizes. Most business-ethics failures are not failures of ethics in opposition to economics. They are failures of *time horizon*: the right ethical answer is also the better economic answer if you measure economics over a long enough window. Warren Buffett — a useful authority here because no one has ever credibly accused him of being a soft-headed altruist — has summarized the test as a single sentence: *do nothing you would not be happy to have an unfriendly but intelligent reporter write about on the front page of a newspaper.* Apply that sentence to the Equifax executives' stock sales, and Option A would never have been on the table.

Buffett's other sentence on the matter is sharper still. He instructs his managers: *lose money for the firm, even a lot of money, and I will be understanding; lose reputation for the firm, even a shred of reputation, and I will be ruthless.* The asymmetry is the lesson. Money is recoverable. Reputation, in Buffett's view, is not — at least, not on the time scale Buffett operates on, which is decades.

### Stakeholders: the broader audience the long game serves

A firm that has only one stakeholder — the shareholder — has a simple objective function: maximize shareholder return. A firm that recognizes that customers, employees, suppliers, communities, and the environment also bear consequences of its decisions has a more complicated objective function. The complication is the price of ethics.

A practical exercise. Take any pending decision. Draw three columns on a page. In column one, list every stakeholder you can identify, ordered by the strength of their interest. In column two, write what each stakeholder wants — what their goals or interests would be, in their own words. In column three, write the likely effect of your decision on each. Some entries will fall into more than one column — an employee may also be a customer, a community member may also be a supplier — and that is fine; the exercise is supposed to reveal those overlaps.

The exercise has two effects. First, it forces stakeholders to be *named*. You cannot write *the community* in column one — you have to specify which community, and that specification often reveals that *the community* in your head was a flattering composite. Second, it turns ethical analysis from an instinct into a procedure. Two reasonable people can do the exercise on the same decision and disagree about column three; they can resolve the disagreement by examining column three's evidence. They cannot resolve a disagreement that lives only in instinct.

### Corporate social responsibility: the long game, formalized

When a company commits in writing to act in ways that benefit society as well as shareholders, what it is committing to is called *corporate social responsibility*, or CSR. The phrase is shorter than its content. CSR is the explicit recognition that a firm exists *inside* society, not above it, and that the privileges it enjoys — limited liability, access to courts, public infrastructure — come with obligations.

A useful illustration is TOMS Shoes, founded by Blake Mycoskie in 2006. The model is one-for-one: for every pair of shoes the company sells, it donates one pair to a child in need. As of May 2018, the company had given more than seventy-five million pairs to children in seventy countries. The model is not pure philanthropy — TOMS is a for-profit firm and the giving is part of its brand and customer-acquisition strategy. That blending of profit and giving is precisely the point of CSR: it is not a separate budget line, it is an operating principle.

The model is also not above critique. Some development economists have argued that flooding low-income markets with free shoes can undercut local cobblers and create dependency rather than self-sufficiency. CSR is not a synonym for *good*; it is a commitment to think about effects beyond the shareholder return. The thinking can still arrive at wrong answers. Doing CSR well requires doing CSR seriously — measuring effects, not just announcing intentions.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that ethical relativism — the view that ethics depends entirely on context, with no right answer outside the context — is the *sophisticated* position. It is not. Relativism is sometimes confused with humility, but the two are different. Humility says *I might be wrong about what is ethical*. Relativism says *there is no answer to be wrong about*. The first is a virtue. The second is a capitulation. The position has a specific cost: it makes a person impossible to trust, because the standard they will apply tomorrow is not the one they applied today, and they do not owe you any explanation of which they will pick. A consistent ethical code, applied across contexts, is not a sign of unsophistication. It is the precondition for being trustworthy.

The second misconception is that "good for shareholders" and "good for stakeholders" are the same thing. They are not. They overlap on most ordinary days. They diverge sharply on the days that matter most — the days when the firm has the option to extract value from a non-shareholder party (a customer, a community, the environment) and pass it through to shareholders. Those are the days that test the claim *we serve all our stakeholders*. Most of business-ethics history is the record of those days.

The third misconception is that doing the right thing is a tax on doing well. The available evidence does not support this claim. The most-admired companies in *Fortune*'s annual ranking outperform the broader market on a long-term basis. Goldman Sachs, after the 2008 financial crisis, found in its own internal analysis that customers who reported high trust in the firm were the customers most likely to bring repeat business. Patagonia's *1% for the Planet* program, which donates one percent of all sales to environmental causes, has not prevented the company from becoming a market leader in outdoor gear; on the available evidence, it has helped. The empirical case for the *good is also profitable* claim is not airtight — selection effects are real — but the case for its opposite is weaker still.

## Integration: putting the three concepts to work on one decision

Sometime in early 2001, in a Minnesota office of a company called Interlink Communications, a sales executive named Mark Faris is staring at his revenue numbers. Interlink resells networking equipment, much of it refurbished, much of it sold under a partnership with Cisco Systems — a partner roughly ten thousand times Interlink's size. Cisco has recently begun to discourage its larger customers from buying refurbished equipment from resellers like Interlink and to pressure resellers to deemphasize the offering. Interlink's top-line revenue has dropped about thirty percent.

Faris is the vice president for sales. He decides to make up the difference by lying.

The mechanism, once he and his colleagues have it running, is simple. Cisco's *SMARTnet* contracts promise customers advance hardware replacement when their equipment fails. Interlink begins to file fraudulent SMARTnet claims — reporting failures that have not occurred, receiving replacement equipment Cisco was not contractually obligated to send, then selling the replacement equipment to other customers and depositing the payments. Between January 2001 and August 2002, Faris and others run the scheme one hundred and forty times. The total fraud against Cisco is more than four hundred thousand dollars.

In 2008, federal prosecutors indict him. He pleads guilty to conspiracy to commit mail fraud, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, and conspiracy to commit money laundering. On July 31, 2009, Faris is sentenced to one year and one day. He serves nearly nine months at the Duluth Federal Prison Camp and two months at a halfway house in Minneapolis. From the day his wife and two sons drove him to Duluth to the day he came home, he is away from his family for three hundred and eighteen days. He is ordered to pay two hundred and fifteen thousand dollars in restitution, on a payment plan that runs through 2013.

Apply the three concepts to the decision Faris made in 2001.

*Concept 1 — what ethics is and is not.* When Faris began the fraud, he was already in legal violation — there is no ambiguity here, the way there was with Equifax. But the deeper point is what was happening *inside* him. He was a person of integrity in some rooms — he loved his family, he was responsive to colleagues, he did not steal from his neighbors — and a person of fraud in one specific room, the room with the SMARTnet claim form. Integrity, recall, is the unity between what we say and what we do — across rooms. Faris had fragmented. He had different ethics for the family room and the sales-pipeline room. The fragmentation does not announce itself. It does not feel, while it is happening, like the abandonment of a self. It feels like a small, contained, *I will fix this when conditions improve* exception. The exceptions accumulate. By exception one hundred and forty, the self that made exception one is no longer the self holding the form.

*Concept 2 — the three lenses.* Run the decision through each.

From the consequentialist lens, the math is straightforward and damning. The benefit to Interlink: roughly four hundred thousand dollars over twenty months, a stopgap against the thirty-percent revenue decline. The cost to Cisco: the same four hundred thousand dollars in equipment fraudulently obtained. The cost to Faris personally, after federal indictment and sentencing: nine months in federal prison, two months in a halfway house, three hundred and eighteen days away from his wife and sons, two hundred and fifteen thousand dollars in restitution, the loss of his career in the industry, and a felony record for the rest of his life. Even on a strict utilitarian count — adding up the harms across all parties — the scheme is negative-sum from the moment it starts. The fact that it took several years for the negative sum to be settled is a feature of how slowly the legal system clears its docket, not evidence that the scheme was ever positive-sum.

From the deontological lens, the violation is at the bone of the framework. Faris had a duty to deal honestly with Cisco. He had a duty not to use his role as a means to a fraudulent end. He had a duty of fidelity to his employees, who were now criminally complicit in his decision. The lens does not ask whether the consequences could have been worse or better; it asks whether the duty was honored. It was not.

From the virtue lens, the question that bites is the one Faris himself now puts to corporate audiences as a public speaker: *what kind of legacy do you want to leave for your kids?* The character that did the SMARTnet fraud is not the character he wanted his sons to inherit. Discovering that, in federal prison, is the worst possible time to discover it.

*Concept 3 — the long game.* In the room where Faris made the decision, the time horizon was the next quarter — *will this revenue gap close before someone notices the trajectory?* The horizon that actually mattered was longer. Cisco's internal audit and forensics function did notice. Federal prosecutors took the case. The sentence ran into 2010. The restitution ran through 2013. The reputational consequence — *Mark Faris, federal felon, mail fraud, wire fraud, money laundering* — runs forever, in the sense that any prospective employer, any potential client, any future business partner can look up the docket. Buffett's reporter would have written, in 2002, *executive at small Cisco reseller felt squeezed by a partner thirty thousand times his size and committed fraud for twenty months.* That is a sentence that writes itself, and that, once written, does not unwrite.

There is a second movement in the Faris case that is also worth attending to. After his release, he founded a small company called MPV Ethics and began touring corporate trainings and university campuses, frequently with Hank Shea — a professor of law at the University of St. Thomas in Minnesota and a former federal prosecutor. He talks about the path. He explains how a person who would have rejected a hundred-thousand-dollar bribe in 2000 was, by 2002, calmly running the fraud machine he had once thought was impossible to be drawn into. The story is not redemption arc as flattery; it is a record of what the recovery from a fragmented self actually looks like, and what it costs. Virtue ethics's claim — that character is built one habit at a time — runs in both directions. It can be built. It can also be unbuilt. Faris's two acts together are the most honest possible illustration of both halves.

The integration is the central claim of the chapter. The three concepts are not three separate tools. They are three views of one decision, and they tend to agree more often than the cynical version of business culture would predict. The Faris decision violated Concept 1 (the integrity test failed silently, exception by exception), failed Concept 2 on every lens (worse outcomes, broken duties, deformed character), and depended on a short time horizon under Concept 3 — an under-twenty-month window that was always going to be shorter than the audit and prosecution timelines that eventually closed it. Decisions like this are not rational. They are short-horizon-rational and long-horizon-irrational, which is not the same thing. Mark Faris in 2001 was making a decision that looked smart from inside the next quarter and disastrous from anywhere outside it. The point of carrying the three concepts in your head — *together*, not one at a time — is to be able to see the *anywhere outside it* before you make the decision, not after.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before, or could not do as cleanly.

You can distinguish ethical conduct from legal compliance and identify the gap between them — the place where most of business ethics actually lives. You can apply three lenses, named with their philosophical pedigrees but operational in practice, to any concrete business decision and report what each lens reveals. You can build a stakeholder map of a decision rather than waving in the direction of "the community." You can argue against ethical relativism without retreating into a religious or cultural premise. And you can defend the long-term-goodwill case without requiring anyone to be a saint — the case is made on the firm's own self-interest, correctly time-horizoned.

The single idea that matters most, if you remember nothing else, is this. The right thing to do and the smart thing to do diverge only on short time scales. On any time scale long enough to matter to the firm — measured in years, not quarters — they tend strongly to converge. Most business-ethics failures are not failures of values. They are failures of horizon: someone is solving for the next ninety days when the firm's actual life expectancy is decades.

The common mistake to watch for is the sophisticated-looking shrug — *it depends, ethics is contextual, who are we to say*. This mistake is sometimes confused with humility. It is not humility. Humility says *I might be wrong about what is right*. The shrug says *there is no right to be wrong about*. The first is a virtue. The second is the position of a person you cannot rationally trust with your money or your time.

You should now be able to teach the cashier scenario, the three lenses, and the Equifax timeline to someone who has not read this chapter, in roughly fifteen minutes, without notes. If you cannot, that is the part of the chapter to revisit.

## Connections forward

The next chapter widens the time horizon further. We have been working as if the three lenses arrived intact from the philosophy seminar room. In fact, each of them has a history — millennia of argument, refinement, and counter-argument that produced the form we have inherited. That history is not decoration. It is what tells you which parts of each lens are load-bearing and which are stylistic, which arguments have survived sustained criticism and which have been quietly abandoned. Chapter 2 — *Ethics from Antiquity to the Present* — walks the lineage from the Greeks through the Enlightenment to the modern philosophical landscape, so that when you reach for utilitarianism or deontology in a meeting, you reach for it knowing what survived two thousand five hundred years of argument and what did not.

The chapter after that, Chapter 3, narrows the focus from the lenses to the *targets* of ethical action — the stakeholders. Chapter 1 introduced them as a list. Chapter 3 examines how to prioritize among them when their interests conflict, which they will.

You have the framework. The rest of the book is the specialization.
