# Chapter 1 — Why Ethics Matter
*The Law Is the Floor. Ethics Is What You Build Above It.*

In the summer of 2017, three executives at Equifax — a company that held the financial fingerprints of almost half of all American adults — quietly sold nearly two million dollars of company stock. They sold it after learning, in an internal briefing, that hackers had been inside the company's servers since May. They sold it before telling the public. The public found out on September 7. The share price fell about fourteen percent. Within three weeks, the chief executive had resigned.

Pause on that sequence for a moment, because inside it there is a question — actually two questions — that this entire chapter is built around.

The first question: was selling that stock *legal*?

The second question: was it *right*?

These are not the same question. You can have a firm opinion on the first that has nothing to do with your opinion on the second. And here is the thing that matters: almost everything in business ethics — nearly all of it — happens in the gap between those two questions. The law is the floor. Ethics is what you decide to do above the floor, when no one is checking whether you stay above it.

---

## What ethics actually is

Let me start with what ethics is *not*, because the confusions here are old and stubborn.

Ethics is not the same thing as law. The law lags behind ethics in virtually every domain where the question is alive. Slavery was legal for most of recorded human history, long after it was understood to be wrong by the people practicing it — long after it was understood, by many of the slaveowners themselves, to be wrong. Advertising cigarettes to children on television was legal in the United States until the 1960s, long after the internal documents of tobacco companies showed they understood the product was lethal. Insider trading — buying or selling stock on information the public doesn't have — was legal in the U.S. until Congress made it illegal in stages between 1934 and 1988. *Legal* and *ethical* are two different axes. A four-celled diagram would put them at right angles to each other: legal-and-ethical, legal-and-unethical, illegal-and-ethical, illegal-and-unethical. Most of the interesting cases in business ethics — most of the real decisions you will face — live in the cell that rarely gets named aloud: legal and unethical.

Ethics is also not the same thing as religion. Most religious traditions teach honesty, fairness, and respect for other people, and those teachings are genuinely useful. But the claim that you should treat other people fairly doesn't require a religious premise. It can run on a secular battery. You can be an atheist and a person of integrity. You can be devout and corrupt. The questions — *what do you believe about the universe* and *how do you treat the cashier* — do not have to track each other.

So what *is* ethics?

Ethics is the set of standards you hold yourself to in the absence of enforcement. Notice the direction of force. Law presses on you from the outside. Ethics is the code you press onto yourself from the inside. When the two agree, you can't tell them apart from your behavior — you do the right thing whether the auditor is in the room or not. When they disagree, that's when you find out which one is actually running you.

Three terms do most of the work, and each is worth pinning down precisely.

**Ethics**: the standards of behavior to which we hold ourselves in our personal and professional lives. The operative phrase is *to which we hold ourselves*. The standards are chosen and maintained from inside, not imposed from outside.

**Compliance**: the extent to which a company's operations conform to applicable regulations and law. Compliance is the floor. The law sets the floor. Ethics is what you build on top of it — voluntarily, because you have decided the floor isn't sufficient.

**Integrity**: from the same Latin root as *integer*, meaning whole, undivided. A person of integrity is the same person on Monday and Saturday, with the auditor and without, in the boardroom and at the dinner table. The opposite of integrity is not dishonesty, exactly — it's fragmentation. Different ethics for different rooms.

One more term that will do heavy lifting throughout this book: **stakeholders**. These are the people who bear consequences from your decisions: clients, employees, suppliers, investors, customers, the surrounding community, the government, the environment. The list is concrete, not vague. *Stakeholder* is not a mood or a gesture toward abstract responsibility. It is a roster you can actually write down.

A practical test of whether you're doing ethics or doing public relations: a real stakeholder analysis names individuals or groups in writing and specifies how each would be affected. If you can't name them, you're not analyzing. You're performing.

## The cashier

Here is the smallest possible version of the question.

A cashier at a clothing store — call her J. — is standing behind the counter on a Tuesday afternoon. Her cousin walks in, picks out a hundred dollars of merchandise, and asks for J.'s employee discount: twenty-five percent off.

The store's written policy allows the discount on personal purchases. There is no policy explicitly forbidding it on a relative's purchase. The store's surveillance system doesn't capture the transaction in a way anyone will ever review. The transaction will save J.'s cousin twenty-five dollars. It will cost the store the same twenty-five dollars in margin.

What should J. do?

Notice what the question is *not* asking. It's not asking whether J. will be caught. She won't. It's not asking whether it's legal. It is. It's not asking whether a policy technically prohibits it. None does. Strip all of that away and what's left is the only question ethics has ever really asked: *given that you can, should you?*

Run J.'s decision through each of the three terms above.

*Ethics.* What standard does J. hold herself to? If her standard is "do nothing the audit would catch," she applies the discount without hesitation. If her standard is "act in a way I could explain to my manager without flinching," she pauses — and probably declines. The standard she has chosen, before the cousin walked through the door, is the answer.

*Compliance.* The store's written policy is silent on relatives. There's no legal violation. Compliance doesn't resolve the question. This is the entire point: compliance was never designed to.

*Integrity.* If J. applies the discount for her cousin on Tuesday, and on Wednesday asks a customer to honor the store's no-return policy on a discounted item, the two stances are inconsistent. She's asking customers to follow the spirit of a policy she just declined to follow herself. The fragmentation may be invisible to the customers. It is not invisible to J.

The discount is worth twenty-five dollars. The integrity — if she trades it — is worth substantially more than that. To her, to her relationship with her manager, to the person she is in the habit of being. The math isn't close. It only looks close because the twenty-five dollars is on the counter and the integrity isn't.

## Three lenses for one decision

Philosophy has been working on this problem for a long time, and it has produced three distinct frameworks worth carrying. Think of them as lenses — each one stares at the same decision from a different angle, and each sees something the others miss.

A decision has three parts. There is the *end* it produces — who benefits, who is harmed, in what amounts. There is the *means* by which it gets carried out — which duties are honored, which are violated. And there is the *character* of the person making it — what kind of person they are becoming by making this kind of decision, repeatedly, over a career. Three lenses, one for each part.

**Consequentialism — the lens of ends.** Sometimes called utilitarianism. The claim: an action is ethical if its consequences produce the greatest good for the greatest number. To apply it, ask: *who is helped, who is harmed, by how much, with what probability?* Add it up. Consequentialism is the language most business schools already speak — expected value, return on investment, net present value — extended beyond money to include human welfare. The trade-off it makes is that it doesn't ask whether the means used to reach a good outcome were themselves acceptable. A surgeon who harvested one healthy patient's organs to save five others would, on a strict utilitarian count, have done good. Most people have a strong intuition that the surgeon has done something deeply wrong. That intuition is where the next lens enters.

**Deontology — the lens of means.** From the Greek *deon*, meaning duty. Most associated with Immanuel Kant in the eighteenth century. The claim: certain duties — tell the truth, keep promises, don't use a person as a mere instrument of your ends — bind regardless of the consequences. The means lend moral weight to the ends; the ends do not launder the means. To apply it, ask: *what duties are owed to each person in this transaction, and does my proposed action honor or violate them?* The trade-off is the mirror of consequentialism: a strict deontologist refuses to lie even when a lie would prevent enormous suffering. The duty wins; the math loses. This is the language of contracts, professional codes, and fiduciary obligation.

**Virtue ethics — the lens of character.** Roots in the Greek tradition — Aristotle, the cardinal virtues of courage, justice, self-control, and practical wisdom, practiced as habits over a lifetime. The claim: ethical action flows from ethical character, and character is built by the routine practice of virtuous habits. To apply it, ask: *what kind of person — and what kind of organization — does this decision make me?* The trade-off is that this lens is slow. You cannot install courage in a bonus cycle. Character is built the way a cathedral is built: one stone at a time, over a horizon longer than any quarterly meeting. Its strength is also that slowness. A person of strong character doesn't have to *decide* to behave well in a crisis. The behavior is already there.

The honest move is to use all three, together. A decision that looks clean through one lens but ugly through the other two deserves re-examination. A decision that all three lenses reject is the easy case — and most genuine business-ethics failures are *not* easy cases. They are the cases where one lens approves and the others squirm.

Aristotle named the capacity to take a general principle and apply it well to a specific situation *phronesis* — practical wisdom. It's what separates someone who knows what utilitarianism says from someone who can actually use it to make a decision in a meeting that's running late. Knowing the frameworks is the prerequisite. Practical wisdom is what makes them work.

## The long game

Toyota began selling cars in the United States in 1957. For a long stretch, measured in years, Toyota accepted losses on each vehicle sold in America.

A Wall Street analyst in 1962 would have asked the obvious question: why is this company tolerating losses? The answer Toyota was actually following looked soft from the outside: it was buying a relationship of trust with what would become its U.S. customer base, and it was slowly dismantling an inherited assumption that Japanese manufactured goods were cheap and unreliable. Both purchases paid out — slowly, and then enormously — over the following decades.

This is the move the long game is built around. The thing you can't put on the balance sheet is often the thing whose presence or absence will, over a long enough horizon, determine the fate of the business.

**Long-term perspective** means evaluating a decision on the horizon that actually matters to the firm's life expectancy — years, not quarters. A decision whose costs land in the current quarter and whose benefits arrive over the next forty quarters will look bad on every horizon shorter than ten years and look good on every horizon longer. The horizon you choose determines the answer you get.

**Goodwill** is the value of a business beyond its tangible assets: its reputation, the loyalty of its customers, the attitude of its workforce, the strength of its brand. Goodwill can't be earned in a quarter. It also can't be entirely destroyed in a day — but it can be deeply damaged in one. September 7, 2017, was such a day for Equifax.

**The social contract** is the implicit agreement among members of a society to cooperate for mutual benefit. Applied to a business: in exchange for the privileges society grants — limited liability, access to courts, public infrastructure — the firm owes an obligation to act in ways that benefit the broader community, not only the shareholders.

The Nobel laureate Milton Friedman argued in 1970 that the only social responsibility of a business is to increase its profits. The position gave business schools a clean, measurable objective — shareholder value — and from the early 1980s through the mid-2000s it dominated boardrooms. The 2008 financial crisis began to erode it. In August 2019, 181 chief executives of the Business Roundtable — the trade association whose own governance principles had endorsed shareholder primacy since 1997 — signed a statement committing to the interests of customers, employees, suppliers, communities, *and* shareholders. Whether the signatures changed behavior is a fair question. That they changed the public language of corporate purpose is not.

Warren Buffett has summarized the long-game test in a single sentence: *do nothing you would not be happy to have an unfriendly but intelligent reporter write about on the front page of a newspaper.* His other sentence on the matter is sharper: *lose money for the firm, even a lot of money, and I will be understanding; lose reputation for the firm, even a shred of reputation, and I will be ruthless.* The asymmetry is the lesson. Money is recoverable. Reputation — on the time scale Buffett actually operates, which is decades — is not.

## Equifax, revisited

Return to those executives in late July 2017. The internal briefing has just happened. Imagine a very different conversation in that room.

*What was done.* Quietly sell the stock. Disclose the breach on September 7. Offer credit monitoring after the fact, under legal pressure. Watch the share price fall fourteen percent. Lose the chief executive within three weeks. Face hundreds of private lawsuits and multiple government investigations. Spend years of management time, and roughly a billion dollars, on the consequences.

*What could have been done.* Hold all insider stock sales — not as virtue, but as elementary legal hygiene. Disclose within forty-eight hours of the internal briefing. Offer free credit monitoring proactively. Replace the chief information officer voluntarily, as a credible signal of accountability. Brief key regulators before the press release, not after.

The financial cost of the second path is similar in the first ninety days. The cost trajectory diverges sharply afterward. The first path's costs compound through litigation, regulatory scrutiny, and destroyed trust. The second path's costs flatten, because the narrative — *they found this, they told us, here is what they are doing* — is partially recoverable.

The executives who chose the first path were not being economically rational. They were being rational on the wrong time scale: the next two months. On the time scale that actually mattered to the firm and its shareholders, the first path was the more expensive choice. They lost both the money and the reputation.

This is the pattern. Most business-ethics failures are not failures of values in opposition to economics. They are failures of *time horizon*: someone solving for the next ninety days when the firm's actual life is measured in decades.

## The Faris case

In early 2001, a sales executive named Mark Faris at a small networking-equipment reseller called Interlink Communications was staring at a revenue number that had dropped thirty percent. Interlink resold Cisco equipment; Cisco had begun steering its largest customers away from refurbished-equipment resellers. The revenue gap was real and growing.

Faris decided to make up the difference by lying.

The mechanism, once running, was simple. Cisco's SMARTnet service contracts promised advance hardware replacement when equipment failed. Faris began filing fraudulent SMARTnet claims — reporting failures that hadn't occurred, receiving replacement equipment Cisco wasn't obligated to send, then selling the replacement equipment to other customers. Between January 2001 and August 2002, he and his colleagues ran the scheme 140 times. The total fraud against Cisco was more than $400,000.

Federal prosecutors indicted him in 2008. He pleaded guilty to conspiracy to commit mail fraud, wire fraud, and money laundering. On July 31, 2009, he was sentenced to one year and one day. He served nearly nine months at the Duluth Federal Prison Camp and two months at a halfway house in Minneapolis. From the day his wife and two sons drove him to Duluth to the day he came home, he was away from his family for 318 days. He was ordered to pay $215,000 in restitution.

Run the decision through the three lenses.

*Consequentialist.* The benefit to Interlink: roughly $400,000 over twenty months, a stopgap against the revenue decline. The cost to Cisco: the same $400,000 in fraudulently obtained equipment. The cost to Faris personally: nine months in federal prison, two months in a halfway house, 318 days away from his sons, $215,000 in restitution, his career in the industry, and a felony record for the rest of his life. On a strict utilitarian count — adding up harms across all parties — the scheme is negative-sum from the moment it starts. The fact that several years passed before the sum was settled is a feature of how slowly the legal system clears its docket, not evidence that the scheme was ever positive-sum.

*Deontological.* Faris had a duty to deal honestly with Cisco. He had a duty not to use his position as a means to a fraudulent end. He had a duty to his own employees, who were now criminally complicit in his decision. The lens doesn't ask whether the consequences could have been worse; it asks whether the duty was honored. It was not.

*Virtue.* The question that bites, and that Faris himself now puts to corporate audiences as a public speaker, is this: *what kind of legacy do you want to leave for your kids?* The character that ran the SMARTnet scheme for twenty months is not the character he wanted his sons to inherit. Finding that out at the Duluth Federal Prison Camp is the worst possible moment to find it out.

And the long game? The time horizon Faris used in 2001 was the next quarter — *will the revenue gap close before someone notices the trajectory?* The horizon that actually mattered was the one on which Cisco's internal audit noticed, federal prosecutors took the case, the sentence ran through mid-2010, and the restitution ran through 2013. The reputational consequence — *Mark Faris, federal felon, mail fraud, wire fraud, money laundering* — is permanent in the sense that any future employer or business partner can find the docket.

After his release, Faris founded a small company called MPV Ethics and began speaking at universities and corporate trainings, frequently alongside Hank Shea, a law professor at the University of St. Thomas and a former federal prosecutor. He explains how a person who would have refused a $100,000 bribe in 2000 was, by 2002, calmly operating the scheme he'd once imagined he could never be drawn into. The story is not a redemption arc designed to flatter either teller or audience. It is a record of what the decay of integrity actually looks like from the inside — exception by exception, small accommodation by small accommodation — and what the recovery costs. Virtue ethics claims that character is built one habit at a time. It runs in both directions. Faris's two acts together are the most honest illustration of both halves.

## What this chapter asks you to carry forward

The three concepts in this chapter are not three separate tools. They are three views of one decision, and they agree more often than the cynical version of business culture would predict.

The Equifax executives' stock sales failed all three. They produced worse outcomes (consequentialism). They violated the duty of disclosure owed to investors and the public (deontology). They were the choices of an organization that, when the pressure was on, treated customers as a liability to be managed rather than people to be told the truth (virtue). And they depended on a time horizon — the next two months — that was always going to be shorter than the litigation and regulatory timelines that eventually closed in.

Decisions like this are not irrational. They are *short-horizon-rational* and long-horizon-irrational. The point of carrying all three lenses, together, is to be able to see the long horizon before the decision is made — not after.

The single idea that survives if you forget everything else: the right thing to do and the smart thing to do diverge only on short time scales. On any horizon long enough to matter to the life of the firm, measured in years rather than quarters, they converge. Most failures of business ethics are failures of horizon, not failures of values. Someone solved for the next ninety days when the firm's actual life expectancy was measured in decades.

The common mistake to watch for is the sophisticated-sounding shrug: *it depends, ethics is contextual, who are we to say.* This gets mistaken for humility. It isn't. Humility says *I might be wrong about what is right.* The shrug says *there is no right to be wrong about.* The first is a virtue. The second is the position of a person you cannot rationally trust with your money or your time.

## LLM Exercises

**Exercise 1.A — Stakeholder analysis.** Describe a real or realistic business decision involving an ethical dimension. Ask the model to identify all stakeholders affected, specify each stakeholder's interest, and predict the likely impact on each stakeholder of two different courses of action. Then ask the model to identify which lens — consequentialist, deontological, or virtue — each stakeholder's concern maps most naturally onto. Evaluate whether the model named stakeholders concretely or hid behind vague categories like "the community."

**Exercise 1.B — Legal vs. ethical.** Give the model a decision that is clearly legal and ask it to argue that the decision is also clearly ethical. Then ask it to argue the opposite — that the decision is legal but unethical. Evaluate the quality of both arguments. Does the model distinguish the legal from the ethical axis cleanly, or does it conflate them?

**Exercise 1.C — Three-lens triangulation.** Choose one of the three cases in this chapter — J. the cashier, Equifax, or Faris — and ask the model to apply all three ethical lenses to the decision. Ask it to specify: what each lens concludes, what each lens misses, and where the three lenses agree or disagree. Then ask: which lens is most persuasive in this case, and why? Push back if the model picks a lens without defending the choice.

**Exercise 1.D — The long game.** Ask the model to construct the "Option B" version of the Equifax case in detail — what the firm should have disclosed, when, to whom, in what form. Then ask the model to estimate, as specifically as possible, whether Option B would have been more or less expensive than what actually happened, accounting for litigation costs, regulatory fines, executive turnover, and long-term brand damage. Evaluate whether the model is able to reason about a long time horizon without reverting to vague claims about "reputation."

**Exercise 1.E — Integrity and fragmentation.** Ask the model to generate three examples of workplace situations where a person might have strong integrity in one domain and fragmented integrity in another — where the ethical behavior in one room doesn't match the ethical behavior in another. Then ask: what does the Faris case suggest about how fragmentation develops over time? Does it arrive as a dramatic choice, or as a sequence of small accommodations? Evaluate whether the model's account of fragmentation is mechanistic and specific, or generic and motivational.
