# Chapter 2 — Ethics from Antiquity to the Present

*The question is 2,400 years old. The tools for answering it are what's new.*

---

Here is a question. You are a manager at a banana company. Your firm runs plantations in Colombia. One afternoon a man comes to your office with a demand: pay us, or your workers on the ground will suffer for it. The man represents an armed group. You pay. A year later, the same man comes back. You pay again. You pay for seven years.

When does a victim become a participant?

That question is not new. Aristotle was asking a version of it in 335 BCE — not about Colombian banana companies, but about the decisions that form the character of a person or a city over time. He did not give a clean algorithmic answer, and neither will I. What I will give you is the four main tools that serious thinkers have built for getting at the answer, where each tool came from, what it was designed to fix, and where each one breaks. By the end of this chapter you will see that the banana company case is not a hard case at all — every major school of moral thought, on honest accounting, produces the same verdict. The interesting question is *why they agree*, because they arrive from completely different directions.

## The First Tool: Character

In Athens, around 335 BCE, Aristotle sets up a school at the Lyceum. He walks while he teaches — his school will later be called the Peripatetics, from the Greek word for walking. The big question he is working on is: *what is the good life, and how does a person get there?*

His answer is *eudaimonia*. English translates this as *happiness*, but that is a misleading translation. A happy afternoon is not *eudaimonia*. A happy decade is closer. A *flourishing life* is better. Aristotle is thinking about the full arc of a human being's time on earth, and his answer is that a flourishing life is produced by practiced virtue — by doing the right thing, repeatedly, until it becomes the kind of person you are.

The crucial word is *practiced*. Aristotle is not describing a feeling. He is describing a skill — the way musical ability or athletic ability is a skill. You become brave by doing brave things. You become generous by giving. You become just by making just decisions, again and again, until justice is not something you reason your way to but something you just *do*, the way a pianist does not reason their way to the next chord.

The master virtue in Aristotle's system is *phronesis* — practical wisdom. Practical wisdom is what lets you take a general principle and apply it correctly in a specific situation. Knowing that honesty is good does not tell you what to say, in this room, to this person, about this thing. *Phronesis* does. It is the virtue that makes the other virtues useful.

Around 500 BCE — a century before Aristotle, eight thousand kilometers east — a teacher named Confucius is asking something structurally identical. The Zhou dynasty has been disintegrating for centuries. The Warring States period is violent, the social fabric frayed, the inherited expectations about who owes what to whom no longer holding. Confucius, like Aristotle, thinks the repair starts with the individual — with the cultivation of the *junzi*, the gracious, cultivated person who has developed the habits of right conduct.

Here is the structural difference worth pausing on: Aristotle locates the practice of virtue in the *individual*. Confucius locates it in *relationships*. The five great relationships — parent and child, husband and wife, elder and younger sibling, master and apprentice, ruler and subject — are the actual school of character in Confucius's system. Three of those five relationships live inside a household. Confucius is saying, with a seriousness that should not be lost, that you build the character that will serve you at the negotiating table by the way you treat your siblings at the dinner table, for decades, before the negotiating table ever comes.

Confucius's counterpart to Aristotelian *phronesis* is *quan* — sometimes translated as expediency, better understood as situational judgment, the sensitivity to what the particular case actually calls for. The word originally referred to the counterweight on a scale. *Quan* is what you use to know how much weight to put on which principle, in this case, with these people, right now.

What the two systems share is the deepest commitment: ethics is not a calculation you run. It is a thing you become. The unit of measurement is not the transaction but the character. And character is the cumulative product of a thousand small decisions, each one either building or eroding the kind of person you are.

Now go back to the banana company. *Phronesis* asks: what is the courageous action — not in the abstract, but in this specific situation, with these specific stakes? The first payment, under direct threat to workers' lives, is at least defensible. But by the seventh year, by the seven-hundredth payment, *phronesis* is asking a different question. *What kind of organization have we become?* The Aristotelian indictment is not about a bad decision. It is about a character formed by hundreds of small ones.

## The Second Tool: Consequences

In London, on June 6, 1832, the philosopher Jeremy Bentham — recently dead at eighty-four — is dissected in front of medical students, as he requested. His skeleton is then reassembled, dressed in his own clothes, fitted with a wax head (the actual head having mummified badly), and placed in a glass case at University College London, which he helped found. The display is still there. Bentham called it his *auto-icon* — his self-image, his last argument. The body is a body. The skeleton is a skeleton. The questions that seem holy are, on close inspection, soluble.

![Bentham's final polemic: the body is a body. Ethics is a calculation, not a mystery.](images/02-ethics-from-antiquity-to-the-present-fig-02.png)
*Figure 2.2 — Photograph of Bentham's auto-icon as displayed at UCL*

The framework Bentham proposed for solving them is utilitarianism. Its claim: an action is right if it produces more utility — more net well-being, summed across everyone affected — than any available alternative. The calculating unit is utility. The scope is universal: everyone's well-being counts equally, regardless of relationship or proximity or social standing. The procedure is rational: you can reason about it, add it up, compare alternatives.

The radical move is what this eliminates. *Character is irrelevant.* *Motive is irrelevant.* *Tradition is irrelevant.* The only morally relevant fact is the consequence of an action, measured across all parties affected. Technical name: consequentialism.

John Stuart Mill, Bentham's intellectual successor, kept the machine but worried about what it could be made to justify. Mill introduced two modifications that matter for business ethics.

First, not all utility is equal. Bentham treated all pleasure as equivalent — a children's game was as valuable as poetry if it produced equal enjoyment. Mill rejected this. Some pleasures are higher than others. *Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.* The aggregate measure stays; the units get weighted by quality.

Second, and more important: Mill's harm principle, stated in *On Liberty* in 1859. The only purpose for which power can rightfully be exercised over a person, against their will, is to prevent harm to others. *Their own good* — even the greater happiness of the majority — is not sufficient warrant for coercing a non-harming individual. Mill is smuggling a rights-based constraint back into a consequentialist framework. He is saying: the mob that tortures one innocent person for the entertainment of a thousand is not, in fact, doing the utilitarian calculus correctly. The person has a claim that the calculus cannot override.

Here is what I want you to sit with. Bentham was building utilitarianism in the early industrial period, when the decisions being made — about factory conditions, about colonial policy, about public health — were affecting hundreds of thousands of people who could not possibly all be consulted. Virtue ethics works well in a community where you know most of the people affected by your decisions. It does not scale to a textile mill in Manchester that employs three thousand workers whose names the owner does not know. What scales is a calculation procedure. Bentham was not being cold. He was being practical about a world that had suddenly gotten much larger.

The weakness is the one that Mill saw and tried to patch. Pure consequentialism has no answer to the surgeon who saves five patients by harvesting the organs of one healthy person. The arithmetic is positive. The human case is monstrous. Every serious defender of utilitarianism eventually invokes something that looks like a rights-based constraint — Mill's harm principle, rule-utilitarianism, indirect utilitarianism — to block the organ-harvesting conclusion. And every time they do, they are borrowing from the next school on our list.

Back to the banana company. Run a full utilitarian accounting: the lives protected by the payments go on the benefit ledger. Against them: armed groups directly funded, Colombian civilians killed by those groups, the corroding effect of a functioning extortion model on an already fragile state, the eventual implosion of the firm's credibility. On full accounting, on a sufficiently long time horizon, the seven-year strategy is net-negative. It was net-positive only on a short horizon and a narrow frame — counting workers saved but not counting everything downstream.

## The Third Tool: Duty

In Königsberg, in 1785, a fifty-seven-year-old professor publishes a short book called *Groundwork of the Metaphysic of Morals*. The professor is Immanuel Kant. The book is not easy to read. What it contains, once you get there, is a principle for moral reasoning that does not ask about consequences at all.

Kant's principle is the categorical imperative. *Categorical* because it is unconditional — it holds regardless of outcomes. *Imperative* because it commands. The core formulation: act only on a rule that you could, at the same time, rationally will to be a rule for everyone in similar circumstances.

The test is universalization. Before you take an action, ask: what rule am I following? And then ask: what happens if everyone follows this rule?

Kant's illustration is the lying promise. You need money. You consider borrowing from a friend with a promise to repay that you have no intention of keeping. The rule you are following: *make false promises when convenient*. Universalize it: imagine everyone follows this rule. The institution of promising collapses — no one would believe a promise — and the rule defeats itself. You could not rationally will this state of affairs. Therefore the action is wrong.

Notice what this test does not ask. It does not ask whether you will be caught. It does not ask whether the friend can spare the money. It does not ask about net utility. It asks only: is the rule self-consistent when generalized? If not, the action fails the test.

There is a second formulation of the categorical imperative that is often cleaner to apply in practice: act so as to treat humanity — in your own person or in the person of anyone else — always as an end, and never merely as a means. The lying friend-borrower treats the friend as a tool for obtaining money, not as a person with their own claims. The organ-harvesting surgeon treats the healthy patient as a means to save five others. These actions fail the test independently of what consequences they produce.

In 2016, Samsung releases the Galaxy Note 7. Within weeks, phones are catching fire. The cause is a faulty battery pushed to market under pressure to beat Apple's iPhone 7 launch. Samsung's first response is to deny and blame suppliers — a classically consequentialist response, in the sense that the expected cost of admitting fault looks, in the short-term accounting, worse than the expected cost of delay. The framework of consequences, run on a short time horizon with incomplete information, gives Samsung's communications team cover to wait.

Samsung's second response — after the denials fail and the reports compound — is to recall two million phones, replace them, then recall the replacements when those also fail, and finally pull the product entirely. The financial cost is in the billions. The Kantian reading of this sequence is not that Samsung became virtuous; it is that Samsung eventually stopped treating customers as a variable in the cost-benefit calculation and started treating the duty of non-harm as unconditional. Whether Samsung's executives framed it that way or not, the structure of the decision is Kantian. The duty holds regardless of the quarterly number.

## The Fourth Tool: Fairness

In Cambridge, Massachusetts, in 1971, a political philosopher named John Rawls publishes *A Theory of Justice*. It re-orders the field. It has not been undone in the half-century since.

Rawls's project is to fill a gap. Virtue ethics tells you how to form your character; it does not tell you how to design a just institution. Utilitarianism tells you how to maximize aggregate well-being; it does not protect individuals from being sacrificed for the aggregate. Kant tells you what duties you hold; it does not tell you how cooperative gains should be distributed. Rawls offers a procedure for distribution.

The procedure has two pieces.

The first is the *original position*: imagine a group of rational people designing the rules of their society from scratch, before anyone knows what their place in that society will be.

The second is the *veil of ignorance*: in the original position, no one knows their age, sex, ethnicity, education, income, native talent, or social status. They are designing the rules without knowing whose life they will live inside those rules.

Rawls's argument: behind the veil, rational self-interested deliberators would choose rules that protect the least advantaged — because no deliberator can rule out that they will end up among the least advantaged. The principle of *justice as fairness* is not imposed from outside; it is what every rational person would choose if they genuinely did not know which person they were going to be.

![Rawls's contribution is the procedure: design the rule before you know which side of it you'll be on.](images/02-ethics-from-antiquity-to-the-present-fig-04.png)
*Figure 2.4 — Two-stage diagram of the veil-of-ignorance procedure*

Apply this to a real business case. A multinational firm employs workers at two facilities — one in a high-cost-of-living country, one in a low-cost-of-living country. The firm's executives, all of whom live in the high-cost-of-living country, are deciding whether to close the first facility and shift production entirely to the second.

Veil of ignorance: you are about to be one of the people affected by this decision. You do not know which one. You might be an executive. You might be the worker in the facility being closed. You might be a worker in the surviving facility who will absorb additional output without proportional wage increase. Knowing that you might be any of these people — what rules do you choose for how the decision gets made?

Not *maximize total profit*. Because you might be the one whose job disappears. Something more like: if production shifts, displaced workers receive substantial transition support; the wage differential between facilities narrows over time; the decision is made with input from affected parties, not announced from a distance. The exact rules are negotiable. The structure of the procedure is what Rawls contributes: design the rule from behind the veil, and the rule becomes humane. Design it from inside your own position, and it optimizes for you.

## Why They All Agree

Here is what is strange about the banana company case. Four schools of moral thought, arriving from completely different starting assumptions, using completely different methods, converge on the same verdict.

Aristotelian virtue: by year seven, the organization's character has been formed by hundreds of payments. The habit of paying is the character now. *Phronesis* would have identified this trajectory much earlier.

Confucian relational ethics: the company has broken its relationship with the surrounding community by becoming — even as a coerced participant — a funder of the community's violence. Restoring right relation is a project that money alone cannot accomplish.

Utilitarian consequentialism: on full accounting, on an honest time horizon, the payments fund more harm than they prevent. The short-term worker-protection benefit is real. The long-term structural harm is larger.

Kantian deontology: universalize the rule *fund armed groups if they threaten your employees*. The world produced by this rule universalized is one in which armed groups proliferate, because threatening multinationals has been demonstrated to be a profitable business model. The rule is self-defeating. It fails the categorical imperative test.

Rawlsian fairness: no rational deliberator, behind the veil of ignorance, would design a regime of multinational operation in conflict-affected states that permits quiet payments to armed groups. The deliberator might be the Colombian farmer whose family is killed by the funded militia. The veil produces disclosure requirements, mandatory withdrawal conditions, and international coordination mechanisms — roughly what international law has been moving toward, slowly, since.

In 2007, Chiquita Brands International pleads guilty to terrorism financing violations and pays a $25 million fine. Colombian families file suit under the Alien Tort Claims Act alleging complicity in extrajudicial killings, torture, forced disappearances, and crimes against humanity. The legal proceedings were partial. The moral verdict, from four independent traditions, was not.

When all four major schools of moral philosophy produce the same answer, the question is no longer really philosophical. It is a question of organizational courage — of whether the people inside the firm, over those seven years, had the *phronesis* to see what the habit was building, the utilitarian honesty to count the downstream effects, the Kantian steadiness to apply the test even when the quarterly number argued against it, and the Rawlsian imagination to ask what rule they would have chosen if they might have been on the other side.

## What Survives Translation

One more question worth sitting with: is there a universal business ethic? Is there something all four traditions agree on that holds across cultures, or is ethics ultimately local?

The honest answer is: yes, on a thin core, and no, on the thick details.

The thin core survives translation:

- Honest dealing with parties to a transaction. Aristotle calls this *commutative justice*. Confucius calls it *zhōngxìn* — wholehearted sincerity. Bentham would call it the precondition of any meaningful utility calculation. Kant calls it a categorical duty. Rawls derives it from the original position.
- Some form of fair distribution of cooperative gains. Every tradition has a version of this.
- A constraint against treating other parties as instruments only. The most explicitly Kantian of the four formulations, but echoed in all the others.

The thick details — what counts as *fair*, who is owed special duties, when an inequality is acceptable — are culturally specific and contested even within cultures. A practitioner operating across borders needs both: the thin core as a non-negotiable commitment, and the cultural literacy to recognize how the thick details vary in the specific places where the firm operates.
