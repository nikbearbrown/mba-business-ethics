# Ethics from Antiquity to the Present

In a colonnade in Athens, around 335 BCE, an old man paces while he teaches. He has recently returned from twenty years away — most of them spent tutoring the boy who would become Alexander the Great — to set up a school of his own at the Lyceum. His students walk with him as he talks. They are working through a question that, two and a half thousand years later, every business-ethics seminar in the world will still be working through: *what is the good — and how does a person, or a firm, know whether they are pursuing it?*

The man is Aristotle. He will write down the answer in a book called the *Nicomachean Ethics*. The answer will not settle the question. The question is not, in fact, settleable — every century since has produced a serious thinker who reformulated it from scratch. What Aristotle did do is start a conversation. The chapters of that conversation are the schools we use today: virtue ethics, utilitarianism, deontology, contract theory. Each one was someone's attempt to fix what they saw as the previous answer's blind spot. Each one is, in present-tense business practice, a tool you can pick up.

Chapter 1 introduced three lenses for examining a decision and treated each as if it had arrived intact from the philosophy seminar room. They did not. Each survives because it has answered serious objections from serious critics for a long time. This chapter is about the conversation that produced them.

The student should notice the long quiet middle — and the explosion of moral-philosophical reframing during and after the Industrial Revolution.]

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Compare** Aristotelian and Confucian virtue ethics and **identify** the structural difference between an individual-centered and a family-centered ethical system.
- **Trace** the move from virtue ethics to utilitarianism and **explain** what Bentham and Mill were trying to fix.
- **Apply** Kant's categorical imperative to a contemporary business decision and **distinguish** what it asks from what utilitarianism asks.
- **Use** Rawls's veil of ignorance as a procedural test on a real policy choice.
- **Argue** for or against the claim that there is a universal business ethic that survives translation across cultures.

## Prerequisites

You need only Chapter 1's three lenses and the willingness to take seriously that thinkers separated by twenty-five centuries can still talk to one another. The conversation continues. You are entering it mid-sentence.

## Why this chapter matters

Each of the four lenses already in your toolkit — consequences, duties, character, and the contractual frame that holds them together — has a *form* it has today because of objections raised by specific named people in specific dated arguments. Knowing those arguments tells you which parts of each lens are load-bearing and which are stylistic. When a colleague invokes "the greatest good for the greatest number" without realizing that exactly two centuries of careful objection have refined that phrase into something quite different from what Bentham first wrote, you will know what is missing from their account. That is not a parlor trick. It is what stops you from making business-ethics decisions on the basis of a half-remembered slogan.

## Concept 1: Virtue traditions — character first

A different scene, again ancient, but eight thousand kilometers east. In what is now the Shandong province of China, around 500 BCE, a teacher named Kung Fu Tzu — Master Kung, whom European scholars two millennia later will Latinize to *Confucius* — is walking the same kind of teaching walk. The Zhou dynasty has been disintegrating for centuries. The era is called the Warring States period and it deserves the name: violence is constant, the social order has frayed, the previously stable expectations of who owes what to whom no longer hold. Confucius, like Aristotle a century later, is trying to reconstruct the social fabric by working from a small piece of it: a single person of good character, in right relation to the people around them.

Two thinkers, separated by a hundred years and a continent, are arriving at the same answer to the question of what the good is. The answer is: *virtue*. Not virtue as a list of pieties — virtue as a set of *practiced habits*, built one decision at a time, that become character. Both will say the same thing in different words: a person of good character does the right thing not because they reason it out but because they have made themselves the kind of person for whom the right thing is the natural one. The reasoning happens upstream, over years, in the formation. The action, when the moment comes, is fast.

### The mechanism: virtue as habituated character

Aristotle's word for the goal of virtuous activity is *eudaimonia* — sometimes translated as *happiness*, more accurately rendered as *flourishing*. It is not a feeling, the way *happy* is in English. It is a sustained mode of living a complete life — and a life is the unit of measurement. *One swallow does not make a summer*, Aristotle writes; one good day does not make a flourishing life. A flourishing life is the cumulative product of years of practiced virtue.

The virtues themselves are habits. Two kinds: *intellectual* virtues, acquired through learning, and *moral* virtues, acquired through practice. The moral list includes courage, self-control, liberality, magnificence, honor, patience, amiability. Each is the *golden mean* between two failure modes — courage between cowardice and recklessness, generosity between stinginess and prodigality. The doctrine of the mean is not "do everything in moderation"; it is the more demanding claim that for each virtue, there is a *correct* amount, calibrated to the situation, and finding it is the work of a lifetime.

Sitting on top of all of these is one master virtue — *phronesis*, practical wisdom. *Phronesis* is what allows a person to take general principles and apply them well in a specific case. It is the virtue Aristotle considers most important, because all the others are useless without it. Knowing that courage is good does not tell you, in this room, with this customer, with these stakes, what the courageous action is. *Phronesis* does.

Confucius arrives at a structurally similar picture by a different route. His *junzi* — a person who is gracious, magnanimous, and cultured — is the East Asian counterpart to the Aristotelian virtuous person. The path to becoming one is the same: practice. But Confucius locates the unit of practice not in the individual but in the *family*. The five great relationships — parent and child, husband and wife, elder and younger sibling, master and apprentice, ruler and subject — are the school in which character is formed. Three of those five live inside a household. The point is not metaphorical. Confucius is saying that you become a person of good character by the way you behave at the dinner table, with your siblings, with your parents, year after year, before you ever sit down at a negotiating table.

The two systems also share a concept that is structurally identical and named differently. Aristotle's *phronesis* — practical wisdom — has a Confucian sibling in *quan*, which translates roughly as *expediency* but is more carefully understood as the practical consideration of relative rightness in a moral dilemma. The word *quan* originally referred to a piece of metal used to balance a scale. It is the virtue that lets you know, in this case, with these people, how much weight to put on which principle.

The student should notice that the systems are not identical — but that both treat ethics as a thing one *becomes* rather than a thing one *calculates*.]

### The trade-off: virtue is durable but slow

The virtue traditions, East and West, optimize for the same thing: durable, deeply formed character that does not need to be re-derived in every situation. The cost is time. You cannot install courage by reading a memo. You cannot become *junzi* by attending a seminar. The trade-off is that what these systems produce is what you actually want when the pressure is on — a person who behaves well *automatically*, without having to consult a flowchart — but the production timeline is measured in decades.

There is also a structural cost to both systems that contemporary critics rightly press on. Aristotle defended slavery as economically necessary. Confucius's family-relationship hierarchy assumes a stratified society in which juniors defer to elders, wives to husbands, subjects to rulers — a hierarchy whose modern reception is more critical than its ancient one. The systems work, in the form their authors handed down, only inside the social structures of their time. The harder question — *what survives translation, and what doesn't?* — is alive in business ethics today and will return in §Concept 4 of this chapter.

### Worked example: Chiquita Brands International, Colombia, 1997–2004

Sometime in 1997, a senior manager at Chiquita Brands International — an American produce conglomerate that grows and ships bananas, among other crops, from plantations across Latin America — receives a delivery to a Colombian banana plantation. The delivery is not produce. It is a demand for *protection money* from one of two armed groups operating in the region: a right-wing paramilitary or a left-wing Marxist guerrilla force, depending on which week and which plantation. Refusal will, on plausible warning, cost the lives of Chiquita employees on the ground. Compliance will fund armed groups that the United States designates as terrorist organizations.

Between 1997 and 2004, Chiquita pays. The payments total millions of dollars over seven years. The company will later describe itself as a victim of extortion — the choice, in the company's framing, was money or workers' lives.

Run this through Aristotle's virtue lens. *Phronesis* asks: what would a person of good character do, in this specific situation, with these specific stakes? The answer is contested, and the source materials are honest about that — there is a real moral question here, not a manufactured one. But notice what virtue ethics *demands* of the manager: not a calculation of best outcome, but a self-examination. *What kind of company are we becoming, by making this payment, the second time, and the third, and the seven hundredth?* The first payment may be defensible as protection of life. By the seven hundredth payment, the company is no longer a producer paying tribute; it is, in operational fact, a participant in the violence its money sustains.

Run it through Confucius's relational lens. The relationships Chiquita owes duty to are layered: workers on the plantation, customers in U.S. supermarkets, shareholders, the Colombian state, the surrounding community. The relational frame asks: *whose duty are you discharging by writing this check, and whose duty are you breaching?* Workers' lives are protected. Customers' implicit trust — that their fruit is not bought with terrorism financing — is broken. The community's expectation that a foreign producer will be a stabilizing presence is broken in a different and worse way.

In 2007, Chiquita pleads guilty to violations of the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act and pays a $25 million fine. Colombian families, also in 2007, file suit under the Alien Tort Claims Act alleging the company's complicity in extrajudicial killings, torture, forced disappearances, and crimes against humanity. The Supreme Court declines to hear the case in 2015. The legal proceedings are partial. The character question is not — by 2004, the company that had once been Chiquita the producer was, on the virtue ethicist's reading, Chiquita the seven-year accomplice, whether or not it had intended that name.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that virtue ethics is a soft, vague system because it does not produce a numerical answer. It does not produce a numerical answer because it is asking a different question. *What kind of person should I be* is a different question than *what action will produce the best outcome*, and the reason philosophers keep returning to virtue ethics is that the first question turns out to be the more useful one in cases where the situation is unfamiliar enough that the rules are not yet written.

The second misconception is that virtue ethics is intrinsically conservative — that because it asks you to imitate exemplars, it cannot accommodate change. It can. The exemplars themselves change as the practice of virtue is extended into new domains. Aristotle's exemplars did not include women or non-Athenians. The contemporary practitioner's do. Practice grows the tradition.

## Concept 2: The calculation turn — Bentham, Mill, and what consequences cost

In London on June 6, 1832, the body of the philosopher Jeremy Bentham — recently dead at age eighty-four — is dissected in front of medical students at the new University College London, an institution he helped found and to which he has bequeathed the bulk of his estate, on one condition. After dissection, his skeleton is to be reassembled, dressed in his own clothes, fitted with a wax head (the actual head having mummified poorly), seated in a glass case, and displayed for posterity. The display is what Bentham called his *auto-icon* — his self-image, his statement that the body that hosts a thinking life has no special standing worth burying in a churchyard. It is still on display at UCL today.

The auto-icon is a piece of polemic. Bentham was, among many other things, the most aggressive proponent of the eighteenth-century English Enlightenment's commitment to materialism: the view that ethical questions are *not* mysterious, that the right framework will resolve them by calculation, and that what makes ethics seem hard is that previous philosophers were sentimental. The display of his preserved body is the argument-by-spectacle. *The body is a body. The skeleton is a skeleton. The questions you thought were holy are, on close inspection, soluble.*

The framework Bentham proposed for solving them is utilitarianism. Its claim is famous in the form Bentham wrote it: *the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people*. An action is right if it produces more happiness — or, more precisely, more *utility*, measured in units Bentham called *utils* — than any available alternative. Utility was to be calculated by what Bentham called the *hedonic calculus*, attending to four properties of any consequence: its intensity, its duration, its certainty, and the probability that it would actually occur. The framework is universal (everyone counts), objective (no one's happiness is intrinsically more valuable than anyone else's), rational (it can be reasoned about), and quantifiable (it produces, in principle, a number).

### The mechanism: consequences as the unit of analysis

The radical move in Bentham's framework is what it eliminates. *Character is irrelevant*. *Motive is irrelevant*. *Tradition is irrelevant*. The only thing that matters is the *consequence* of an action — measured across all parties affected, with no one given special weight. The technical name for this position is *consequentialism*: actions are judged by their consequences alone.

Bentham was unapologetic about how much of the existing moral vocabulary this swept away. He famously called the language of natural rights — the rhetoric of the American and French revolutions, of the *Declaration of the Rights of Man* — *nonsense upon stilts*. In Bentham's view, you could not do ethics by appealing to abstract rights that had no measurable cash value in human happiness. You did ethics by adding up consequences. Anything else was sentiment dressed as principle.

His protégé, John Stuart Mill, agreed in spirit but worried about the implementation. Mill — born 1806, raised by his father James Mill on a relentless, custom-designed program of education that had him reading Greek at three and Plato at seven — eventually arrived at a position that softened Bentham's hard edge. Mill kept the consequentialist machinery but introduced two crucial modifications.

The first was that not all utility is created equal. Bentham had treated all happiness as equivalent — *pushpin* (a children's game) was as valuable as poetry if it produced equal pleasure. Mill rejected this. Some pleasures, he argued, are higher than others. *Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied.* The aggregate measure stays, but the units are weighted by quality, not just quantity.

The second modification is the more important one for business ethics. In *On Liberty*, published in 1859, Mill articulated what is now called the *harm principle*:

> *"The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant."*

The harm principle smuggles a deontological commitment back into a consequentialist framework. It says: there is one thing — the prevention of harm — that justifies coercion, and nothing else does. *His own good* is not enough. *The greater happiness of the majority* is not enough either, if it requires coercing a non-harming individual against their will. Mill is keeping consequentialism's framework while protecting individuals from its most aggressive application: the lynch mob that produces net happiness by sacrificing one.

### The trade-off: scalable and objective, but ignores rights and dignity

Utilitarianism's strength is that it produces a procedure. Cost-benefit analysis is utilitarianism in operation. Insurance pricing is utilitarianism in operation. Every decision a public-health official makes about which intervention to fund is, formally, a utilitarian calculation. The framework scales. It works in an industrial society where the people affected by a decision are too numerous to consult and the stakes are too high to leave to character.

Its weakness is the lynch-mob problem. Pure consequentialism, applied without Mill's harm-principle correction, has no answer to the surgeon who saves five patients by harvesting one healthy person's organs. The numerical case is positive; the human case is monstrous. Bentham would have shrugged. Mill would have invoked the harm principle. Most thoughtful contemporary practitioners do something in between — they use consequentialist reasoning when the stakes are large and the affected parties are numerous, and they refuse to use it when applying it would require treating someone as a means to a greater end. *That refusal* — that there are decisions consequentialism cannot make — is what brings us to Kant.

### Worked example: a streetcar, a switch, and one decision

The best-known application of consequentialist reasoning is a thought experiment originally constructed by the philosopher Philippa Foot in 1967, though variants of it predate her formulation. A runaway streetcar is hurtling toward five workers on the track. You stand at a switch. Throwing the switch will divert the streetcar onto a side track where one worker stands. Five live or one lives — your choice.

The strict utilitarian answer is to throw the switch. Five lives are saved. One is lost. The hedonic calculus is unambiguous.

What makes the experiment hard is the second variant. Same setup, but instead of a switch, you stand on a footbridge over the track next to a large stranger. Pushing the stranger off the bridge will stop the streetcar. Five live or one lives — your choice. The arithmetic is identical. The intuition is sharply different. Most people who would throw the switch will not push the stranger.

The utilitarian framework cannot explain the difference, because the framework only counts outcomes. The deontological framework, which we will reach in a moment, can. The reason this thought experiment is in every introduction-to-ethics curriculum is that it makes consequentialism's blind spot visible. *There is something about how the action is done* — about whether you are using the stranger as a means to save five strangers, or merely re-routing a vehicle — that the lens of consequences cannot see. You can throw the switch and still be a person who does not push strangers off bridges. The utilitarian ledger does not record this distinction. The next concept's lens is built specifically to record it.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that utilitarianism is a *cold* or *unfeeling* framework. It is not. It is universal in its concern — every affected person counts equally, regardless of relationship, status, or proximity. The frequent intuition that it is cold is actually the intuition that it does not give *special* weight to the people we feel close to. That is a feature, not a bug, in many domains (public-health policy, institutional design, charitable allocation). It becomes a bug when the situation involves duties of fidelity that cannot be averaged across strangers.

The second misconception is that *cost-benefit analysis* is a different thing from utilitarianism. It is not. Cost-benefit analysis is utilitarianism made operational, with quantifiable units of utility (usually monetary) and a procedure for adding them. When a public agency or a corporation does cost-benefit analysis, it is doing a Benthamite calculation, whether or not the analysts have ever read Bentham.

## Concept 3: The duty turn and the fairness turn — Kant's no, Rawls's procedure

In Königsberg, in what was then East Prussia, in 1781, a fifty-seven-year-old professor of logic and metaphysics publishes a book that runs about eight hundred pages of dense argument. The book is the *Critique of Pure Reason* — *Kritik der Reinen Vernunft* — and the professor is Immanuel Kant. Up to this point, Kant has been an obscure provincial academic in a city most Europeans had never heard of. Within a decade, he will be the most discussed philosopher in Europe, and the *Critique* will be the canonical statement of a philosophical position — call it *transcendental idealism* — that effectively ends the long-running fight between European rationalists and British empiricists by changing the question both sides had been asking.

What concerns us here is not the *Critique of Pure Reason* but its companion, *Groundwork of the Metaphysic of Morals* (1785), and the principle Kant articulated there. Where Bentham was building utilitarianism out of the empirical materials of consequence and pleasure, Kant was building deontology — *deon*, recall, is Greek for duty — out of structures Kant believed lived *prior* to experience, in the architecture of reason itself.

### The mechanism: act on a maxim you could universalize

Kant's central principle has a name that sounds like a piece of legal jargon and is actually a precise philosophical commitment: the *categorical imperative*. *Categorical* because it is unconditional — it applies regardless of the consequences. *Imperative* because it commands. The full statement, from the *Groundwork*, is:

> *"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law."*

Pause on this. The principle says: before you take an action, ask whether the rule you are following — the *maxim* of your action — is one you could rationally will to be a rule for *everyone* in similar circumstances, including everyone who might one day be on the receiving end of the action. If the answer is no, the action is wrong, regardless of how good its consequences turn out to be.

Kant's stock illustration is the lying promise. Suppose you need money. You consider borrowing from a friend with a promise to repay that you have no intention of keeping. The maxim of the action is: *make false promises to obtain money when convenient*. Now universalize: imagine everyone, in similar circumstances, made false promises. The institution of promising would collapse. No one would believe a promise; the maxim would defeat itself. The action fails the categorical-imperative test, and Kant says it fails on the test alone — independent of whether you would actually be caught, or whether you actually need the money, or whether the friend can spare it.

A second formulation of the imperative, sometimes called the *humanity formulation*, makes the same demand from a different angle: *act so as to treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always as an end and never as a means only*. The streetcar pusher fails this test. The harvest-the-organs surgeon fails this test. The fraudster of Chapter 1 fails this test, because the customer to whom the fraudulent product is sold is being used as a means to the firm's revenue and not honored as an end with their own claims to truthful information.

### The trade-off: principled but rigid

The strength of Kant's framework is what it refuses. It refuses to treat people as means. It refuses to be optimized away by clever reasoning about consequences. It refuses to permit the lynch-mob outcome that pure consequentialism cannot block. It is, in the modern phrase, *principled* in a way utilitarianism cannot be.

Its weakness is its rigidity. Kant himself wrote, controversially, that it would be wrong to lie even to a murderer who came to your door asking where his intended victim was hiding — because lying violates the duty of truth-telling, which is universalizable, and the consequences of the lie do not enter the moral calculation. Most readers find this conclusion unacceptable, and several generations of Kant scholars have produced subtler interpretations to soften it. But the difficulty is real and is the cost of the framework. Kant pays in operational flexibility for what he buys in principled refusal of the worst utilitarian outcomes.

### Worked example: Samsung's Galaxy Note 7, August 2016

In August 2016, Samsung Electronics releases its Galaxy Note 7 smartphone, an aggressive move timed to beat Apple's iPhone 7 launch later that fall. Within weeks, reports begin to surface of phones spontaneously catching fire while charging. The cause is eventually traced to faulty batteries — and to the corner-cutting that the iPhone-launch-window pressure had produced inside Samsung's supply chain.

Samsung's first response is the consequentialist response: deny, blame suppliers, push the problem outside the firm. This is the kind of move utilitarian reasoning gives cover to — *the expected cost of admitting fault now is greater than the expected cost of denying and hoping the issue contains itself*. Inside the consequentialist framework, the calculation is at least defensible. The framework does not ask whether the move treats customers as ends or means.

Samsung's second response — after the initial denial fails and the reports compound — is the Kantian response. The company recalls more than two million phones, replaces them, then recalls the replacements when those also fail, and finally takes the entire product off the market. The financial cost is in the billions. The reputational cost is significant in the short run and far smaller in the long run than the cost of having continued to deny. The shift is not framed by Samsung in Kantian language, but its structure is exactly Kantian: the firm stops asking *what consequence does this maximize* and starts asking *what duty do we owe the customer who put their phone next to their head while it slept on the bedside table*. The duty is the duty of non-harm and of truthful disclosure. Honoring it is more expensive in the next quarter and far cheaper in the next decade — the Concept 3 long-game point of Chapter 1, restated in deontological vocabulary.

### Rawls and the fairness turn

A century and a half after Kant, in his Cambridge office at Harvard, the political philosopher John Rawls publishes a book called *A Theory of Justice*. The year is 1971. The book runs about six hundred pages and immediately re-orders Anglophone moral philosophy in a way that has not been undone in the half-century since.

Rawls's project is not to replace either Kant or utilitarianism but to fix what he sees as their shared gap: neither framework gives a procedure for *distribution* — for working out how the goods produced by a cooperative society should be allocated. Utilitarianism's answer (whatever maximizes total utility) permits intolerable inequality. Kant's answer (treat people as ends) is silent on what each person is owed in concrete material terms. Rawls offers a procedure that he claims would, if rationally followed, generate the right answer for any society.

The procedure has two pieces. The first is the *original position* — a hypothetical situation in which rational deliberators are designing the rules of their society from scratch. The second is the *veil of ignorance* — the constraint that, in the original position, no deliberator knows what their position in the resulting society will be. They do not know their age, sex, ethnicity, education level, income, physical attractiveness, native talents, or social status. They are designing the rules without knowing whose life they will live inside those rules.

Rawls's argument is that, behind the veil of ignorance, rational self-interested deliberators would converge on a small set of principles — including, crucially, that any inequalities permitted in the resulting society must be arranged so as to benefit the *least advantaged*, because none of the deliberators can rule out that they themselves will end up among the least advantaged. The principle of *justice as fairness* is not a principle imposed from outside; it is the principle every rational person would choose for themselves if they did not know which person they would be.

### Worked example: applying the veil to a real business decision

Consider a familiar business case. A multinational firm employs workers at two facilities, one in a high-cost-of-living country and one in a low-cost-of-living country. Wages and benefits at the first facility are several times those at the second. The firm's executives, all of whom live in the high-cost-of-living country, are deciding whether to close the first facility and shift production entirely to the second.

Run the decision through the veil of ignorance. Imagine a rational deliberator behind the veil, who does not know which facility's worker she will be when the rules go into effect. What policy would she choose? Not, certainly, *whatever maximizes the firm's total profit* — because she might be the one whose job vanishes. Probably some version of *if production must shift, the firm provides substantial transition support, retraining, or severance to the displaced workers, and the wage differential at the surviving facility narrows over time as compensation for inherited cost-of-living differences*. The exact answer is contested. The structural insight is that the *procedure* — designing the rule from behind the veil — produces a humane answer that the executives, deliberating from inside their own positions, would not produce on their own. Rawls's contribution to business ethics is the procedure, not the specific outcome. The procedure can be applied to any policy where the deciders are positionally separated from the affected parties — which is to say, almost every important business policy.

### Common misconceptions

The first misconception is that Kant's deontology and Rawls's contract theory are opposed frameworks. They are cousins. Both place the rational individual at the center of moral analysis. Both refuse to permit one person to be sacrificed for the aggregate good. Both produce principles that hold *categorically* once derived. They differ in derivation, not in spirit — Kant derives his principles from the structure of reason itself; Rawls derives his from a procedure of impartial choice — but the principles they reach are mutually compatible.

The second misconception is that the veil of ignorance is unrealistic and therefore useless. The veil is *deliberately* unrealistic — that is the point. It is a thought experiment whose purpose is to subtract the bias that flows from knowing your position. As a procedure for actually making decisions, it asks: *what policy would I support if I did not know which side of this policy I was going to be on?* That question is answerable in five minutes by any honest decision-maker, and it forces a kind of impartiality that ordinary deliberation does not. Robert Nozick, Rawls's Harvard colleague, raised the most serious sustained objection — that the redistributive principles Rawls derives are coercive in ways that violate property rights established by legitimate transaction. The Rawls-Nozick exchange runs through every contemporary debate about taxation, healthcare, and corporate responsibility. Neither has put the other away.

## Concept 4: Is there a universal business ethic?

Across the four traditions just surveyed — Aristotelian and Confucian virtue ethics, Benthamite-Millian utilitarianism, Kantian deontology, Rawlsian contractualism — a question keeps resurfacing. *What survives translation?* What can a Singaporean firm and a Norwegian firm and a Chilean firm all be expected to do, and what is reasonably understood as culturally local?

The four traditions agree on substantially more than the surface differences suggest. Each insists, in different vocabulary, on:

- Honest dealing with the parties to a transaction. Aristotle calls this *commutative justice*. Confucius calls it *zhōngxìn* — wholehearted sincerity. Bentham would call it the precondition of any meaningful utility calculation. Kant calls it a duty. Rawls would derive it from the original position.
- Some form of fair distribution of cooperative gains. Aristotle's *distributive justice*. Confucius's relational obligations. Mill's harm principle as protection against exploitation. Kant's humanity formulation. Rawls's difference principle.
- A constraint against treating other parties as instruments only. The most Kantian of the four formulations, but echoed in the others — Aristotle's strictures against using slaves as means (within the constraints of his society's stratification), Confucius's relational ethics, Mill's harm principle.

What does *not* survive translation cleanly is the question of who counts as a moral patient — whose interests are entered into the calculation in the first place. Aristotle's circle excluded slaves and women. Confucius's circle was ordered by family hierarchy. The Enlightenment thinkers expanded the circle dramatically but unevenly. Contemporary business ethics inherits a circle that, at least in formal terms, includes all human beings — and is increasingly being asked to include non-human animals, future generations, and the natural environment itself. Chapter 4 will return to that expansion in detail.

The honest answer to *is there a universal business ethic* is *yes, on a thin core, and no, on the thick details*. The core — honesty, non-coercion, fair dealing, treating parties as ends — is genuinely universal across the major traditions. The thick details — what counts as *fair*, who is owed special duties, when an inequality is acceptable — are culturally specific and contested even within cultures. A practitioner who hopes to operate ethically across borders needs both: the thin core as a non-negotiable commitment, and the cultural literacy to recognize how the thick details vary in the specific markets where the firm operates.

## Integration: applying four lenses to one decision

Return to Chiquita Brands International. The case is, in its way, a perfect integration exercise because each of the four schools has something distinct to say about it.

*Aristotelian virtue.* Begin with character. *Phronesis* asks: in this specific situation — extortion under threat of violence in a foreign country with weak state capacity — what is the courageous action? Almost certainly it is to refuse the second payment, even if the first was defensible. Repetition has built a habit; the habit has built a character; the character is now Chiquita-as-funder-of-armed-groups. Virtue ethics' indictment is not about a single bad decision but about an organizational character formed by hundreds of small ones.

*Confucian relational ethics.* The relevant relationships are workers, customers, the surrounding Colombian community, the U.S. legal order, and shareholders. Confucius would be most concerned with the rupture in the relationship to the surrounding community — the company has, in fact, become a participant in the community's violence even if as a coerced one. The category Confucius would use is *li* — the proper order of relationships — and the company has placed itself outside that order. The repair is more demanding than financial penalty; it requires re-entering right relation, which is a multi-year project.

*Utilitarianism.* The hedonic calculus is grim and surprising. Worker lives saved by the payments are real benefits; they belong on the ledger. But against them: armed groups funded, Colombian civilians killed by those groups, the broader corrupting effect on a fragile state, the eventual collapse of trust in Chiquita as a partner. The utilitarian conclusion, on full accounting, is that the seven-year strategy was net-negative. It was net-positive only on a short horizon and a narrow accounting frame.

*Kantian deontology.* The categorical imperative test is unforgiving. Universalize the maxim *fund armed groups operating in your area of business if they threaten your workers*. The world produced by this maxim universalized is one in which armed groups multiply, because doing so is a profitable business model: threaten a multinational, receive a payment. The maxim is self-defeating. It cannot be universalized. The action is wrong on Kant's framework regardless of consequences.

*Rawlsian fairness.* Behind the veil of ignorance, designing the rules of multinational operation in conflict-affected states, no rational deliberator would assent to a regime in which threatened firms quietly fund their threateners. The deliberator might be the Colombian villager whose family is killed by the funded militia. The veil produces a regime of mandatory disclosure, mandatory withdrawal under specified conditions, and an international funding mechanism for genuine worker protection — the rough outlines of what is now being built into the laws around corporate operation in fragile states.

The four lenses do not give identical answers in every detail. They converge, however, on the same overall verdict: Chiquita's seven-year strategy was wrong, and the wrongness is detectable from each of the major moral frameworks of the past two and a half millennia. That convergence is, in itself, the point. When all four major schools agree, the question is not really philosophical anymore. It is one of operational courage.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can identify the structural difference between Aristotelian virtue ethics (individual locus, character formed over a life) and Confucian virtue ethics (family locus, character formed in relationship), and you can speak to what is genuinely shared between them. You can trace the move from virtue ethics to utilitarianism as a response to the Industrial Revolution's scale, and you can articulate what Bentham gained — universality, calculability — and what he lost — character, dignity, the lynch-mob protection. You can apply Kant's categorical imperative to a contemporary business decision and can say what it asks that consequentialism does not. You can use Rawls's veil of ignorance as an operational test rather than as a philosophical curiosity, applying it to compensation structures, layoff policies, and supply-chain decisions. And you can argue, in either direction, the question of whether there is a universal business ethic — knowing both the strong case and the strong counter-case.

The single idea that matters most is that the lenses you use today were forged by specific people answering specific objections from earlier specific people. None of them is the final word. Each one is the current best instrument for a particular kind of ethical question, and the practitioner who carries all four — and knows when to reach for which — will outperform the practitioner who carries only one and applies it to every case.

The common mistake to watch for is intellectual loyalty. Defenders of one tradition often present it as the framework that subsumes the others. *Real* utilitarians often think Kantians are kidding themselves. *Real* Kantians often think utilitarians are missing the point. *Real* virtue ethicists often think both have given up on character. They are all partly right and partly wrong. The frameworks are tools, not creeds. Treat them as creeds and you will reach for the wrong one when the case calls for a different one.

You should now be able to teach Aristotle's golden mean, Bentham's hedonic calculus, Kant's categorical imperative, and Rawls's veil of ignorance to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in roughly thirty minutes, without notes, and to indicate which of the four you would apply to a specific case the colleague brings you.

## Connections forward

Chapter 3 returns from intellectual history to present-tense business practice with the question Chapter 1 introduced as a list and Chapter 2 has now equipped you to think about more carefully: *who counts*. Stakeholder analysis, applied seriously, is one of the most demanding things a business does. It requires you to identify every party affected by a decision, to weigh their interests, and to make defensible trade-offs. The frameworks of this chapter give you the tools to do that weighing. Chapter 3 gives you the *map* — the stakeholders and their characteristic claims — that the tools will be applied to.

The chapter after that (Chapter 4) takes three special stakeholders — society as a whole, the environment, and the government — and works each one in detail, because the duties owed to those three are different in kind from the duties owed to a customer or an employee. The intellectual history we have just walked is the apparatus for thinking about that difference.

You have the lenses. You have the lineage. The rest of the book is the practice.
