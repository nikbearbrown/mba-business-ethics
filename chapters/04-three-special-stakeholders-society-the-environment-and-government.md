# Three Special Stakeholders: Society, the Environment, and Government

In August 2006, the *Probo Koala*, a cargo ship operated by the multinational commodities trader Trafigura, anchored off the coast of Abidjan, the largest city in Côte d'Ivoire. Aboard the ship were several hundred tons of liquid waste — a slurry of caustic soda, hydrogen sulfide, and other byproducts of an industrial process the ship had earlier carried out off the coast of Gibraltar. The waste was supposed to be processed at a port in the Netherlands. The Dutch port, on inspection, refused it. Trafigura turned the ship south. A local Ivorian contractor, Compagnie Tommy, agreed to take the waste at a price of about three U.S. dollars per ton. The same waste, processed in Europe, would have cost about three thousand dollars per ton.

In the early hours of August 19, the contractor began discharging the waste at open dumping sites in and around Abidjan. By morning, residents of the city were waking up to a smell — the distinctive rot of hydrogen sulfide — and to symptoms: nausea, headaches, breathing difficulty, vomiting, miscarriages. Over the following weeks, more than one hundred thousand residents sought medical attention. Fifteen people died. The city's hospitals overflowed.

Ask the question that this chapter is going to spend the next twenty thousand words answering. *Whose responsibility was that?*

The trader who chose the cheap disposal route. The contractor who took the contract. The local officials who issued the permits or failed to inspect the sites. The Dutch port that refused the waste and triggered the southward redirection without ensuring an adequate alternative. The international shipping regime that allows toxic waste to be transported across borders. The European industrial process that produced the waste in the first place. The consumers, somewhere downstream, of products made by that process. Each of those parties is in some defensible sense a stakeholder in the events of August 2006. None of them is sufficient on their own. All of them, together, are.

Chapter 3 introduced the apparatus of stakeholder analysis. This chapter takes three of the stakeholders that chapter listed and treats each one in detail, because the duties owed to the three are different in kind from the duties owed to a customer or an employee. The three are *society*, *the environment*, and *government*. Each one shows up on every stakeholder map a serious analyst draws. Each one is also, in business practice, the stakeholder most often treated as background — the assumed condition rather than the named party. The Côte d'Ivoire case is what happens when all three are treated as background simultaneously.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

- **Explain** the legal and ethical history of corporate personhood and the shareholder-primacy doctrine, including the *Dodge v. Ford* and *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby* decisions, and **identify** how the doctrine has shifted across the century since 1919.
- **Apply** the moral minimum, the tragedy of the commons, and earth-jurisprudence frameworks to a specific environmental case.
- **Evaluate** the financial case for corporate sustainability using the published data from McKinsey, Corporate Knights' Global 100 Index, and case-level evidence from companies like Cisco, Dell, and Siemens.
- **Compare** market-based environmental policy mechanisms (cap-and-trade, carbon tax, ISO 14000 certification) and explain what each optimizes for and what each costs.
- **Identify** the structural conflicts of interest produced by the revolving door between government and industry, and **evaluate** specific regulatory failures (BP Deepwater Horizon, Vopak Houston) in light of those conflicts.

## Why this chapter matters

The three stakeholders this chapter covers — society, environment, government — are the three whose claims most often fall outside the firm's normal field of vision. Customers complain. Employees unionize. Suppliers cut ties. Society, the environment, and government usually do not push back until pressure has accumulated for years and the response, when it comes, is large — a regulatory action, a legal judgment, a movement-organized boycott, a Pulitzer-winning investigative series. The firms that handle these three stakeholders well do so because they have built attention to them into their normal operating procedure. The firms that handle them badly do so because each of the three was *somebody else's department* until it suddenly was not.

## Concept 1: Society as a special stakeholder — the corporate-personhood debate and the moral minimum

In the early winter of 1916, Henry Ford — by then the richest manufacturer in the United States, sitting on a cash position of roughly fifty-eight million dollars — testified in a Michigan courtroom that he was not going to declare the special dividend his largest minority shareholders had been demanding. The Dodge brothers, John and Horace, owned about ten percent of Ford Motor Company stock and had been receiving substantial dividends for years. They were now planning to use those dividends to start their own competing automobile company. Ford did not want to fund their competitor. He also did not want to keep extracting maximum profit from his customers. He wanted, instead, to plow the cash back into expanding production, lowering the price of the Model T (which he had already cut from over $800 to $440), raising wages (his famous five-dollar-a-day wage was already in place), and building new manufacturing capacity.

He testified, in his own words, that the purpose of his company was *to do as much as possible for everybody concerned, to make money and use it, give employment, and send out the car where the people can use it*.

The Michigan Supreme Court, in *Dodge v. Ford Motor Company* (1919), ruled against him. The court held that *a business corporation is organized and carried on primarily for the profit of the stockholders. The powers of the directors are to be employed for that end.* The court ordered Ford to declare the dividend. The Dodge brothers received their money. They did, in fact, start a car company.

There is an irony in the case that often gets missed. The same decision that established the shareholder-primacy doctrine also articulated the *business judgment rule* — the principle that courts should not second-guess management decisions made in good faith. The two principles are in tension. Shareholder primacy says management exists to maximize shareholder return. The business judgment rule says management has discretion to define what counts as long-term shareholder interest. A century of corporate law has been working out which of the two principles wins in which cases. The answer has shifted.

### The mechanism: shareholder primacy, the business judgment rule, and the moral minimum

Three terms anchor this concept and the relationships between them are the heart of the modern legal landscape.

**Limited liability** is the foundational bargain of the modern corporation. The state grants the firm a legal status under which the personal assets of its shareholders are protected from the firm's debts. In exchange, the firm is treated as a legal person — capable of entering contracts, owning property, suing and being sued, paying taxes. The bargain produces *socializing of losses*: the firm's failure mode is borne partly by society (creditors who don't get paid, communities affected by closures) rather than entirely by shareholders. The *quid pro quo* — the in-exchange-for-what part — is the moral basis for the claim that the firm owes society more than the law strictly requires.

**Shareholder primacy** is the doctrine that the firm's directors owe their primary fiduciary duty to shareholders, and that profit maximization is the operational expression of that duty. The doctrine traces to *Dodge v. Ford*; it received its sharpest twentieth-century articulation in Milton Friedman's 1970 *New York Times Magazine* essay (the same essay covered in Chapter 1). The shareholder-primacy doctrine has been losing ground in legal practice and in business education for the last twenty years, against challenges from both the right (*Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*, 2014) and the left (the Business Roundtable's August 2019 statement abandoning shareholder primacy as a guiding principle). The Cornell legal scholar Lynn Stout, in her 2012 book *The Shareholder Value Myth*, made the case that the doctrine had never been as legally settled as practitioners assumed; the doctrine, on her reading, was one defensible legal interpretation that had been treated as the only one.

**The moral minimum** is the floor the firm cannot fall below regardless of the prevailing legal regime. It includes the duty not to inflict avoidable harm on stakeholders, the duty not to use the firm's instruments to deceive or coerce, and the duty to honor commitments. The moral minimum is, by definition, narrower than the full set of moral obligations a fully ethical firm would honor — but it is the floor below which conduct is universally condemned, regardless of whether the local legal system happens to permit it.

### The trade-off: shareholder primacy is operationally clean but morally narrow

Shareholder primacy is an attractive doctrine to live under. It produces a single objective function — maximize shareholder return — that can be measured, tracked, and rewarded. It eliminates the difficulty of choosing among competing stakeholder claims by ranking them all below a single one. It allows boards, executives, and analysts to coordinate without continuous renegotiation of priorities.

The trade-off is what the doctrine excludes. It excludes the duty owed to non-shareholder stakeholders affected by the firm's decisions. It excludes the long-game point developed in Chapter 1 — that aggressive shareholder-return optimization on a quarterly horizon often destroys shareholder value on a decadal one. And it excludes the *quid pro quo* — the recognition that the firm owes society some return for the privilege of limited liability, beyond paying taxes and obeying the law.

The Cornell-versus-UCLA exchange in the *New York Times* in April 2015 captured the contemporary version of the debate. Steven Bainbridge, the UCLA law professor: *"If directors were allowed to deviate from shareholder wealth maximization, they would inevitably turn to indeterminate balancing standards, which provide no accountability."* Lynn Stout, replying: *"Modern corporate law does not require for-profit corporations to pursue profit at the expense of everything else, and many do not."* Both are partly right. The doctrine permits more discretion than its caricature allows. The discretion still has to be exercised by someone, and someone is accountable for the exercise. The legal system is, slowly, working out the rules of that accountability.

### The case sequence: Dodge, Wrigley, Hobby Lobby

Three cases trace the arc.

*Dodge v. Ford* (1919) established shareholder primacy and the business judgment rule simultaneously. The doctrine and its escape clause arrived in the same opinion.

*Shlensky v. Wrigley* (1968) tested the escape clause. William Wrigley Jr.'s Chicago Cubs had refused to install stadium lights for night baseball, in part because Wrigley had argued that night games would damage the surrounding Wrigleyville community. Shareholders sued, claiming the decision suppressed profits. The Illinois court ruled in Wrigley's favor: management had discretion to balance stakeholder interests against shareholder return. The lights were eventually installed in 1988, after the team had been sold to the Tribune Company in 1981 and the new ownership had spent six years fighting the holdouts.

*Burwell v. Hobby Lobby* (2014, 5-4 decision) tested the doctrine from the other direction. Hobby Lobby — a closely-held corporation owned by an evangelical family — objected on religious grounds to the Affordable Care Act's mandate that employer health insurance cover certain forms of contraception. The U.S. Supreme Court held that closely-held corporations could decline to provide such coverage on the basis of the corporation's religious beliefs. Justice Alito's majority opinion described the corporation as *simply a form of organization used by human beings to achieve desired ends*. The decision was hailed by some as a correction of shareholder primacy (corporations have moral commitments beyond profit) and condemned by others as a dangerous expansion of corporate personhood (corporations have moral commitments that override their employees'). It was, in fact, both.

The arc the three cases trace is *not* linear. It is a hundred-year argument about how much discretion management has, on what grounds, and to whose benefit. The argument is unresolved. The chapter's reader is now, by virtue of having understood it, in a better position to participate in it than ninety percent of the people doing so.

### Public health as the test case

The cleanest test of the moral minimum is the case where the firm's lawful product produces real and quantifiable harm to identifiable populations. Three industries are the standard test cases.

Tobacco, in the United States, produces revenue exceeding five hundred billion dollars annually. The Centers for Disease Control attributes approximately four hundred and eighty thousand U.S. deaths per year to tobacco use — about one in five. The industry has known of the harm internally since the 1950s and was found in 2006, in *United States v. Philip Morris*, to have engaged in a decades-long racketeering conspiracy to deceive the public about the risks. The legal regime has gradually tightened — advertising restrictions, warning labels, a 1998 master settlement of $206 billion to state attorneys general — but the product remains lawful and the firms remain operational.

Alcohol produces approximately seven percent of all global deaths annually — about three and a half million people — according to the World Health Organization. Global alcohol sales exceed one trillion dollars per year. The legal regime is far more permissive than tobacco's; no warning labels equivalent to the cigarette pack, no comparable advertising restrictions in most jurisdictions.

The opioid crisis presents the same structural question in a more recent form. In 2016, U.S. opioid overdose deaths approached sixty thousand — nearly double the year's gun-violence death toll. The pharmaceutical industry's role has been, by hundreds of billions in lawsuits and settlements, found insufficient on the moral minimum. Purdue Pharma's *Sackler family* received its formal settlement in 2021, with provisions for personal contribution to victim funds; the criminal-vs-civil distinction in pharmaceutical-executive accountability remains, in 2026, an active legal question.

Each of these three cases produces a question the chapter has now equipped you to ask. *Where is the moral minimum, and where is the firm's discretion above the minimum?* In each case, the answer has shifted toward higher firm responsibility over the last fifty years. None of the three has stabilized.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that the law settles ethics. It does not. The law is the floor; ethics asks what is owed above the floor.

The second is that *Hobby Lobby* was a victory for one political coalition over another. It was, more accurately, a milestone in the transformation of the legal-corporate-personhood doctrine. The expansion of corporate moral standing cuts in multiple political directions and will continue to.

The third is that public-health-relevant industries are uniquely subject to scrutiny because of their product. They are subject to scrutiny because the *quid pro quo* of limited liability is most visible when the product produces population-level harm. The same scrutiny applies, in principle, to any firm whose operations affect non-customer populations — which is to say, almost every firm of meaningful size.

## Concept 2: The environment as a stakeholder — sustainability, externalities, and the tragedy of the commons

It is the middle of the night in international waters off the coast of, perhaps, Florida, perhaps Alaska, perhaps the Mediterranean. A cruise ship is at sea. Below decks, behind a closed door labeled *waste management*, valves are opened. Somewhere between several hundred thousand and several million gallons of sewage — graywater from showers and sinks, blackwater from toilets, bilge water containing fuel residue — is released directly into the ocean. The discharge is, in international waters, legal. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency has estimated that the global cruise industry collectively discharges approximately one billion gallons of sewage into the oceans annually. Cruise ships do not, on the whole, dump during daylight hours; the timing is, the EPA notes, designed to minimize visible documentation.

The cruise discharge is invisible. It is also one of the cleanest examples of what economists call an *externality* — a cost imposed on parties outside a transaction that does not appear on the transaction's ledger. The cruise passenger pays for the cruise. The cruise line pays for the fuel and the labor. The ocean — and through the ocean, the marine ecosystem, the coastal communities, the people who eat fish caught downstream of the discharge — pays for the disposal.

Externalities are not unique to the cruise industry. They are the structural failure mode of a particular kind of resource — what the British economist William Forster Lloyd, in the mid-nineteenth century, called *the commons*. A common resource is one that is non-excludable (anyone can use it) and rival in consumption (one user's use diminishes another's). Pasture, fisheries, the atmosphere, groundwater. The ecologist Garrett Hardin, in a 1968 *Science* article titled *The Tragedy of the Commons*, generalized the analysis: in the absence of a mechanism to charge users for the cost they impose on the commons, individually rational use will, in aggregate, exhaust the resource. The tragedy is not that any single user behaves badly. It is that the structure of the situation produces collective failure even when every individual is acting in their own legitimate interest.

The environment, on the analyst's stakeholder map, is the commons writ large. Most of the chapter's hardest questions sit at the place where individually rational firm behavior produces collectively damaging outcomes, and where no single firm is solely responsible for the damage but each contributes to it.

### The mechanism: sustainability, the carbon footprint, the moral minimum, and the policy instruments

Several technical terms anchor the concept and each does specific work.

**Sustainability** is the operational discipline of running a business so that its use of resources can be continued indefinitely without degrading the underlying conditions of its own operation. The phrase is more than aspirational. It can be measured — energy use per unit of output, water consumption per unit of revenue, greenhouse gas emissions per employee — and the measurement is the basis for the third-party rating systems (Dow Jones Sustainability Indices since 1999; Corporate Knights' Global 100 since 2005) that have moved investor attention noticeably.

**Carbon footprint** is the total volume of greenhouse gas emissions produced, directly or indirectly, by a firm's activities. The footprint includes the emissions of the firm's own facilities (Scope 1), the emissions associated with the energy the firm purchases (Scope 2), and the emissions associated with the firm's supply chain and product use (Scope 3). Most firms report Scope 1 and 2 reasonably well; Scope 3 is the difficult and contested part of the accounting.

**Earth jurisprudence** is the legal-philosophical position that the natural environment itself has standing — that ecosystems, watersheds, species, in some formulations even individual rivers, can be parties to legal proceedings, with human representatives speaking on their behalf. The position is associated with the South African environmental attorney Cormac Cullinan and the eco-theologian Thomas Berry. Ecuador's 2008 Constitution incorporates the doctrine; New Zealand recognized the legal personhood of the Whanganui River in 2017; several U.S. municipalities have passed ordinances along similar lines. The doctrine is contested — its critics argue that legal standing for non-human entities collapses the distinction between rights-bearers and the environments they inhabit — but it is in active legal development.

**Cap-and-trade** is one of two main market-based instruments for pricing environmental externalities. The regulator sets a cap on total emissions for a sector; the cap is divided into permits; firms trade the permits among themselves; firms whose abatement is cheap sell permits to firms whose abatement is expensive. The European Union Emissions Trading System, operational since 2005, is the largest example; California's program, since 2013, is the largest in North America.

**Carbon tax** is the alternative instrument. The regulator imposes a per-ton charge on emissions; the firms pay the charge. The charge produces revenue (which can fund abatement, transition support, or rebates to households); it also produces a price signal that pushes firms toward cleaner alternatives. The Climate Leadership Council's 2017 proposal for a U.S. carbon tax — supported by a coalition that included Exxon Mobil, Shell, BP, Total, and Conservative former Republican administration officials — proposed a starting price of about forty-five dollars per metric ton, projected to reduce U.S. emissions by more than forty percent over the first decade. The proposal has not been enacted at the federal level. As of 2026, more than forty countries and several major U.S. states and Canadian provinces operate one or both of the two instruments.

### The trade-off: profit-and-environment is positive-sum more often than zero-sum

The empirical case for sustainability as a *business strategy*, separate from its case as a moral commitment, is stronger than the conventional skepticism allows. Several lines of evidence converge.

Corporate Knights' Global 100 — published annually since 2005, ranking the 100 most sustainable large public companies — has, over its lifespan, modestly outperformed the broader MSCI All Country World Index. A $250 investment placed in the 2005 Global 100 portfolio would have grown to about $580 by 2015, against $520 for a comparable index fund.

McKinsey's 2016 report *Sustainability's Strategic Worth* surveyed 2,500 executives across thirty-five countries: cost reduction was the single most-cited rationale for sustainability investments (cited by 36% of firms), narrowly ahead of corporate-reputation considerations.

Specific cases reinforce the aggregate. Dell Technologies' *Connected Workplace* program — flexible work arrangements, work-from-home, shared offices — reportedly avoids about 7,000 metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions annually and saves the company approximately $12 million per year in facility and commute-related costs. Cisco — the U.S. company most consistently top-ranked on the Global 100 — has built its sustainability commitments into supplier requirements, which has reduced supplier-side risk and shortened response times to material disruptions. Siemens' energy-efficiency rating (highest in its industrial-conglomerate sector) translates directly into lower operating costs at every facility.

The trade-off is not, in the aggregate, between profit and sustainability. The trade-off is between *short-term* margins and *medium-term* sustainability investments. The latter typically pay back within three to seven years and continue to compound. The firms that invested early in sustainability practices did so under more uncertainty than the firms investing today; they are also, on the available data, the firms with stronger long-term performance.

### Worked example: the West African toxic waste cases

The Côte d'Ivoire case from the chapter's cold open is one of two well-documented post-1990 cases of multinational toxic-waste dumping in West Africa. The other — sometimes called the *Koko, Nigeria case* — occurred in the late 1980s, when an Italian-arranged shipment of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and other industrial waste was discharged in barrels in a coastal village in southern Nigeria. The disposal was not paid for at international rates; it was arranged through a local landlord who was paid for storage rather than processing. The barrels leaked. Local residents experienced acute and chronic illness. The Nigerian government repatriated the waste to Italy at substantial cost.

Apply the chapter's apparatus.

The *moral minimum* is unambiguously violated in both cases. Disposing of waste at three dollars per ton in a country with no infrastructure to process it, when the same waste in Europe would cost three thousand dollars per ton, is the cleanest possible example of using a stakeholder (the local population) as a means to a cost-reduction end. There is no plausible reading of the moral minimum on which this is permitted.

The *tragedy of the commons* is operating, but with a twist. The atmosphere and oceans are commons in Hardin's classical sense. The land of Abidjan, the groundwater of Koko, are not commons — they belong to specific people and specific governments. The structural failure is that international waste trade can move waste from a high-cost-of-disposal jurisdiction to a low-cost-of-disposal jurisdiction *as if the lower-cost jurisdiction had agreed to bear the disposal cost*, when the lower-cost jurisdiction is actually populated by parties who have not agreed and would not agree if asked.

The *earth-jurisprudence* response is to give the affected ecosystem (the lagoon, the village, the watershed) standing in legal proceedings. Several Ivorian civil-society groups attempted this in the years after 2006. The cases produced settlements but not the establishment of broader doctrine. The doctrine is still developing.

The *policy-instrument* response is the 1989 Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes, which restricts export of hazardous waste from OECD countries to non-OECD countries unless specifically authorized. The Côte d'Ivoire case occurred after Basel's entry into force, on a transit route that exploited a regulatory gap. The 2010 *Bamako Convention* among African states tightened the local prohibition. Enforcement remains partial. The waste keeps flowing. The work continues.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that environmental damage is mostly visible. Most of it is not. Cruise sewage, groundwater contamination, microplastic accumulation, methane emissions from cattle and gas pipelines — the bulk of the damage is invisible at the scale of any single observer. The discipline of sustainability accounting is the discipline of forcing the invisible to be measured.

The second is that environmental responsibility is a tax on profitability. The aggregate evidence does not support this. Sustainability investments compound; sustainability avoidance produces eventual costs that are larger than the deferred investment would have been.

The third is that environmental policy is a binary choice between regulation and market mechanisms. It is not. The two complement each other. The most effective regimes combine *quantity-based caps* (regulation), *price-based instruments* (markets), *standards-based requirements* (ISO 14000, LEED), and *legal-standing innovations* (earth jurisprudence) into a layered system. Each layer covers a failure mode of the others.

## Concept 3: Government as a stakeholder — partnership, capture, and the BP Deepwater Horizon failure

At about 9:50 PM Central Daylight Time on April 20, 2010, on the *Deepwater Horizon* — a semi-submersible offshore drilling rig leased by BP from Transocean Ltd., operating in the *Macondo* field about forty miles off the coast of Louisiana — a high-pressure surge of methane gas from the wellbore reached the rig's drilling deck. The gas ignited. The rig burned for thirty-six hours and then sank. Eleven workers were killed. Seventeen were injured. The wellhead, a mile below the ocean surface, continued to discharge oil for eighty-seven days before it was capped. Estimates of the total discharge range from 4.9 million barrels (the National Commission's official figure) to higher numbers in independent assessments. By any count, it is the largest accidental marine oil spill in history.

The Deepwater Horizon disaster is the integration case for this chapter. Society's stakeholders failed: eleven workers and their families. The environment's stakeholders failed: the Gulf of Mexico, its fisheries, its coastal wetlands, its migratory bird populations, the Louisiana coastline whose erosion was already accelerated and which the spill accelerated further. Government's stakeholders failed: the Minerals Management Service of the U.S. Department of the Interior, the agency responsible for offshore oil regulation, was found in the post-disaster review to have been so deeply compromised by industry capture that the spill's structural cause traces partly to its enforcement failure.

The chapter's third concept is about government as a stakeholder. The disaster is what government failure produces.

### The mechanism: the revolving door, regulatory capture, and the public-private partnership ideal

Three terms anchor the concept.

**The revolving door** is the movement of personnel between government regulatory agencies and the industries those agencies regulate. The phenomenon is not in itself corrupt; expertise developed on one side is genuinely useful on the other. The risk is structural: when the same individual moves repeatedly back and forth, when the prospect of post-government employment in a regulated industry incentivizes regulators to be less aggressive while in office, when industry hires former regulators specifically to leverage relationships and not just expertise — the door has stopped revolving and started rotating in only one direction. The U.S. Government Accountability Office's 2008 study found that roughly 90,000 individuals had moved from the Department of Defense into private defense contractors over the preceding eight years.

**Regulatory capture** is what happens when a regulator's primary loyalty has shifted from the public interest to the regulated industry's interest. Capture is rarely deliberate. It typically emerges through the cumulative effect of access — the regulated industry's lobbyists are in the regulator's office every week, the public-interest groups are in once a quarter — combined with revolving-door incentives, combined with the asymmetry of expertise (the regulator depends on the industry for technical information). The Minerals Management Service was, in the years before Deepwater Horizon, by multiple post-disaster accounts a captured agency. Inspectors were socializing with the firms they inspected. Required safety audits were being signed off without site visits. Industry-proposed rules were being adopted without independent review.

**Public-private partnerships** (PPPs) are the affirmative version of the same relationship — collaborations between government and industry to deliver outcomes that neither sector can produce alone. PPPs have been the dominant model for transit infrastructure, for many forms of medical research, for technology development in defense and aerospace. Done well, they combine government's coordination capacity with industry's execution capacity. Done badly, they shade into capture. The boundary between a successful PPP and a failed one is largely the boundary between mutual accountability and mutual coziness.

### The trade-off: government regulation is friction-with-purpose

The conventional business framing of regulation is as friction — slowing transactions, imposing compliance cost, requiring documentation that produces no immediate revenue. The framing is correct on the surface. It is also incomplete.

Regulation's purpose is to internalize the externalities that the market on its own does not internalize. The cost of regulation is paid by the regulated firms; the benefit is paid to the third parties — workers, communities, ecosystems — who would otherwise bear the cost. The trade-off the regulatory regime is making is the explicit allocation of that cost. *Less* regulation means lower compliance burden on firms and higher burden on third parties. *More* regulation means higher burden on firms and lower burden on third parties. There is no zero-cost option; the regulation just chooses where the cost lands.

Where regulation goes wrong, the failure is typically in one of two directions. *Overregulation* imposes compliance costs that exceed the externality being addressed; the marginal investment in compliance produces less third-party benefit than it costs the firm. *Underregulation* leaves externalities uninternalized; the firm captures the savings, the third parties bear the cost. The Deepwater Horizon disaster is, on the available evidence, an underregulation failure. The cost of an acoustically-triggered shutoff valve — a backup safety device required by Norwegian and Brazilian regulators for offshore wells, not required by the U.S. Minerals Management Service — was approximately $500,000 per well. The cost of the spill, including BP's $20 billion settlement, the Transocean settlement, the federal criminal fines, the Gulf states' claims, and the long-tail environmental and economic damages, exceeded $65 billion. The deferred half-million-dollar safety investment was, on the available evidence, the most expensive money BP and Transocean ever did not spend.

### The Norwegian / Brazilian comparison

The point is not that the United States cannot operate offshore oil safely. The point is that the regulatory regime determines whether it does.

Norway operates substantial offshore oil production in the North Sea — comparable in scale to the U.S. Gulf production. The Norwegian regulator, the Petroleum Safety Authority, requires acoustic shutoff backup, requires independent safety auditing, requires public posting of incident reports, requires the operating firm to be majority-owned by entities (often the Norwegian state, which holds majority stakes in *Equinor*, formerly Statoil) that have direct stake in long-term Norwegian environmental outcomes. The Norwegian regime has, over its operational history since the 1970s, produced approximately one-tenth the per-barrel environmental incident rate of the U.S. Gulf operations.

Brazil's regime, structured around *Petrobras* (also majority state-owned), shows a similar pattern: stricter independent regulation, lower per-barrel incident rate. The difference is not cultural. It is institutional. The U.S. regime, operating under conditions of more revolving-door movement, more lobbying influence, and less independent technical review, produces correspondingly worse safety outcomes.

The post-Deepwater Horizon U.S. response — the reorganization of the Minerals Management Service into three separate agencies (the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management, the Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement, the Office of Natural Resources Revenue), with the goals of separating revenue collection from safety regulation — is the United States acknowledging the structural problem the Norwegian regime had already solved. Whether the reorganization has produced operational improvement remains, in 2026, partly contested.

### Worked example: Vopak Houston, 2017

Government regulation does not always fail. The 2017 settlement between the U.S. Department of Justice, the Environmental Protection Agency, the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality, and Vopak (a Houston-based chemical-storage operator) is an example of working regulation. Vopak had been operating a wastewater-treatment facility in the Houston Ship Channel area in violation of Clean Air Act requirements; the violations resulted in excess emissions of hazardous air pollutants and volatile organic compounds in an area that was already not meeting ground-level ozone standards. The settlement required Vopak to install state-of-the-art pollution control technology, deploy infrared cameras for continuous emission detection, and pay a $2.5 million civil penalty.

The Vopak case is the *boring* version of regulation working. No catastrophic incident. No headline. No congressional hearing. Just an inspection regime that found a violation, an enforcement action that produced a settlement, a remediation requirement that produced an improvement. The aggregate of Vopak-style cases is what the Clean Air Act since 1970 has actually produced — a fifty-year arc of measurable air-quality improvement in U.S. metropolitan areas, even as economic activity has grown. The visible failures (Deepwater Horizon, Vopak under the prior regime) make the news; the steady successes do not.

### Common misconceptions

The first is that regulation is *anti-business*. Some regulation is. Most regulation that survives political contestation is the kind that the affected industries themselves now prefer — predictable, applied uniformly to competitors, with clear compliance pathways. The most-frequently-cited corporate complaints about regulation are about its inconsistency, its slowness in adapting to new technology, and the lobbying advantage it gives to incumbents who can afford the compliance infrastructure that smaller competitors cannot. None of these complaints requires the conclusion that *no regulation* would be better; they require the conclusion that *better regulation* would be better.

The second is that the revolving door is symmetric. It is not. The flow from government into industry is heavier than the reverse flow, and the reverse flow concentrates at senior levels. The pattern is what regulatory capture would predict.

The third is that government and industry are in opposition. The most durable structures — public-private partnerships that work — are ones in which both sides have aligned long-term incentives and independent accountability. The relationship is not adversarial. It is also not symbiotic. It is supposed to be *collaborative-with-checks*. When the checks fail, capture develops; when the collaboration fails, friction develops. The good regimes balance both.

## Integration: the BP Deepwater Horizon failure across all three special stakeholders

Return to the rig. Eleven workers are dead. Seventeen are injured. The wellhead is discharging. The narrative of what happens next, over the eighty-seven days from April 20 to July 15, 2010, when the well was finally capped, is the integration case for this entire chapter.

*Society as stakeholder.* The eleven dead workers were not random parties; each was a named individual with named family members. The Gulf coastal communities — fishing families in Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Florida, the small businesses dependent on Gulf tourism, the local governments dependent on Gulf-related tax base — became the second-order affected society. BP's response progressed through stages familiar from Chapter 1's analysis of crisis management: initial denial of severity, eventual acceptance, large compensation funds (the $20 billion *Gulf Coast Claims Facility* administered by Kenneth Feinberg), criminal prosecutions of individual executives. The company's CEO, Tony Hayward, lost his job; his replacement, Bob Dudley, oversaw the long settlement period.

*Environment as stakeholder.* The 4.9-million-barrel discharge was not a single event but an eighty-seven-day continuous release. The cleanup involved 47,000 personnel, 6,500 vessels, controlled burns of surface oil, dispersants applied at depth (themselves a contested intervention with their own environmental consequences), and a long-term Natural Resource Damage Assessment that found ongoing damage to deep-sea coral, marine mammal populations, and migratory bird habitat. Five years after the spill, tar balls were still washing up on Louisiana beaches. Mangrove root death was contributing to ongoing land loss. The environmental cost is not closed in 2026 and will not be for decades.

*Government as stakeholder.* The Minerals Management Service was, in the post-disaster Presidential Commission's findings, a captured agency. Industry-proposed safety standards had been adopted with insufficient independent review. Acoustic shutoff valve technology — required in Norway and Brazil — had been considered and rejected after industry lobbying. The post-disaster reorganization separated the agency into three pieces (energy management, safety regulation, revenue collection) explicitly to reduce capture risk. The reorganization is what happens when the regulatory failure is too large to deny. The structural question — *why was the agency captured in the first place* — is the question Concept 3 spent its length addressing.

The four lenses from Chapter 2 each produce a verdict.

*Virtue ethics:* What kind of company is BP becoming, by operating with a regulatory regime it had helped weaken? The character that produced the deferred safety investment is the character that produced the eleven deaths. Character is built one decision at a time, in both directions.

*Utilitarianism:* The benefit of the deferred safety investment was approximately $500,000 per well. The cost, summed across all parties, exceeded $65 billion. The hedonic calculus is not close.

*Deontology:* BP and Transocean had a duty to their workers, to the affected communities, and to the marine ecosystem. The duties were not honored. Kant's universal-law test — *can the maxim of operating without backup safety on offshore oil rigs be universalized?* — fails in the Norwegian and Brazilian comparisons.

*Rawlsian:* Behind the veil of ignorance, designing the rules of offshore oil regulation, no rational deliberator would assent to a regime in which firms are permitted to operate without backup safety because the industry's lobbyists have asked the regulator nicely. The deliberator might be the worker who dies in the explosion, the fishing family whose livelihood disappears, the bird whose habitat is destroyed.

The four lenses converge on the verdict that the Deepwater Horizon failure was avoidable and was, in operational fact, avoided in jurisdictions with stronger regulatory regimes. The convergence is the chapter's most important integration finding. *The verdict is not philosophical; it is operational.* The chapter you have just finished is what the operational verdict looks like when the analyst takes society, the environment, and government seriously as stakeholders in the firm's decision-making.

## Chapter summary

You can now do five things you could not do before. You can articulate the legal-philosophical history of corporate personhood and the shareholder-primacy doctrine, including the case sequence (*Dodge v. Ford*, *Shlensky v. Wrigley*, *Burwell v. Hobby Lobby*) that traces its arc, and you can identify where in that arc the doctrine sits today. You can apply the moral-minimum, tragedy-of-the-commons, and earth-jurisprudence frameworks to a specific environmental case and produce a defensible analysis of where responsibility lies. You can evaluate the financial case for sustainability using primary data — Corporate Knights' Global 100 returns, McKinsey's strategic-worth survey, case-level cost savings at Dell, Cisco, and Siemens — rather than treating sustainability as an article of faith. You can compare the major market-based environmental policy instruments and explain what each optimizes for and what each costs. You can identify the structural conditions under which the revolving door becomes regulatory capture and apply that diagnosis to specific cases (BP Deepwater Horizon, Vopak Houston).

The single idea that matters most is that society, the environment, and government are *not* background. They are stakeholders with claims, with the capacity to inflict consequences when their claims are not honored, and with the moral standing to deserve attention before consequences are inflicted. The firms that handle these three well do so because their attention to the three is built into normal operation. The firms that handle them badly do so because the three were *somebody else's department* until it was too late.

The common mistake to watch for is treating these stakeholders as constraint. They are constraint, in the same sense that gravity is constraint on aircraft design — that is, the constraint is the condition of doing the work, not a tax on the work. Aircraft designers do not treat gravity as a tax; they design with it. The discipline of this chapter is to make society, the environment, and government parts of the design problem rather than excuses for design failures.

You should now be able to teach the *quid pro quo of limited liability* and the *tragedy of the commons* and the *revolving door / regulatory capture* triad to a colleague who has not read this chapter, in twenty-five minutes, and to identify which of the three most directly applies to a current case the colleague brings you.

## Connections forward

Chapter 5 returns from the institutional level to the cultural one. The frameworks of this chapter — corporate personhood, sustainability accounting, regulatory capture — are products of specific cultural-historical arrangements. They survive translation across some cultures and fail in others. Chapter 5 asks the cross-cultural question: when a firm operates simultaneously in a context that has internalized the *quid pro quo of limited liability* and one that has not, what does ethical operation look like?

Chapter 6 then narrows from the firm's external relationships to its internal ones — what employers owe employees. The frameworks of Chapter 4 have been about parties largely outside the firm. Chapter 6 turns to the parties most inside it.

You have the apparatus for analyzing the firm in its relationship to society, the environment, and government. The apparatus is not theoretical. It is operational. The remainder of the book takes the apparatus and applies it to the specific relationships and decisions that make up business practice.
