# Figure briefs — Chapter 3: Defining and Prioritizing Stakeholders

## Figure 3.1: Concentric stakeholder rings around Starbucks

**Placement:** Chapter opening, after the Nelson and Robinson scene, before Learning Objectives.

**Description:** Radial diagram. Center: "Starbucks." Three concentric rings outward: (1) *internal stakeholders* — board, CEO, executives, baristas, store managers; (2) *primary external stakeholders* — customers, coffee growers, milk suppliers, retailers, institutional investors, local communities; (3) *secondary external stakeholders* — regulators, competitors, media, NGOs, social-platform-organized publics, future customers. Three labeled points connecting specific named individuals into the diagram: "Nelson and Robinson" (in the secondary ring becoming primary), "the manager who placed the 911 call" (internal), "the bystander with the phone" (secondary, diffused, becoming active within hours).

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that (a) stakeholders are not abstractions — specific named individuals can be located in the map, and (b) a person's position in the map is not fixed; the bystander moved from background to active stakeholder by the act of recording.

**Style notes:** Radial concentric rings, plain serif. The three named individuals as small labeled points with thin lines pointing to their ring. No company logo, no color hierarchy.

## Figure 3.2: Stakeholder taxonomy × awareness level

**Placement:** Concept 1, after "Categories: four types of stakeholders, four levels of awareness."

**Description:** Two-dimensional 4×4 grid. Horizontal axis (left to right): *enabling*, *normative*, *functional*, *diffused*. Vertical axis (top to bottom): *active*, *aware*, *latent*, *nonpublic*. Each cell labeled with one Starbucks-specific example. Selected examples:

- Enabling × Active: institutional investors voting on board recomposition during a crisis
- Functional × Aware: baristas in the affected store
- Diffused × Active: bystander video and resulting protest movement
- Functional × Nonpublic: coffee growers in Ethiopia (not currently affected)
- Normative × Latent: peer coffee chains watching the Starbucks response

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that the *active* row across all four columns is the row a firm should be tracking continuously, and that movement between rows can happen on timescales of hours.

**Style notes:** 4×4 grid. Plain serif. Cells with one short example each. No icons. The *active* row visually emphasized.

## Figure 3.3: The power-interest matrix applied to Malaysia Airlines

**Placement:** Concept 2, after "The trade-off."

**Description:** Two-by-two grid. Horizontal axis: *low power* (left) / *high power* (right). Vertical axis: *low interest* (bottom) / *high interest* (top). Four cells:

- **Top-right (key stakeholders, high power × high interest):** Malaysian government, families of MH370/MH17 victims (moral standing as functional power)
- **Top-left (interested observers, low power × high interest):** hajj-bound Malaysian Muslims (individually low power, collectively the *active public* of a strategic segment)
- **Bottom-right (latent powers, high power × low interest):** international competitors (Singapore Airlines, AirAsia, Emirates), international regulators
- **Bottom-left (background, low power × low interest):** routine non-frequent passengers

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that the *individual* power axis can mislead — hajj-bound passengers have low individual power but enormous collective interest, which makes them the strategic priority Bellew correctly identified.

**Style notes:** Two-by-two grid, plain serif, no color. Each cell with named examples, not generic labels.

## Figure 3.4: Triple bottom line — three overlapping circles and four failure modes

**Placement:** Concept 3, end of "common misconceptions."

**Description:** Three nested overlapping circles labeled "PROFIT," "PEOPLE," "PLANET." The central overlap (where all three circles meet) labeled "GENUINE CSR." Four areas outside the central overlap labeled with failure modes:

- *Profit only* (the area where only the profit circle is engaged): "extraction"
- *Profit + People, no Planet*: "philanthropy without environmental accountability"  
- *Profit + Planet, no People*: "greenwashing without labor commitments"
- *Language of all three but operations only in profit*: "structural greenwashing" (in the area where the labels overlay but the operations do not)

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that the central overlap is the only place genuine CSR lives, and that each outer area is a recognizable failure mode they can name when they see it.

**Style notes:** Three overlapping circles. Plain serif. No color hierarchy. Each labeled area in a distinct visual texture or hatch pattern (no colors needed).
