# Figure briefs — Chapter 4: Three Special Stakeholders

## Figure 4.1: The three special stakeholders, with the Côte d'Ivoire case as the failure pattern

**Placement:** Chapter opening, after the Trafigura cold open scene.

**Description:** Three concentric stakeholder rings, all containing the firm at the center. Each ring labeled: SOCIETY (residents of Abidjan), ENVIRONMENT (dumping sites and groundwater), GOVERNMENT (Ivorian permitting officials and Dutch port authority and the international shipping regime). Specific failure points marked in each ring with brief annotations (residents' symptoms; lagoon contamination; permit gaps).

**Pedagogical purpose:** Student should notice that the three rings *interact*. No single ring fully contains the failure; the three failed in interaction with one another. The chapter's central argument is that none of the three is a background; all three are stakeholders.

**Style notes:** Concentric rings, plain serif, no color. Annotations as small labeled points in each ring. The firm at the center as a small labeled box.

## Figure 4.2: Cap-and-trade vs. carbon tax — what each instrument fixes

**Placement:** Concept 2, after the policy-instruments paragraph.

**Description:** Two-column comparison.

| Instrument | What the regulator sets | What the market sets | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cap-and-trade | Total quantity of emissions allowed (the cap) | The price of emission permits (via trading) | Certainty about emissions outcome | Uncertainty about cost to firms |
| Carbon tax | The price per ton of emissions | The total quantity (firms reduce to optimize cost) | Certainty about price | Uncertainty about emissions quantity |

**Pedagogical purpose:** The two instruments solve the same problem from opposite directions. Student should understand the policy-design choice as one between *quantity certainty* and *price certainty* — and that the two are not interchangeable.

**Style notes:** Plain table, serif. No icons.

## Figure 4.3: The Norwegian / Brazilian / U.S. offshore-oil regulatory comparison

**Placement:** Concept 3, after the Norwegian/Brazilian comparison.

**Description:** Three-column comparison of regulatory regimes.

|  | Norway | Brazil | U.S. (pre-2010) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acoustic shutoff valve required? | Yes | Yes | No |
| Independent safety auditing? | Yes | Yes | Limited |
| Public posting of incident reports? | Yes | Yes | Limited |
| Operator majority-state-owned? | Yes (Equinor / former Statoil) | Yes (Petrobras) | No |
| Per-barrel environmental incident rate (relative) | 1× | ~1× | ~10× |

**Pedagogical purpose:** Student should notice that the regulatory regime, not the geological or economic conditions, determines safety outcome. The same industry can be operated more safely or less safely depending on institutional design.

**Style notes:** Plain comparison table. No color. Each row a single fact. Row 5 (incident-rate ratio) is the row that does the most work.
