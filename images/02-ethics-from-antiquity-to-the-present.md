# Figure briefs — Chapter 2: Ethics from Antiquity to the Present

## Figure 2.1: The arc of moral philosophy, 500 BCE to 2000 CE

**Placement:** Chapter opening, immediately after the Aristotle cold-open scene, before "Learning objectives."

**Description:** A horizontal timeline running from ~500 BCE to ~2000 CE. Anchor points (left to right): Confucius (5th century BCE, ~500 BCE), Aristotle (4th century BCE, ~335 BCE), Aquinas (13th century, ~1265), Bentham (1748–1832), Mill (1806–1873), Kant (1724–1804), Rawls (1921–2002). The long quiet interval between antiquity and the Enlightenment should be visually emphasized as empty space; the eighteenth- and nineteenth-century cluster should be visually dense with anchor points to dramatize the explosion of moral-philosophical activity around the Industrial Revolution.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice (a) the long quiet middle between ancient virtue ethics and modern reformulation, (b) the density of philosophical activity around the Enlightenment and the Industrial Revolution, and (c) that Rawls's contribution is *recent* — within living memory of the chapter's older readers.

**Style notes:** Schematic timeline. Plain serif typography. One color band for the philosophers, a contrasting band beneath labeled "social context" with anchors for: classical Greece, Han dynasty, Reformation, Industrial Revolution, post-WWII international order. Captions for each philosopher show dates of birth-death and the single most-associated work.

**Source for figure data:** OpenStax Business Ethics Ch 02, all source files; standard intellectual histories.

## Figure 2.2: The Aristotle / Confucius parallel

**Placement:** Concept 1, after the "mechanism" subsection, before "the trade-off."

**Description:** Two parallel columns — left labeled "Aristotle (4th century BCE, Athens)" and right labeled "Confucius (5th century BCE, China)." Side-by-side rows showing structurally similar concepts:

| Aristotle | Confucius |
|---|---|
| *Eudaimonia* (flourishing as life-mode) | The *junzi* (cultivated person) |
| Moral virtues as a list (courage, self-control, etc.) | The five great relationships (parent/child, husband/wife, etc.) |
| Habituation through practice | Practice through right relation in family |
| *Phronesis* (practical wisdom) | *Quan* (situational judgment, balance) |
| Locus: the individual | Locus: the family |

The visual emphasis should make clear that the systems are not identical; the rows align structurally but the *content* of each row differs in important ways.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that virtue ethics was not a Greek invention — that two cultures, separated by space and effectively unaware of one another, arrived at structurally similar answers to *how is character formed*. This independent emergence is itself an argument for the universality of the underlying question.

**Style notes:** Two-column comparative table. Plain serif. The two header rows (Aristotle / Confucius) styled identically to avoid privileging either tradition. No icons. Footnote each row with the specific source citation (e.g., for the *eudaimonia* row: *Nicomachean Ethics* 1097b; for the five-relationships row: *Analects* via Huang translation).

**Source for figure data:** OpenStax Business Ethics Ch 02, files 02-m66944.md (Aristotle) and 03-m66945.md (Confucius), with explicit comparison from 04-m66946.md.
