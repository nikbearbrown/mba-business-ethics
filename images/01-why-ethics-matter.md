# Figure briefs — Chapter 1: Why Ethics Matter

## Figure 1.1: The Equifax disclosure window

**Placement:** Chapter opening, immediately after the cold-open scene, before "Learning objectives."

**Description:** A horizontal timeline running left to right. Four anchors: (1) "mid-May 2017 — initial intrusion" at the left; (2) "late July 2017 — internal discovery; insider stock sales begin" near the center-left; (3) "September 7, 2017 — public announcement; share price falls ~14%" near the center-right; (4) "September 26, 2017 — CEO resigns" at the right. Between anchors 2 and 3, the six-week interior should be visually shaded or boxed to emphasize that this is the chapter's opening puzzle.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice the *six-week interior* between internal discovery and public disclosure. That gap is where the chapter's opening question lives — what the executives knew, what the law required them to do, and what ethics required them to do.

**Style notes:** Schematic timeline, not photorealistic. Black ruled line with date markers. Minimal color (one accent color for the shaded gap). Labels in plain serif. No company logo. Source link for each anchor in a small caption beneath: original *NYT* and SEC filings.

**Source for figure data:** OpenStax Business Ethics, source file 03-m66942.md, "The Equifax Data Breach" sidebar.

## Figure 1.2: The legal-by-ethical matrix

**Placement:** Concept 1, after the "Common misconceptions" subsection (specifically, after the second misconception that ethics and law are the same thing).

**Description:** A two-by-two grid. Horizontal axis: "Legal" / "Illegal." Vertical axis: "Ethical" / "Unethical." The four cells, each with one example labeled:

- **Legal × Ethical** (top-left): "Paying employees a living wage."
- **Legal × Unethical** (top-right): "Equifax executives' insider stock sales (2017)."
- **Illegal × Ethical** (bottom-left): "A whistleblower leaking client records to expose tax fraud (Bradley Birkenfeld, UBS, 2007–2009)."
- **Illegal × Unethical** (bottom-right): "Enron's accounting fraud (2001)."

The top-right cell should be visually emphasized (slightly shaded or with a starburst) to indicate that this is where most business-ethics problems live.

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that *legal* and *ethical* are two independent axes, not one. The exercise of placing each example forces the student to make the distinction concrete.

**Style notes:** Diagrammatic. Plain serif. Single color for the matrix lines. The emphasized cell uses a soft secondary tint, not a bold one. Labels short — example name plus year if relevant.

**Source for figure data:** OpenStax Business Ethics, source file 02-m66941.md (legal vs. ethical distinction); 03-m66942.md (Equifax sidebar; UBS/Birkenfeld sidebar). The Enron example is widely cited public knowledge but should be footnoted to a primary regulatory filing if the figure caption permits.

## Figure 1.3: The three lenses, one decision

**Placement:** Concept 2, end of the "mechanism" subsection, before "The trade-off."

**Description:** A central rectangle labeled "ONE DECISION." Three arrows pointing into the rectangle from the left, right, and bottom. Each arrow labeled with a lens: "ENDS — consequentialism" (left arrow), "MEANS — deontology" (right arrow), "CHARACTER — virtue ethics" (bottom arrow). Each arrow should also carry a one-line specification of what the lens asks: "Who is helped, who is harmed?" / "What duties are owed?" / "What kind of person/firm does this make me?"

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that all three lenses examine *the same decision* and that disagreement among the lenses is the signal that the decision is genuinely difficult. The figure visually grounds the chapter's central analytical move.

**Style notes:** Diagrammatic, schematic. Central decision-rectangle in plain serif title. Arrows in three slightly different stroke styles (solid, dashed, dotted) to make them distinguishable in black-and-white print. No icons.

**Source for figure data:** OpenStax Business Ethics, source file 02-m66941.md, "Ends, Means, and Character in Business" section.

## Figure 1.4: The horizon crossover — short-term shortcut vs. long-term investment

**Placement:** Concept 3, immediately after the "mechanism" subsection (after the term *social contract* is introduced).

**Description:** A line graph. X-axis: "Time horizon (years)" from 0 to ~10. Y-axis: "Cumulative business value." Two curves:

- **Short-term ethical shortcut**: rises sharply from year 0 to ~year 2, peaks, then declines through year 10 as cumulative reputational, regulatory, and customer-loss costs compound.
- **Long-term ethical investment**: rises slowly from year 0 to ~year 4, then steeply from year 4 onward, surpassing the first curve at the crossover and continuing to rise through year 10.

The crossover point should be marked clearly, with a caption label: "Above this horizon, ethics is the cheaper choice."

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice that the curves *cross*. Any analyst looking only at the horizon shorter than the crossing point would conclude (incorrectly) that the ethical shortcut is the better business choice. The figure makes the time-horizon argument visceral.

**Style notes:** Schematic line graph. Two curves in different stroke styles. Axes labeled clearly. The crossover marked with a vertical dashed line and a small label. No specific numerical values on the y-axis — the figure is illustrative, not empirical.

**Source for figure data:** Not from a single source figure; this is a chapter-original schematic synthesizing the long-term-vs-short-term argument from source file 03-m66942.md (Toyota example, Friedman 1970, Buffett quotations, *Fortune* most-admired correlations).

## Figure 1.5: A worked stakeholder analysis grid

**Placement:** Concept 3, after the "Stakeholders: the broader audience the long game serves" subsection.

**Description:** A three-column table for a hypothetical layoff decision (Option B from Concept 2's worked example):

| Stakeholder | Their interests | Likely impact of Option B |
|---|---|---|
| Affected employees (~500) | Income, health insurance, dignity | Severe near-term — lost income and benefits, compressed search timeline |
| Surviving employees | Job security, workload, morale | Moderate negative — increased workload, fear, productivity dip |
| Customers | Service continuity, reliability | Mild near-term — service interruptions during transition |
| Local community | Tax base, retail spending, civic stability | Moderate negative — sudden contraction of local payroll |
| Shareholders | Near-term EPS, share price | Mild positive in next two quarters; uncertain long-term effect |

**Pedagogical purpose:** The student should notice how *naming the rows* changes the felt weight of the decision compared to the abstract phrase "we need to cut costs." The exercise transforms ethical analysis from instinct into procedure.

**Style notes:** Plain table, serif, no color. Column widths tuned so each row fits on one line in the printed text. Header row visually distinct (bold or shaded) but not heavy.

**Source for figure data:** OpenStax Business Ethics, source file 03-m66942.md, "Stockholders, Stakeholders, and Goodwill" section, the three-column-screen exercise.
