# Source map — Chapter 1: Why Ethics Matter

## Source files

- `01-m68350.md` — Chapter introduction (237 words). Sets the central question of the book ("Should we care about ethics in our lives? In our practices in business and the professions?") and frames career success as a question about whether wealth alone constitutes a successful career. Contributed: the framing of the cold open's central question; the chapter's "why this chapter matters" voice.
- `02-m66941.md` — "Defining and Applying Business Ethics" (2,653 words). The conceptual heart of the chapter. Contributed: definitions of ethics, compliance, integrity, stakeholders; the Patagonia 1% for the Planet example; the legal-vs-ethical distinction; the three normative ethical theories (consequentialism, deontology, virtue ethics); Kant and *deon*; the cardinal virtues (courage, self-control, justice, wisdom); Socrates; Aristotle's *phronesis* and the Nonaka–Takeuchi HBR 2011 framing of practical wisdom; the Hippocratic Oath; Johnson & Johnson's *Our Credo* (Robert Wood Johnson); *Forbes* most-ethical companies list; Mark Faris white-collar fraud case (kept lightly).
- `03-m66942.md` — "Profitability and Success: Thinking Long Term" (3,808 words). The long-game spine of the chapter. Contributed: short-term vs. long-term perspective; stockholder vs. stakeholder; Milton Friedman's 1970 NYT Magazine claim; the social contract; Bradley Birkenfeld and UBS ($104M reward, 40 months prison, $780M fine, 4,500 U.S. private clients); Toyota's 1957 U.S. entry and *The Toyota Way* (Liker 2005); examples of long-term ethical companies (Fluor, illycaffè, Marriott, Nokia); Warren Buffett quotations (invest-in-yourself, front-page reporter, money-vs-reputation); the 2008–2009 unethical-company bankruptcy wave; Yelp/Facebook information asymmetry; goodwill as intangible asset; the Equifax 2017 data breach (timeline, stock sales, ~14% share fall, executive resignations); corporate culture; corporate social responsibility (CSR); TOMS Shoes (Blake Mycoskie, 75 million pairs to 70 countries by May 2018); the three-column stakeholder analysis exercise.
- `04-m66943.md` — "Ethical Relativism vs. Single Ethical Standard" (1,167 words). Contributed: definition of ethical relativism; the case against relativism from each of the three normative theories; the universal principles list (honesty, integrity, loyalty, fairness, respect for law, respect for others); the cousin's-discount vignette; the transparency/hypocrisy argument.

## Concept coverage

- **Concept 1 — What ethics is, and what it is not.** Primary source: `02-m66941.md` (definitions, compliance distinction, integrity, stakeholders, Hippocratic Oath, J&J Credo). Supplementary: `01-m68350.md` (framing); `04-m66943.md` (the cousin's-discount vignette as the worked example).
- **Concept 2 — Three lenses for examining a decision.** Primary source: `02-m66941.md` (the three normative theories, *deon*, Kant, Socrates, the cardinal virtues, *phronesis*, Nonaka & Takeuchi HBR 2011). The layoff worked example is chapter-original — it constructs a hypothetical that triangulates the three lenses; the construction is consistent with the source's voice and uses no source-foreign facts.
- **Concept 3 — The long game, goodwill, and the cost of a double standard.** Primary source: `03-m66942.md` (long-term/short-term, stockholder/stakeholder, social contract, Friedman, Toyota, Buffett, goodwill, Equifax, TOMS, CSR). Supplementary: `04-m66943.md` (the case against ethical relativism, woven in as one of the chapter's "common misconceptions" in Concept 3 because the relativism argument is fundamentally a long-game argument: a person who shifts standards across contexts cannot be trusted across time).
- **Integration — the Mark Faris case.** Primary source: external research (DOJ press release on the 2008 indictment; Markkula Center for Applied Ethics, *Coming to Grips With Guilt: A White Collar Felon*, Santa Clara University; *Twin Cities Business*, *Under Attack: Honesty*; *The Register*, March 2007). The OpenStax source (`02-m66941.md`) referenced Faris in passing as a sidebar but did not provide specific facts. The integration section uses the documented case (Interlink Communications, Cisco SMARTnet fraud, 140 fraudulent claims January 2001–August 2002, $400,000+ in equipment, sentenced July 31, 2009 to one year and one day, Duluth Federal Prison Camp, $215,000 restitution, post-prison founding of MPV Ethics, university tours with Hank Shea of St. Thomas University) as the chapter's worked single-decision case spanning all three concepts.

## Source-level notes

- **No author byline.** Source files contain only OpenStax module IDs (m66941, m66942, m66943, m68350) and UUIDs. There is no Nik Bear Brown attribution, so the "first person as Bear" rule does not fire. The chapter is written in the standard AxF v1.1 voice (companionable second-person and inclusive "we" where the figuring-out is shared).
- **Empty figure placeholders.** All `<figure id="OSX_Ethics_..."` blocks in the source are empty — no actual image data is preserved in the markdown. The five chapter-original `[FIGURE: ...]` placeholders in the rewrite map to: (1) the Equifax timeline (chapter opening), (2) the legal-by-ethical matrix (chapter-original schematic, replaces Concept 1 figure gap), (3) the three-lenses diagram (replaces source figure for Kant), (4) the horizon-crossover graph (chapter-original schematic), (5) the stakeholder grid (replaces OSX_Ethics_01_02_Exercise). The source's OSX_Ethics_01_02_Buffet figure (Buffett portrait) is not preserved as a figure — Buffett's quotations carry the relevant content; a portrait would be decoration.
- **No source contradictions discovered.** The four source files cohere: each builds on the others without contradicting them.
- **One simplification.** The source treats *ethics* and *morals* as interchangeable but acknowledges philosophers who distinguish them. The rewrite makes the same move and surfaces it explicitly in "Why this chapter matters."
- **Friedman timeline anchored to milestones, not round numbers.** An earlier draft of this chapter said Friedman's view "dominated American business education for thirty years." That phrasing was replaced after research with a milestone-anchored sequence: 1970 NYT Magazine essay; 1976 Jensen & Meckling "Theory of the Firm" agency-theory paper as the quantitative amplifier; dominance from the early 1980s through the mid-2000s in boardrooms and business education; 2008 financial crisis as the inflection point; August 2019 Business Roundtable statement signed by 181 CEOs as the formal repudiation, against the backdrop of every prior version of their *Principles of Corporate Governance* since 1997 having endorsed shareholder primacy. All temporal anchors are sourced from public Business Roundtable documents and corporate governance histories.
- **The Mark Faris case carries the chapter's integration section.** The source's brief sidebar mention has been expanded into a full single-decision worked example using verified public-record facts (DOJ filings, Markkula Center for Applied Ethics, *Twin Cities Business*). The Hypothesis Pizza hypothetical in the earlier draft is gone — Faris is real, source-anchored, and triangulates all three concepts cleanly. The case carries the chapter's central claim that ethical failures are typically failures of time horizon rather than failures of values, because Faris's decision was rational only on a window shorter than the prosecution and audit timelines that eventually closed it.
- **Citations preserved.** All source citations (Nonaka & Takeuchi HBR 2011, Yang *Washington Post* 2013, Kocieniewski *NYT* 2012, Liker 2005, Mejia & Ward CNBC 2017, Cunningham *NYT* 2015, Georgescu *Forbes* 2017, Durden *Zero Hedge* 2017, Naeini et al *Business Today* 2015) are available for the chapter's reference list. The rewrite cites the most prominent of these inline (Buffett quotes traced to Cunningham; Nonaka & Takeuchi attributed by name and venue; Friedman attributed to *NYT Magazine* 1970; Liker's *The Toyota Way* attributed); the lesser citations (the *Zero Hedge* Equifax write-up, the *Business Today* TOMS case study) are background sources that informed factual claims but are not named in the prose. A formatted endnotes section can be added in a final pass.
